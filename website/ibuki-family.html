Powered by Eon — the Family Financial Operating System

A family is one economic unit. Your bank sees four customers.

Shared goals, shared bills, money moving between members, an allowance for a teenager, a parent’s account someone needs to watch. Banks serve all of it as unrelated individuals. Eon elevates the household to a first-class financial entity — and Mira is the relationship manager who runs it.

01The household relationship

The hardest financial coordination problem most people face has no owner.

Who paid the utility this month? Is the joint goal on track? Did the kid’s allowance go through? Is Dad’s account seeing charges he wouldn’t recognize? Are we over-saving in one place and overdrawing in another? A household runs dozens of these threads at once, across members and across banks — and no single person, and certainly no banking app, holds them all.

Eon makes the household the unit of record: a family graph of members and their relationships, shared wallets and goals, and delegated permissions that say exactly who can see and do what. On top of it, Mira coordinates — the household financial manager a family could never hire, bound by consent and reversible by design.

Multi-generational goals held over years, not sessions — the long view a busy family can’t keep, kept for them.

03The Eon layer beneath

What makes household banking possible — the Family Financial Operating System.

Family graph
Who belongs to the household and how they relate — spouses, children, dependents, a parent under care. The entity the bank has never modeled, made first-class.
Shared wallets & goals
Collective objectives with individual contributions — the trip, the down payment, the college fund — tracked across members and institutions.
Delegated permissions
Fine-grained, consent-bound authority — what each member can see, what each can do, on whose behalf. A teenager’s limits, a caregiver’s scoped view, a spouse’s shared access.
Relationship memory
What was promised, decided, and done — goals set, settlements made, consents granted, so the household never re-explains itself.
Guardrails & consent
Privacy between members by default — a child cannot see a parent’s salary; a caregiver sees only what was granted; every grant is logged and revocable instantly.
04The rule that governs everything

“Solve for the family before you solve for the bank.”

Eon’s consent model is built so members trust it with the most sensitive thing they have — visibility into each other’s money. That trust is fragile and absolute: privacy between members is the default, delegation is explicit, and the family can revoke anything in a tap. Get this right and the household brings its entire financial life; get it wrong once and the relationship is gone.
05Why the bank wins

Win the household, and you win every member — for a generation.

Family relationships are the stickiest in banking and the rarest to be served as families. A bank that hosts the household becomes the place the next generation inherits, the account the caregiver trusts, the coordinator no competitor can dislodge without dislodging the whole family at once.

Consolidation

Generations

Stickiness

Leaving means uprooting every member’s shared goals and settlements at once.

Breadth

Household needs — allowance, caregiving, goals, protection — span far more products than an individual.
