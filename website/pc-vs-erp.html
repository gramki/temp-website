PC vs ERP: Why individual AI isn't enough.

You can put a PC on every desk and still have a slow business. You can give every employee a copilot and still have an institution organized around queues and handoffs. This is not about being better at the old way. It is about a fundamental shift in what you can optimize.

The analogy When to use each
01The PC era vs. the ERP era

A lesson from enterprise software history.

The analogy is not perfect, but it is instructive. Two waves of enterprise software have washed through banks. The first made people faster. The second changed how institutions operated.

The PC Era

(1980s–2000s)
  • Personal productivity: word processor, spreadsheet, email on your own machine
  • Adoption: bottom-up, by individual choice
  • Result: every employee faster — but the organization stayed the same
  • The edges (data sharing, process sync between teams) remained untouched
  • You could have 10,000 PCs and still have slow business processes

The ERP Era

(1990s–2010s)
  • Organization-wide system: single source of truth across all divisions
  • Adoption: top-down, requires process redesign
  • Result: the edges dissolved — handoffs became instantaneous, data unified
  • Processes could compress because the topology was explicit
  • Cost was not the software — it was reimagining how work flowed

The cost in both eras was not the technology. It was the organizational choice: what are you willing to optimize? In the PC era, you optimized individuals. In the ERP era, you optimized institutions.

02Copilot as PC. Agentic system as ERP.

The same tension plays out with AI.

A copilot is a PC: it optimizes the individual at their task. An agentic system is an ERP: it optimizes the institution's work. The question is not which is better. The question is what you are willing to optimize.

Copilot

Individual productivity tool
  • Bound to a person: assists them at their step in the workflow
  • Adoption: bottom-up, by individual choice, team by team
  • Result: people faster at their jobs — but the workflow stays the same
  • Queues, handoffs, reconciliations all remain untouched
  • You can give every analyst a copilot and still have 11-day disputes

Agentic System

Organization-wide capability
  • Bound to work: resolves a domain's commitments end-to-end
  • Adoption: top-down, requires reimagining the Work Model
  • Result: edges dissolve — the domain's work is resolved without handoffs
  • Processes can compress because the work is explicit
  • Cost is not the platform — it is the choice to model and govern work explicitly

The asymmetry: A copilot optimizes the 90 minutes of human work in an 11-day dispute. An agentic system removes the 11 days. A copilot makes your structure faster. An agentic system changes your structure.

03A practical decision matrix

When to deploy copilots. When to deploy agentic systems. When you need both.

Scenario
Use Copilot When…
Use Agentic System When…
Knowledge work
Writing, analysis, drafting — work that stays within one person's domain and doesn't require coordination across teams
The same work, but it spans teams or requires decisions across the institution (dispute investigation, credit decisions, regulatory responses)
Process speed
You want the process slightly faster. You don't need to change the process itself.
You want the process fundamentally faster. You're willing to redesign it.
Adoption
Bottom-up adoption works fine. Teams buy in individually.
Top-down commitment needed. This is an institutional choice, not a team choice.
Economics
Cost per employee. Budget goes up as headcount grows.
Cost per domain per outcome. Budget tied to work absorbed, not headcount.
Compounding
Diminishing returns. The 50th copilot costs the same as the 1st. No leverage.
Compounding returns. The 2nd domain costs less than the 1st. The 50th is cheaper than the 2nd.
The handoff problem
Handoffs stay. The copilot helps people across the boundary, but the boundary remains.
Handoffs dissolve. Work stays unified. The boundary is erased by making the work model explicit.

Both have a place. Most banks will use both. Copilots for individual productivity (drafting loan documents, analyzing credit files). Agentic systems for domain transformation (disputes, onboarding, servicing). The choice is not either-or. The choice is where you accept the constraint of improvement-via-individuals vs. the investment of redesign-via-institution.

04The competitive implication

Banks buying only copilots will optimize like banks did in the PC era.

You will be faster at the same processes. Your competitors will be doing different processes. Five years from now, you will not be competing on feature parity. You will be competing on fundamentals: cost per domain, speed of change, ability to serve customers who bring their own agents.

The question is not whether to pick one or the other. The question is which one is your primary strategic lever. If you want incremental improvement, copilots are the answer. If you want transformation, agentic systems are the foundation, and copilots are the complement.

Which path does your strategy choose?

Faster at what you do. Or different at what you do.
