How do you meet the next-generation customer — agents and agent-augmented humans?

Your customer's own agent is filing disputes, arranging credit, and moving money. Your customer never sees your app. Your engagement point has moved. It's no longer on your properties. Your revenue surface is no longer the banking app you built. It's the invisible infrastructure their agents call on, and the narrow direct channels where you influence them.

The shift New revenue models
01The customer engagement point has moved

And your banking app is invisible.

Five years ago, your competitive moat was your mobile app — the interface where customers experienced your bank. Today, your customer is using their own assistant. The assistant arranges their finances, files disputes, compares products, executes payments. The customer sees the outcome but not your interface.

Where is your relationship now? Three places:

1. In the agent-to-agent interface

Your Swarm serves the customer's agent. Fast, reliable, machine-readable. The customer's agent verifies delegation, coordinates on behalf of the customer. Your bank is trusted infrastructure, not a UI.

2. In narrow direct channels

The places you still speak to the customer directly — a text, an email, a notification — are where you influence behavior. Not broad messaging to everyone. Narrow, direct, personalized influence: customer X should know about product Y because their agent just discovered they qualify for it.

3. In outcomes the customer sees

You're not a app anymore. You're the bank that makes things happen: disputes resolved in hours, not weeks; credit available the same day; products available to customers the incumbents rejected as unprofitable.

The margin at risk

Today, a customer doesn't refinance their loan because they don't check your rates. They carry suboptimal products because switching is friction. They hold deposits despite better rates elsewhere because leaving is effort. That inertia margin — the profit that rests on customer laziness — is eroding. A customer with an agent loses that constraint. Your agent competitor gains it. The question is not whether you lose the margin. It's when and to whom.

02Three new revenue surfaces

Where customer value escapes through old banking apps.

Operating leverage through agents

The mass-market advisory play

You can't afford a financial advisor for every customer. But an agent — operating at marginal cost — can. Your advisor-grade intelligence delivered through the customer's agent creates a revenue surface (advisory fees, higher NIM from better product holding) that wasn't profitable with humans.

  • Real-time rebalancing suggestions
  • Micro-advisory for everyday financial decisions
  • Proactive offers based on actual behavior (not inferred segments)

Embedding in the customer's workflow

The SME operational banking play

A small business doesn't open your banking app to manage cash flow. It runs its business in its ERP, its accounting software, its operational stack. You're not a destination. You're integrated into their workflow — operating account reconciliation, flagging liquidity, suggesting credit lines, handling settlement — all transparent, all at machine speed.

  • APIs your customers' systems call natively
  • Transparent operational banking (not a user interface)
  • Higher wallet share because you're invisible infrastructure

The relationship fork

Utility vs. relationship — you get to choose

You can't be both the best transaction utility and the relationship bank. Some customers want utility — fast, cheap, integrated, no surprises. Some want guidance — advice, proactive help, someone who knows them. Agents let you fork: provide excellent utility for some, relationship infrastructure (advisor behind the agent) for others.

  • Utility-first products (transactions, settlement, treasury)
  • Advisory-first products (guidance, wealth management, business banking)
  • Same customer, different agents, same bank

The old banking app was a funnel: get the customer to open the app, show them products, close transactions. The new banking model is a network: be everywhere your customer operates, provide value they didn't have to seek, influence through outcomes. You're not building for visits anymore. You're building for trust.

03What this means for your business

The metrics that matter are no longer app-centric.

Apps
Old metric: Daily active users, session length, feature adoption. New metric: Agent coverage — what % of your customer workflows go through agents?
Engagement
Old metric: Push notification click-through, app opens. New metric: Agent-initiated actions — how many customer decisions happen because an agent surfaced them?
Growth
Old metric: New customer acquisitions through your app. New metric: New segments addressable (SMEs, mass-market advisory) now that AI makes them profitable.
Margin
Old metric: Spread per customer. New metric: Revenue per agent (advisory fees, activity-based pricing), which grows as agents handle more decisions.

Your strategy is no longer "build a better app." It's "become invisible infrastructure that operates every financial decision a customer makes — through their agent or our narrow direct channels."

04Why this is hard and why it matters

You can't retrofit this onto a product-centric architecture.

Your current bank is organized around products: checking, savings, credit. Your customer interfaces are organized around products. Your profit model measures product adoption. None of that changes when the customer brings an agent. What changes is that the customer stops using your properties at all.

You need a different foundation: one that models work, not products. One where a Swarm of agents can coordinate on behalf of a customer without ever routing through a human team. One where "machine-readable" is not an afterthought integration layer but the first-class interface. One where you've already transformed internally to serve agents the way you serve customers.

This is not a feature release. It is a restructuring of how your bank operates. Start now, before the customer's agent is already your competitor.
