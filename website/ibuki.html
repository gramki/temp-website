01The relationship gap

Banks serve customers as a bundle of products. Customers live as relationships.

A family is not a checking account, a mortgage, two cards, and a 529 held by four people. It is one economic unit with shared goals and constant coordination. A professional practice is not a business checking account plus a personal one — it is an integrated financial life where the owner’s draw, the practice’s cash flow, and the retirement plan are the same question.

Banks know this. They solve it the only way they historically could: a human relationship manager, for the customers profitable enough to justify one. The RM is the most valuable role in banking — and the least scalable. Ibuki makes the role programmable: an agent-native relationship manager that every customer gets, governed and consent-bound, at software economics.

02What Ibuki is — and is not

A relationship core is a new category. The fastest way to understand it is by what it refuses to be.

Ibuki is
  • A relationship core — it hosts the relationship, aggregates its products, and orchestrates every interaction between the bank and the relationship’s participants
  • A system of record for the relationship — goals, obligations, consents, the household or business graph, the memory of what was said and promised
  • An agent layer — an ensemble of specialized agents behind one trusted face (Mira), acting with policy-bounded, reversible autonomy
  • Channel-agnostic — present in your app, over SMS and email, by voice, and through the customer’s own agent
Ibuki is not
  • Not a CRM (Salesforce) — a CRM serves the bank’s salespeople; Ibuki serves the relationship, and solves for the customer first
  • Not servicing (ServiceNow) — it is proactive relationship management, not a ticket queue
  • Not a core banking suite — it sits alongside the cores and orchestrates them; it does not hold the ledger
  • Not digital marketing (Adobe) — it influences through trusted guidance, not campaigns, and never at the relationship’s expense
  • Not KYC / AML / risk (Alloy) — those remain the bank’s, by design; Ibuki consumes their verdicts, it does not make them

04How it is built

One trusted face. An ensemble of agents. A bank-resident operating fabric beneath.

The face
Mira — a single, trusted persona the customer talks to. One relationship, one voice, across every channel. The customer never sees the machinery.
The ensemble
Specialized relationship agents — cash-flow planner, affordability advisor, dues and fee minder, collections assistant, wealth advisor, and more. Each deep in its domain; all coordinating behind Mira.
The autonomy
Policy-bounded and reversible — agents suggest, confirm, and execute within explicit limits. Suitability-checked nudges, a human escalation path always open, every action recorded and revocable.
The fabric
Eon — the bank-resident, agent-native operating fabric: identity and consent, the relationship graph and memory, guardrails and policy. The household is a first-class entity here.
The boundary
The bank keeps the bank’s job — KYC, AML, risk, and the product ledgers stay where they are. Ibuki orchestrates around them; it never takes them over.
05How Mira behaves

The operating principles. This is the difference between a relationship and a feature.

“Solve for the user and the relationship before you solve for the bank.”
Trust is the asset. It compounds only when the customer’s interest comes first — and it is the only thing that makes the bank’s value follow.
“Humans are inherently forgetful. Be the solution for it.”
Remember and exercise what the customer can’t hold in their head — dues, renewals, promises, goals.
“Never convey a problem or rejection without a path to resolution.”
A declined application, a missed payment, a shortfall — every one arrives with a next step, not just a verdict.
“Don’t aim to solve everything. Be really good at what you can.”
Start with high-frequency, high-accuracy jobs to earn trust. Acknowledge limits honestly rather than overreach.
06Why the bank wins by serving the customer first

Relationship depth is the business model. Trust is the moat.

When the relationship is hosted, served, and remembered, the commercial outcomes follow without being chased: deposits consolidate toward the bank that coordinates the whole picture; product breadth rises as Mira surfaces what genuinely fits; and churn collapses because leaving means leaving the relationship, not switching an app. The economics are a consequence of the principle, not a workaround of it — Ibuki is paid as relationships deepen, which only happens when the customer is served first.
