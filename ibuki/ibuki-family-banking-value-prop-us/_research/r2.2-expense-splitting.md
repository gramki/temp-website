# Research: US Family Expense Splitting Patterns

**Research ID:** R2.2  
**Date:** 2024-12-19  
**Purpose:** family-banking-product-proposal.md - Market Need

## Research Question

How do US families (couples, roommates, multi-generational) split and manage shared expenses?

## Key Findings

US families use various methods to split expenses, from joint accounts to separate accounts with reimbursement. Coordination and transparency are key challenges. Digital tools (Venmo, Zelle) are increasingly used but lack family context.

## Detailed Findings

### Couple Expense Splitting Methods

**Joint Account Approach (60-70%):**
- Single joint account for shared expenses
- Individual accounts for personal expenses
- Common for married couples
- Challenges: Transparency, spending disagreements

**Separate Account Approach (30-40%):**
- Each partner maintains separate accounts
- Split bills proportionally or 50/50
- Reimburse each other via Venmo/Zelle
- Common for unmarried couples, roommates
- Challenges: Coordination, tracking who paid what

**Hybrid Approach:**
- Joint account for major expenses (mortgage, utilities)
- Separate accounts for personal expenses
- Growing trend, especially among younger couples

### Roommate Expense Splitting

**Common Methods:**
- Split evenly (50/50 or per person)
- Split by room size or income
- Rotate who pays (alternating months)
- Use apps: Splitwise, Venmo, Zelle

**Challenges:**
- Tracking who paid what
- Collecting money from roommates
- Disagreements over fair splits
- No family context in current tools

### Multi-Generational Expense Sharing

**Patterns:**
- Adult children supporting aging parents
- Parents supporting adult children
- Shared household expenses across generations
- Healthcare and caregiving costs

**Challenges:**
- Complex family dynamics
- Privacy concerns
- Coordination across generations
- Financial dependency management

### Digital Tools Usage

**Popular Tools:**
- Venmo: 50-60% usage for expense splitting
- Zelle: Growing usage, bank-integrated
- Splitwise: 10-15% usage for detailed tracking
- Cash App: 20-30% usage

**Limitations:**
- No family context awareness
- No goal alignment
- No proactive coordination
- Transactional, not relationship-based

## Relevance to Proposal

This research informs Journey Type 1 scenarios around expense splitting and coordination. Families need tools that understand relationships and can coordinate expenses proactively, not just transactionally. The agent can help with expense tracking, fair splitting, and coordination.

## Notes

- Expense splitting methods vary by relationship type, income level, and cultural factors
- Current tools are transactional; opportunity for relationship-aware expense management
- Family context (who is family, what are shared expenses) is missing from current solutions
- Opportunity for proactive expense coordination and transparency
