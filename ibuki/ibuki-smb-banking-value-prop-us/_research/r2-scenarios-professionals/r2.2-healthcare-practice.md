# Research: Healthcare Practice Scenarios

**Research ID:** R2.2  
**Date:** 2025-01-15  
**Purpose:** Scenario inventory - Healthcare vertical

## Research Question

What are the financial scenarios and needs specific to healthcare practices (physicians, dentists, therapists, other healthcare providers)?

## Key Findings

Healthcare practices have **29 distinct financial scenarios** across 14 journey types, spanning:
- Daily operations and cash flow management
- Owner compensation and business-personal separation
- Client billing and collections
- Vendor and expense management
- Tax and compliance
- Financing and credit management
- Practice growth and investment decisions
- Crisis management
- Practice lifecycle transitions
- Banking operations and treasury management

**Key insight:** 15 of 29 scenarios have **High or Very High expectation** from banks. These are core banking responsibilities where mid-sized banks must excel to win and retain healthcare practice relationships. Poor experience in these areas drives practices to fintech alternatives.

## Journey Type 1: Daily Cash Flow Operations

### 1.1 Managing Insurance Receivables

**User Story:** As a physician, I need to track and manage insurance claims and receivables so I can understand my cash flow and identify slow-paying insurers.

**Key Needs:**
- Track insurance claims by payer (Medicare, Medicaid, private insurance)
- Monitor claim status (submitted, pending, paid, denied)
- Identify aging receivables (30, 60, 90+ days)
- Calculate expected payment dates based on payer patterns
- Understand total outstanding receivables

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Stress from cash flow uncertainty
- Frustration with insurance payment delays
- Need for visibility into practice financial health

**Frequency:** Daily to weekly  
**Impact:** Very High (insurance receivables are 60-80% of practice revenue)

**Current Bank Coverage:** None - Banks don't provide receivables management

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Practice Management Software (PMS)**
   - Examples: Athenahealth, Epic, eClinicalWorks, NextGen, AdvancedMD
   - Pros:
     - Integrated with billing and EHR systems
     - Real-time claim status tracking
     - Automated aging reports
     - Payer-specific workflows
   - Cons:
     - Expensive ($500-$2,000+/month)
     - Complex setup and training required
     - Limited financial analysis capabilities
     - Doesn't integrate with banking data
   - Cost: $500-$2,000/month
   - Effectiveness: High for claim tracking, Medium for financial analysis

2. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Lower cost ($30-$200/month)
     - Familiar interface
     - Good for general receivables tracking
   - Cons:
     - Manual data entry from PMS
     - Not designed for insurance-specific workflows
     - Limited payer-specific insights
     - Doesn't track claim status
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium (requires manual work)

3. **Excel/Spreadsheets**
   - Pros:
     - Free or low cost
     - Full control and customization
     - Can track any data points needed
   - Cons:
     - Time-consuming manual entry
     - Error-prone
     - No automation
     - Difficult to maintain
   - Cost: $0-$20/month (software)
   - Effectiveness: Low (high time cost)

4. **Medical Billing Services**
   - Examples: R1 RCM, Change Healthcare, The SSI Group
   - Pros:
     - Handles entire billing process
     - Expertise in collections
     - Reduces administrative burden
   - Cons:
     - Expensive (5-10% of collections)
     - Less control over process
     - Limited visibility into details
     - May not improve cash flow timing
   - Cost: 5-10% of collections
   - Effectiveness: High for collections, Medium for visibility

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** Mixed - Some see it as natural extension of cash management, others see it as outside bank's core competency
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Integration with banking data would provide unified view of cash flow; real-time receivables data would improve decision-making
- **What would make them switch?** 
  - Seamless integration with existing PMS (no duplicate data entry)
  - Lower cost than current solutions
  - Better financial analysis than PMS provides
  - Trust in bank's data security and reliability
- **Trust factors:** Banks are trusted for financial data security; concern about bank's healthcare industry expertise
- **Convenience factors:** Single dashboard for receivables and cash flow; automated updates from PMS; proactive alerts

### 1.2 Cash Position Visibility

**User Story:** As a dentist, I need to know my current cash position across all accounts so I can make decisions about expenses and owner draws.

**Key Needs:**
- Real-time view of all practice accounts (checking, savings, money market)
- Integration with personal accounts (for owner-operators)
- Cash flow projection (expected receivables, upcoming expenses)
- Alerts for low cash balance

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Need for control and visibility
- Fear of cash shortfall
- Desire for proactive financial management

**Frequency:** Daily  
**Impact:** High

**Current Bank Coverage:** Partial - Banks provide account balances but not integrated view or projections

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Bank Online Banking (Multiple Logins)**
   - Pros:
     - Free (included with accounts)
     - Real-time balances
     - Secure and reliable
   - Cons:
     - Separate logins for each account
     - No aggregated view
     - No cash flow projections
     - No integration with receivables
   - Cost: $0
   - Effectiveness: Low (fragmented view)

2. **Personal Finance Apps (Mint, YNAB, Personal Capital)**
   - Pros:
     - Aggregates multiple accounts
     - Free or low cost
     - Budgeting and forecasting features
   - Cons:
     - Designed for personal use, not business
     - Limited business-specific features
     - Security concerns with third-party access
     - Doesn't integrate with practice management
   - Cost: $0-$15/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium (not business-focused)

3. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Business-focused
     - Can connect to bank accounts
     - Cash flow statements and projections
   - Cons:
     - Requires manual categorization
     - Not real-time (updates daily or manually)
     - Limited integration with personal accounts
     - Doesn't show receivables in real-time
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (good for historical, limited for real-time)

4. **Excel/Spreadsheets**
   - Pros:
     - Full control
     - Can customize to needs
     - Free or low cost
   - Cons:
     - Manual data entry
     - Time-consuming
     - Error-prone
     - No automation
   - Cost: $0-$20/month
   - Effectiveness: Low (high time cost)

5. **Fintech Business Banking (Mercury, Relay, Novo)**
   - Pros:
     - Modern interface
     - Multiple account views
     - Some cash flow features
   - Cons:
     - Limited integration with practice management
     - Doesn't include personal accounts
     - Limited receivables integration
     - May lack healthcare-specific features
   - Cost: $0-$30/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (better UX, limited integration)

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** Yes - This is seen as core banking functionality; banks should provide unified account view
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Single dashboard would save time; integration with receivables would provide complete picture
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Seamless aggregation of all accounts (business + personal)
  - Real-time updates
  - Integration with receivables from PMS
  - Proactive alerts and recommendations
  - Better than current bank online banking
- **Trust factors:** High trust in banks for account aggregation; concern about data sharing with third-party apps
- **Convenience factors:** Single login; real-time updates; mobile app access; automated categorization

## Journey Type 2: Owner Compensation & Draws

### 2.1 Determining Owner Draw Amount

**User Story:** As a therapist running a solo practice, I need to know how much I can safely pay myself this month given my receivables and upcoming expenses.

**Key Needs:**
- View current cash position
- Understand outstanding receivables and expected payment dates
- See upcoming expenses (rent, payroll, supplies, insurance)
- Calculate safe draw amount (cash - buffer - upcoming expenses)
- Historical draw patterns for reference

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Balancing personal needs with practice stability
- Fear of overdrawing and causing cash flow problems
- Desire for financial security

**Frequency:** Monthly  
**Impact:** Very High (affects personal finances and practice stability)

**Current Bank Coverage:** None - Banks don't provide draw calculation support

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Manual Calculation (Mental Math/Spreadsheet)**
   - Pros:
     - Free
     - Full control
     - Quick for simple situations
   - Cons:
     - Error-prone
     - Doesn't account for receivables timing
     - No historical context
     - Stressful decision-making
   - Cost: $0
   - Effectiveness: Low (high risk of error)

2. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Shows cash position
     - Can track expenses
     - Historical data
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't integrate receivables timing
     - Manual expense entry
     - No automated calculation
     - Doesn't provide recommendations
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium (better data, but manual analysis)

3. **CPA/Bookkeeper Consultation**
   - Pros:
     - Expert advice
     - Comprehensive analysis
     - Tax optimization insights
   - Cons:
     - Expensive
     - Not real-time (monthly/quarterly)
     - May be conservative
     - Requires scheduling
   - Cost: $100-$500/month or $150-$300/hour
   - Effectiveness: High (but delayed and expensive)

4. **Practice Management Software (Limited)**
   - Pros:
     - Shows receivables
     - Integrated with practice data
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't calculate safe draw
     - Limited expense tracking
     - No cash flow projection
   - Cost: Included in PMS ($500-$2,000/month)
   - Effectiveness: Low (shows data but no analysis)

5. **Fintech Apps (Found, Collective)**
   - Pros:
     - Designed for owner draws
     - Automated calculations
     - Tax withholding features
   - Cons:
     - Limited to specific business types
     - May not integrate with practice management
     - Newer, less established
   - Cost: $0-$50/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (good for simple cases)

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** Moderate - Some see it as valuable service, others see it as outside core banking
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Banks have complete cash flow picture; could provide real-time recommendations
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Automated calculation based on real-time data
  - Integration with receivables (know when payments coming)
  - Historical patterns and recommendations
  - Confidence in calculations (reduce stress)
  - Lower cost than CPA consultation
- **Trust factors:** High trust in banks for financial calculations; concern about bank understanding business nuances
- **Convenience factors:** Real-time recommendations; automated updates; mobile access; reduces monthly stress

     - Understands practice profitability

     - Can calculate maximum contributions
   - Cons:
     - Focused on tax, not retirement planning

     - May not optimize long-term strategy

   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (good for calculations, limited planning)

3. **Retirement Account Provider (Fidelity, Vanguard, etc.)**
   - Pros:
     - Retirement product expertise
     - Online calculators
     - Low-cost options
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't integrate with practice profitability
     - Generic calculators
     - No coordination with practice finances
   - Cost: $0-$50/month (account fees)
   - Effectiveness: Low-Medium (products available, but no integration)

4. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks)**
   - Pros:
     - Shows profitability
     - Historical data
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't calculate retirement contributions
     - No retirement planning features
     - Manual analysis required
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Low (shows data but no analysis)

5. **Manual Calculation**
   - Pros:
     - Free
     - Full control
   - Cons:
     - Complex rules (SEP-IRA vs Solo 401k)
     - Error-prone
     - No optimization
     - Time-consuming
   - Cost: $0
   - Effectiveness: Low (high risk of error)

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** Moderate - Some see it as valuable integrated service, others see retirement as separate from banking
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Banks have practice profitability data; could provide real-time contribution calculations; coordinate with cash flow
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Automated calculation based on real practice profitability
  - Integration with practice financials (no manual data entry)
  - Real-time updates (not just annual)
  - Lower cost than financial advisor
  - Tax optimization recommendations
- **Trust factors:** High trust in banks for financial products; concern about retirement planning expertise
- **Convenience factors:** Integrated with banking; automated calculations; real-time updates; reduces annual planning stress
