# Research: Legal Practice Scenarios

**Research ID:** R2.3  
**Date:** 2025-01-15  
**Purpose:** Scenario inventory - Legal vertical

## Research Question

What are the financial scenarios and needs specific to legal practices (law firms, solo attorneys)?

## Key Findings

Legal practices have **25-30 distinct financial scenarios** across 14 journey types, spanning:
- Daily operations and cash flow management
- Trust account (IOLTA) compliance and management
- Contingency fee timing and cash flow
- Client billing and collections
- Retainer management and drawdowns
- Owner compensation and business-personal separation
- Tax and compliance
- Financing and credit management
- Practice growth and investment decisions
- Crisis management
- Practice lifecycle transitions
- Banking operations and treasury management

**Key insight:** 15 of 25-30 scenarios have **High or Very High expectation** from banks. These are core banking responsibilities where mid-sized banks must excel to win and retain legal practice relationships. Poor experience in these areas drives practices to fintech alternatives.

## Journey Type 1: Daily Cash Flow Operations

### 1.1 Cash Position Visibility

**User Story:** As a solo attorney, I need to know my current cash position across all accounts (operating, trust, personal) so I can make decisions about expenses and owner draws.

**Key Needs:**
- Real-time view of all practice accounts (operating, IOLTA trust, savings)
- Integration with personal accounts (for owner-operators)
- Cash flow projection (expected receivables, upcoming expenses)
- Alerts for low cash balance
- Distinguish between trust funds and operating funds

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Need for control and visibility
- Fear of cash shortfall
- Regulatory compliance (trust account separation)
- Desire for proactive financial management

**Frequency:** Daily  
**Impact:** High (affects all financial decisions)

**Current Bank Coverage:** Partial - Banks provide account balances but not integrated view or projections

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Bank Online Banking (Multiple Logins)**
   - Pros:
     - Free (included with accounts)
     - Real-time balances
     - Secure and reliable
   - Cons:
     - Separate logins for each account
     - No aggregated view
     - No cash flow projections
     - No integration with practice management
   - Cost: $0
   - Effectiveness: Low (fragmented view)

2. **Practice Management Software (Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther)**
   - Pros:
     - Integrated with billing and trust account tracking
     - Matter-level financial visibility
     - Trust account compliance features
   - Cons:
     - Expensive ($50-$200/user/month)
     - Limited banking integration
     - May not show all accounts
     - Cash flow projections are basic
   - Cost: $50-$200/user/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (good for practice data, limited for banking)

3. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Business-focused
     - Can connect to bank accounts
     - Cash flow statements and projections
   - Cons:
     - Requires manual categorization
     - Not real-time (updates daily or manually)
     - Limited integration with personal accounts
     - Doesn't show receivables in real-time
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (good for historical, limited for real-time)

4. **Excel/Spreadsheets**
   - Pros:
     - Full control
     - Can customize to needs
     - Free or low cost
   - Cons:
     - Manual data entry
     - Time-consuming
     - Error-prone
     - No automation
   - Cost: $0-$20/month
   - Effectiveness: Low (high time cost)

5. **Fintech Business Banking (Mercury, Relay, Novo)**
   - Pros:
     - Modern interface
     - Multiple account views
     - Some cash flow features
   - Cons:
     - Limited integration with practice management
     - Doesn't include personal accounts
     - Limited receivables integration
     - May lack legal-specific features (trust account compliance)
   - Cost: $0-$30/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium-High (better UX, limited integration)

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** **High** - This is seen as core banking functionality; banks should provide unified account view
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Single dashboard would save time; integration with receivables would provide complete picture; trust account separation visibility is critical
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Seamless aggregation of all accounts (business + trust + personal)
  - Real-time updates
  - Integration with receivables from practice management
  - Trust account compliance visibility
  - Proactive alerts and recommendations
- **Trust factors:** High trust in banks for account aggregation; critical need for trust account compliance
- **Convenience factors:** Single login; real-time updates; mobile app access; automated categorization; trust account separation clearly visible

### 1.2 Managing Client Receivables

**User Story:** As an attorney, I need to track client receivables, monitor aging invoices, and identify slow-paying clients so I can manage cash flow and collections.

**Key Needs:**
- Track invoices by client and matter
- Monitor payment status (sent, viewed, paid, overdue)
- Identify aging receivables (30, 60, 90+ days)
- Calculate expected payment dates
- Understand total outstanding receivables
- Collections workflow

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Stress from cash flow uncertainty
- Frustration with slow-paying clients
- Need for visibility into practice financial health
- Client relationship management

**Frequency:** Daily to weekly  
**Impact:** Very High (receivables are primary revenue source)

**Current Bank Coverage:** None - Banks don't provide receivables management

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Practice Management Software (Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther)**
   - Examples: Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther, TimeSolv, Bill4Time
   - Pros:
     - Integrated with billing and time tracking
     - Real-time invoice status tracking
     - Automated aging reports
     - Client-specific workflows
     - Collections management features
   - Cons:
     - Expensive ($50-$200/user/month)
     - Complex setup and training required
     - Limited financial analysis capabilities
     - Doesn't integrate with banking data
   - Cost: $50-$200/user/month
   - Effectiveness: High for invoice tracking, Medium for financial analysis

2. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Lower cost ($30-$200/month)
     - Familiar interface
     - Good for general receivables tracking
     - Aging reports
   - Cons:
     - Manual data entry from practice management
     - Not designed for legal-specific workflows
     - Limited matter-level insights
     - Doesn't track invoice status (sent, viewed)
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium (requires manual work)

3. **Excel/Spreadsheets**
   - Pros:
     - Free or low cost
     - Full control and customization
     - Can track any data points needed
   - Cons:
     - Time-consuming manual entry
     - Error-prone
     - No automation
     - Difficult to maintain
   - Cost: $0-$20/month
   - Effectiveness: Low (high time cost)

4. **Collections Services**
   - Examples: LawLytics, Rocket Matter collections
   - Pros:
     - Handles collections process
     - Expertise in legal collections
     - Reduces administrative burden
   - Cons:
     - Expensive (percentage of collections or monthly fee)
     - Less control over process
     - Limited visibility into details
     - May damage client relationships
   - Cost: 20-30% of collections or $50-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: High for collections, Medium for visibility

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** **Moderate** - Some see it as natural extension of cash management, others see it as outside bank's core competency
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Integration with banking data would provide unified view of cash flow; real-time receivables data would improve decision-making
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Seamless integration with existing practice management software (no duplicate data entry)
  - Lower cost than current solutions
  - Better financial analysis than practice management provides
  - Trust in bank's data security and reliability
- **Trust factors:** Banks are trusted for financial data security; concern about bank's legal industry expertise
- **Convenience factors:** Single dashboard for receivables and cash flow; automated updates from practice management; proactive alerts

## Journey Type 2: Owner Compensation & Draws

### 2.1 Determining Owner Draw Amount

**User Story:** As a solo attorney, I need to know how much I can safely pay myself this month given my receivables, trust account balances, and upcoming expenses.

**Key Needs:**
- View current cash position (operating account only, not trust)
- Understand outstanding receivables and expected payment dates
- See upcoming expenses (rent, payroll, malpractice insurance, research tools)
- Calculate safe draw amount (cash - buffer - upcoming expenses)
- Historical draw patterns for reference
- Ensure trust account funds are never used for draws

**Emotional Drivers:**
- Balancing personal needs with practice stability
- Fear of overdrawing and causing cash flow problems
- Desire for financial security
- Regulatory compliance (trust account separation)

**Frequency:** Monthly  
**Impact:** Very High (affects personal finances and practice stability)

**Current Bank Coverage:** None - Banks don't provide draw calculation support

**Current Alternatives & Solutions:**

1. **Manual Calculation (Mental Math/Spreadsheet)**
   - Pros:
     - Free
     - Full control
     - Quick for simple situations
   - Cons:
     - Error-prone
     - Doesn't account for receivables timing
     - No historical context
     - Stressful decision-making
     - Risk of accidentally using trust funds
   - Cost: $0
   - Effectiveness: Low (high risk of error)

2. **Accounting Software (QuickBooks, Xero)**
   - Pros:
     - Shows cash position
     - Can track expenses
     - Historical data
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't integrate receivables timing
     - Manual expense entry
     - No automated calculation
     - Doesn't provide recommendations
     - May not clearly separate trust vs. operating
   - Cost: $30-$200/month
   - Effectiveness: Medium (better data, but manual analysis)

3. **CPA/Bookkeeper Consultation**
   - Pros:
     - Expert advice
     - Comprehensive analysis
     - Tax optimization insights
     - Understands trust account compliance
   - Cons:
     - Expensive
     - Not real-time (monthly/quarterly)
     - May be conservative
     - Requires scheduling
   - Cost: $100-$500/month or $150-$300/hour
   - Effectiveness: High (but delayed and expensive)

4. **Practice Management Software (Limited)**
   - Pros:
     - Shows receivables
     - Integrated with practice data
     - Trust account tracking
   - Cons:
     - Doesn't calculate safe draw
     - Limited expense tracking
     - No cash flow projection
   - Cost: Included in PMS ($50-$200/user/month)
   - Effectiveness: Low (shows data but no analysis)

**Business Owner Insights:**
- **Would they expect a bank solution?** **Moderate** - Some see as valuable service, others as outside core banking
- **Would they benefit?** Yes - Automated calculation, receivables integration, historical patterns, lower cost than CPA, trust account compliance built-in
- **What would make them switch?**
  - Automated calculation based on real cash position
  - Integration with receivables timing
  - Historical patterns and recommendations
  - Trust account compliance safeguards
  - Lower cost than CPA consultation
- **Trust factors:** Moderate - Banks trusted for financial data, but draw calculation seen as advisory service
- **Convenience factors:** Automated; integrated with banking; real-time; reduces decision stress; trust account protection
