# SMB Segmentation Framework: A Foundation for Relationship-Focused Banking

**Document ID:** R0.1  
**Date:** 2025-01-15  
**Purpose:** Establish a MECE segmentation framework for the SMB universe to inform segment selection and research priorities

---

## Executive Summary

This document presents a Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive (MECE) framework for segmenting the Small and Medium Business (SMB) universe in the United States. The framework is designed to support strategic decisions about which segments to target with relationship-focused banking offerings.

**Key Insight:** Traditional SMB segmentation by revenue or headcount (e.g., "1-10 employees" or "$1M-$5M revenue") fails to capture meaningful differences in financial needs and banking behaviors. Effective segmentation must reflect how businesses actually operate and what drives their financial complexity.

**Framework Summary:**

| Level 1 | Level 2 | Level 3 Examples |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| Non-Employer | Independent Operators | Licensed Professionals, Knowledge Workers, Skilled Trades |
| Non-Employer | Platform-Dependent | Gig Workers, Marketplace Sellers |
| Employer | Service Businesses | Professional Firms, Agencies, Trades/Construction |
| Employer | Product Businesses | Retail, E-commerce, Manufacturing |
| Employer | Location-Based Services | Food & Beverage, Personal Services, Hospitality |

## 1. Why Segmentation Matters for Relationship-Focused Banking

### The Problem with Traditional Segmentation

Banks typically segment SMBs by:
- **Revenue:** Micro (<$1M), Small ($1M-$10M), Medium ($10M-$50M)
- **Headcount:** Sole proprietor, 1-10, 10-50, 50-500 employees
- **Industry:** SIC/NAICS codes

These approaches fail because:

1. **Revenue bands don't predict financial needs.** A $500K solo consultant has different needs than a $500K restaurant.

2. **Headcount is an attribute, not a segment.** A 5-person law firm and a 5-person landscaping company have almost nothing in common financially.

3. **Industry codes are too granular.** There are 1,000+ NAICS codes; banks can't create tailored offerings for each.

### What Drives Financial Needs

For relationship-focused banking, the meaningful drivers are:

| Driver | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------------|
| **Employment structure** | Determines payroll complexity, multi-stakeholder access needs, regulatory burden |
| **Income/revenue pattern** | Drives cash flow management, financing needs, tax complexity |
| **Business model** | Determines receivables/payables patterns, inventory needs, margin structure |
| **Owner relationship** | Determines business-personal integration needs, succession planning |

A useful segmentation should cluster businesses that share these characteristics.

## 2. MECE Framework: The SMB Universe

### Level 1: Employment Structure

The first and most fundamental split is whether the business has W-2 employees:

```
SMB Universe
│
├── NON-EMPLOYER (Self-Employed)
│   No W-2 employees; owner IS the workforce
│   ~26 million businesses in US (82% of all businesses)
│
└── EMPLOYER (1+ W-2 Employees)
    Has W-2 employees; owner manages/directs workforce
    ~6 million businesses in US (18% of all businesses)
```

**Why this matters for banking:**

| Dimension | Non-Employer | Employer |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| Payroll | None | Critical (timing, compliance) |
| Multi-user access | Rare | Common (owner, bookkeeper, employees) |
| Cash flow driver | Personal capacity | Operational leverage |
| Business-personal blur | High | Lower (more formalized) |
| Regulatory burden | Lighter | Heavier (employment law, benefits) |

---

### Level 2: Non-Employer Sub-Segments

Non-employers split by their **relationship to income sources**:

```
NON-EMPLOYER
│
├── INDEPENDENT OPERATORS
│   Set own rates, find own clients, control their work
│   Income from direct client relationships
│
└── PLATFORM-DEPENDENT
    Rates/clients determined by platform
    Income from platform participation
```

**Why this matters:**

| Dimension | Independent Operators | Platform-Dependent |
|-----------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Income control | High (set own rates) | Low (platform sets rates) |
| Client relationship | Direct and owned | Mediated by platform |
| Income verification | Invoices, contracts | Platform 1099s, statements |
| Switching costs | Build own reputation | Can switch platforms |
| Cash flow pattern | Project/retainer based | Transaction/gig based |

---

### Level 3: Independent Operator Types

```
INDEPENDENT OPERATORS
│
├── LICENSED PROFESSIONALS
│   Credentialed, regulated professions
│   Examples: Physicians, Dentists, Lawyers, CPAs, Architects
│
├── KNOWLEDGE/CREATIVE WORKERS
│   Expertise-based, not licensed
│   Examples: Consultants, Designers, Developers, Writers, Coaches
│
└── SKILLED TRADES (Solo)
    Manual/technical skills, often licensed for specific work
    Examples: Solo contractors, plumbers, electricians, HVAC technicians
```

**Characteristics comparison:**

| Dimension | Licensed Professionals | Knowledge/Creative | Skilled Trades |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Barriers to entry | High (education, license) | Medium (portfolio, reputation) | Medium (skills, certification) |
| Typical income | $150K-$500K+ | $75K-$250K | $60K-$150K |
| Billing pattern | Hourly/project, insurance | Project/retainer | Project/job |
| Business-personal | High integration | High integration | High integration |
| Growth path | Solo → Practice → Firm | Solo → Agency | Solo → Crew → Company |

---

### Level 3: Platform-Dependent Types

```
PLATFORM-DEPENDENT
│
├── GIG WORKERS
│   Time/task-based platform work
│   Examples: Rideshare (Uber, Lyft), Delivery (DoorDash, Instacart), 
│             Task-based (TaskRabbit, Handy), Freelance platforms (Upwork, Fiverr)
│
├── MARKETPLACE SELLERS
│   Product-based platform selling
│   Examples: Amazon 3P sellers, Etsy sellers, eBay sellers, Shopify/DTC
│
└── CONTENT CREATORS
    Content/attention-based platform monetization
    Examples: YouTube, Instagram, TikTok, Twitch, Substack, Patreon creators
```

**Characteristics comparison:**

| Dimension | Gig Workers | Marketplace Sellers | Content Creators |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|------------------|
| Income driver | Time/availability | Product/inventory | Content/audience |
| Capital needs | Low (vehicle, phone) | Medium (inventory) | Low-Medium (equipment, software) |
| Cash flow | Daily/weekly payouts | Platform payout cycles + inventory | Variable (ad revenue, sponsorships, subscriptions) |
| Tax complexity | Mileage, expenses | COGS, inventory accounting | Business expenses, equipment depreciation |
| Typical income | $25K-$75K | $30K-$200K+ (wide range) | $20K-$200K+ (wide range, highly variable) |
| Platform dependency | High | High but multi-platform possible | High but can build brand/audience |
| Growth potential | Limited (time-constrained) | Can scale to business | Can scale significantly (viral growth) |

### Level 2: Employer Sub-Segments

Employers split by **what they sell**:

```
EMPLOYER
│
├── SERVICE BUSINESSES
│   Sell time, expertise, or labor
│
├── PRODUCT BUSINESSES
│   Sell physical or digital goods
│
└── LOCATION-BASED SERVICES
    Sell services at a fixed location
    (Distinct because real estate + local customer base are critical)
```

### Level 3: Service Business Types

```
SERVICE BUSINESSES
│
├── PROFESSIONAL FIRMS
│   Credentialed/licensed service providers with staff
│   Examples: Law firms, Medical practices, CPA firms, Architecture firms
│
├── AGENCIES
│   Project-based expertise businesses
│   Examples: Marketing agencies, Staffing firms, Dev shops, Consulting firms
│
└── TRADES/CONSTRUCTION
    Project-based physical work with crews
    Examples: General contractors, Specialty trades, Landscaping companies
```

**Characteristics comparison:**

| Dimension | Professional Firms | Agencies | Trades/Construction |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|---------------------|
| Revenue per employee | High ($150K-$300K) | Medium ($100K-$200K) | Medium ($80K-$150K) |
| Billing pattern | Hourly/matter | Project/retainer | Project/milestone |
| Cash flow | Receivables-driven | Receivables + retainer | Progress billing, materials |
| Payroll % of costs | 60-80% | 50-70% | 40-60% |
| Growth constraints | Talent, licensure | Talent, capacity | Talent, bonding, equipment |

### Level 3: Product Business Types

```
PRODUCT BUSINESSES
│
├── RETAIL
│   Physical stores selling goods
│   Examples: Boutiques, hardware stores, specialty shops
│
├── E-COMMERCE

│
└── MANUFACTURING/WHOLESALE
    Produce or distribute physical goods

**Characteristics comparison:**

| Dimension | Retail | E-commerce | Manufacturing |
|-----------|--------|------------|---------------|
| Capital intensity | High (inventory + location) | Medium (inventory) | High (equipment + inventory) |
| Cash flow driver | Foot traffic, seasons | Marketing, platform | Production capacity, orders |
| Margins | 30-50% gross | 40-60% gross | 20-40% gross |
| Financing needs | Inventory, tenant fit-out | Inventory, marketing | Equipment, working capital |

│   │       └── Contractors, Plumbers, Electricians, HVAC, Carpenters
│   │
│   └── Platform-Dependent (~8.5-12 million)
│       ├── Gig Workers (~5-6 million active)

│
└── EMPLOYER (~6 million, 18%)
    │
    ├── Service Businesses (~2.5 million)
    │   ├── Professional Firms (~500K)
    │   │   └── Law firms, Medical practices, CPA firms, Architecture firms
    │   ├── Agencies (~300K)
    │   │   └── Marketing, Staffing, Consulting, Development
    │   └── Trades/Construction (~1.5 million)
    │       └── General contractors, Specialty trades, Landscaping
    │
    ├── Product Businesses (~1.5 million)
    │   ├── Retail (~800K)
    │   ├── E-commerce (~400K)
    │   └── Manufacturing/Wholesale (~300K)
    │
    └── Location-Based Services (~2 million)
        ├── Food & Beverage (~700K)
        ├── Personal Services (~800K)
        └── Hospitality (~500K)
```

*Note: Numbers are estimates based on Census Bureau, BLS, and industry data. Categories overlap slightly in practice (e.g., a restaurant owner who also does catering).*

## 5. Implications for Relationship-Focused Banking

### Where Relationship-Focused Banking Adds Most Value

Relationship-focused banking (solving scenarios vs. selling products) is most valuable where:

1. **Financial complexity is high** but **formalization is low** — the business owner needs help navigating complexity without a full finance team

2. **Business-personal integration is high** — the owner needs holistic view across business and personal finances

3. **Cash flow timing is critical** — the business needs proactive help managing irregular income/expenses

4. **Current solutions are fragmented** — no existing provider offers integrated solutions

### Segment Fit for Relationship-Focused Banking

| Segment | Complexity | B-P Integration | Cash Flow Sensitivity | Solution Fragmentation | Fit Score |
|---------|------------|-----------------|----------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Licensed Professionals | High | Very High | High | High | **Excellent** |
| Knowledge/Creative | Medium | Very High | High | High | **Good** |
| Gig Workers | Medium | Very High | Very High | High | **Good** |
| Marketplace Sellers | Medium | High | High | Medium | **Good** |
| Skilled Trades (Solo) | Medium | High | High | Medium | Good |
| Professional Firms | High | Medium | High | Medium | Good |
| Trades/Construction | High | Medium | Very High | Medium | Good |
| Agencies | Medium | Low | Medium | Low | Moderate |
| Retail | Medium | Low | High | Low | Moderate |
| E-commerce | Medium | Medium | High | Medium | Moderate |
| Food & Beverage | High | Low | Very High | Low | Moderate |
| Personal Services | Low | Medium | Medium | Low | Low |

### Recommended Priority Segments

Based on this analysis, three segments stand out for relationship-focused banking:

1. **Professionals** (Licensed Professionals + Knowledge/Creative Workers + Professional Firms)
   - Why: Highest business-personal integration, high complexity, fragmented solutions, high banking value
   - Spans: Solo practitioners through firms with staff

2. **Platform-Dependent Earners** (Gig Workers + Marketplace Sellers + Content Creators)
   - Why: Very high cash flow sensitivity, very high business-personal integration, underserved by traditional banks
   - Distinct: Platform-mediated income creates unique needs
   - Sub-segments: Gig Workers (5-6M), Marketplace Sellers (2-3M), Content Creators (1.5-3M)

3. **Local Operators** (Food & Beverage + Personal Services + Hospitality)
   - Why: High transaction volume, high cash flow sensitivity, location-based creates distinct needs
   - Challenge: Lower margins, higher risk; may require different economics

## 6. Growth Stage Considerations

Within each segment, businesses evolve through growth stages. A complete framework should account for:

```
GROWTH STAGES (applicable across segments)
│
├── SIDE HUSTLE
│   Part-time, supplemental income
│   Still employed elsewhere or multiple gigs
│
├── FULL-TIME SOLO
│   Primary income, no employees
│   Owner does all work + administration
│
├── FIRST HIRE(S)
│   1-3 employees or contractors
│   Owner still does work + manages
│
├── MANAGER-OWNER
│   4-10+ employees
│   Owner manages, may still do some work
│
└── OPERATOR-OWNER
    10-50+ employees
    Owner operates business, doesn't do frontline work
```

**Implication:** A successful relationship-focused banking offering should serve customers as they grow through these stages, not force them to switch products/relationships.

## 7. Summary

### Key Takeaways

1. **Traditional SMB segmentation (revenue/headcount) is inadequate** for relationship-focused banking because it doesn't reflect how financial needs differ.

2. **Employment structure and income pattern** are the most meaningful first-level segmentation drivers.

3. **The SMB universe segments into 12+ distinct types**, each with different financial needs, banking value, and underservice levels.

4. **Relationship-focused banking adds most value** where complexity is high, business-personal integration is high, and current solutions are fragmented.

5. **Three priority segments emerge:**
   - Professionals (solo through firm)
   - Platform-Dependent Earners (Gig Workers + Marketplace Sellers + Content Creators)
   - Local Operators (location-based services)
