## Document 1: Business Stakeholder Narrative

**File:** `org-8.0/what-we-sell/hubs-streams-loops-narrative.md`
**Audience:** Senior management, customers (CIO/CTO, Business teams)
**Purpose:** Convey the approach Zeta takes for modeling complex business domains and addressing various customer needs
**Style:** Narrative prose (similar to the business-model-narrative), accessible, grounded in business outcomes
**Length:** 3-4 pages

### Structure

**1. The Problem: Why Banking Domains Are Hard to Model**

- Banking is not one system — it's dozens of interconnected domains
- Traditional approaches either over-integrate (monolith) or under-integrate (point solutions)
- Neither gives the bank a coherent view of what's happening across a business domain

**2. Hubs: An Operations and Collaboration Fabric Over Existing Systems**

- A Hub does not replace existing systems. It creates an operations and collaboration fabric on top of them — bringing systems together, enabling coordinated scenarios, and providing governed collaboration by human and AI agents
- A Hub is a bounded banking domain — self-contained enough to operate, measure, and govern independently, composable enough to integrate with other Hubs
- What makes a Hub different from an "application" or a "module": it encapsulates the domain model, the operational scenarios, the agents (human and AI), the knowledge, the memory, and the governance — not just the code. The underlying systems (core banking, payment rails, card networks) continue to do what they do; the Hub adds the operational intelligence on top
- Hubs naturally integrate Zeta Product Lines (Tachyon, Neutrion, Electron) as systems within the Hub, but they work equally well with third-party systems. A bank can run a Credit Card Hub with Tachyon Credit Cards or with an alternative card platform — the Hub's value is in the operational fabric, not in mandating specific underlying systems
- This system-agnostic property is critical for enterprise adoption: banks can introduce Hubs incrementally, wrapping their existing systems first, and replacing underlying systems later if and when they choose
- Zeta's Hubs of prominence: Payments, Credit Cards, CLM, Servicing, Customer IAM, Merchant Acquiring, Commercial Cards, etc.

**3. Streams: How Value Is Delivered**

- A Stream is a Value Stream — what a customer intends the system to solve for in a business domain
- A Stream is not a sequence of steps. It is a coordinated collection of Scenarios, each independently resolvable, that together deliver a business outcome
- Streams are episodic — they start, pause, resume across hours or weeks as real business events unfold. A credit card application Stream spans from submission to activation, with business-meaningful pauses between steps
- Streams span boundaries — they may cross Hubs (credit card application involves Credit Card Hub and Customer IAM Hub), systems, and even organizational boundaries (credit bureau, payment network)
- Streams are the business observability unit — they provide the end-to-end view that compliance, customer experience, and operational improvement teams need
- The OpenTelemetry analogy (briefly, for technical readers): like a trace across a distributed system, but prescriptive (defined before execution), business-scoped (not just technical), and episodic (not continuous)

**4. Loops: How Value Improves Over Time**

- Loops are continuous improvement cycles within a Hub
- They consume data generated by Streams, analyze it, and generate actions that change how the Hub operates
- Loop lifecycle: Segment → Hypothesize → Experiment → Monitor → Decide
- Loops are the natural unit of AI-augmented operations — designed to be operable by supervised or autonomous agents
- Banking examples: Compliance Loop, Fraud Loop, Engagement Loop, Risk Loop, Revenue Optimization Loop
- The relationship: Streams produce data → Loops consume it → Loops modify Streams and Hub configuration

**5. Why This Matters for the Bank**

- Hubs don't require ripping out existing systems — they wrap what exists and add operational intelligence on top. This reduces adoption risk and allows incremental modernization
- The bank gets a coherent operating model for each domain, not just software
- Zeta Product Lines integrate natively, but the Hub works equally well with third-party systems — no lock-in to a single vendor's underlying stack
- Compliance teams can reconstruct end-to-end business traces (Stream Traces)
- Business teams can see where value delivery is slow or broken
- AI and automation are applied within a governed structure, not bolted on
- Each Hub compounds over time through its Loops

**Tone:** No platform internals. No mention of Workbench, Scenario Specification Types, Hub Applications, or Olympus-specific terms. The reader should understand the approach without knowing anything about the technology stack. Uses "Stream" without the three-representation distinction.

## Document 2: Product and Engineering Enablement Guide

**File:** `org-8.0/what-we-sell/hubs-streams-loops-engineering.md`
**Audience:** Zeta product managers, architects, engineering leads
**Purpose:** Enable internal teams to build Hubs using Olympus Hub — mapping the business framework to platform concepts
**Style:** Reference document — precise definitions, tables, diagrams, worked examples
**Length:** 5-7 pages (more detailed)

### Structure

**1. Framework Overview**

- Hubs, Streams, and Loops as the business-domain decomposition model
- How it maps to the Olympus Hub platform: Hub = Workbench, Stream = coordinated Scenarios, Loop = agentic improvement cycle

**2. Hub = Workbench (Operational Fabric, Not System Replacement)**

- Core positioning: a Hub does not replace existing systems — it creates an operations and collaboration fabric on top of them. The underlying systems (Zeta Product Lines or third-party) continue to function; the Hub adds scenario-driven operations, governed collaboration, knowledge, and memory
- Business concept (Hub) → Platform realization (Workbench)
- Workbench anatomy (from [workbench-management](olympus-hub-docs/04-subsystems/workbench-management/README.md) and [workbench-anatomy](olympus-hub-docs/02-system-design/workbench-anatomy.md)):
  - Domain Model (entities, lifecycle states)
  - Scenarios, Triggers, Signals
  - Agents (human, AI), Task Queues
  - Knowledge Base, Memory Services
  - Machines and Tools — these are the integration points to underlying systems. Machines connect to Zeta Product Lines natively AND to any third-party system equally. This is the "system agnostic" property in practice
  - Governance (CAF, audit, oversight)
- Workbench lifecycle: Draft → Validated → Published → Active → Archived
- Hub properties table: domain encapsulation, system agnostic (Zeta Product Lines and third-party systems alike), entity-centric, agent-enabled, compliance-grounded, composable
- Adoption implication: a bank can stand up a Hub over its existing systems first (wrapping them as Machines), then introduce Zeta Product Lines incrementally — the Hub's value is independent of which underlying systems it connects to

**3. Stream = Coordinated Scenarios**

- The three representations:
  - **Stream Specification** (prescriptive, design-time): defines what coordinated Scenarios constitute this value delivery, what business context flows between them, what outcomes are expected. Created by Process Architects.
  - **Stream** (operative, runtime): an actual episode of value delivery for a specific customer/entity. Episodic — starts, pauses, resumes as Scenarios fire and resolve.
  - **Stream Trace** (observable, after-the-fact): the end-to-end reconstruction. Consumed by compliance, audit, CX, and operational improvement teams.
- Stream structural properties:
  - Business identity (which customer, which product, which intent)
  - Scenarios as constituent units (coordinated, not sequenced)
  - Context propagation across Scenarios (maps to Hub's cross-workbench context sharing)
  - Cross-Hub span (maps to cross-workbench patterns from [workbench-context-sharing.md](olympus-hub-docs/02-system-design/implementation-concepts/workbench-context-sharing.md))
  - Episodic lifecycle (minutes to months)
  - Scope boundary: organization and immediate ecosystem (vendors/third-parties as egress/ingress points)
- The OpenTelemetry trace analogy: where it holds, where it breaks, what it uniquely adds
- Relationship to Olympus Hub concepts:
  - Stream Specification → set of Scenario definitions within/across Workbenches with coordination rules
  - Stream → set of active/completed Requests linked by business context
  - Stream Trace → observable record reconstructed from Request history, CAF audit, and Memory Services
- Worked example: Credit Card Application Stream (6 Scenarios, 2 Hubs, coordination dependencies, conditional branching, time gaps)

**4. Loop = Agentic Improvement Cycle**

- Loop lifecycle: Segment → Hypothesize → Experiment → Monitor → Decide (with levers)
- Loop properties:
  - Data input (from Stream-generated data)
  - Levers (what the loop can change)
  - Governance (auto-approve, human-approve, committee-approve)
  - Frequency (real-time, daily, weekly, quarterly)
  - Scope (individual customer, segment, portfolio, entire Hub)
- How Loops map to Olympus Hub: Loops are Scenarios with agentic resolution that monitor and act on Hub data
- Cross-Hub Loops vs. Hub-specific Loops
- Banking Loop inventory per Hub (expanded from the existing list)

**5. Cross-Hub Patterns**

- Cross-cutting concerns: Customer Identity, Compliance, Fraud, Servicing
- How they're handled: shared Loops, cross-workbench Streams, infrastructure capabilities
- Mapping to Hub's cross-workbench context sharing and Workbench-as-Machine patterns

**6. Hub-to-Product-Line Mapping**

- Table mapping each Zeta Hub to corresponding Product Line(s)
- Hubs outside Zeta's current scope (Deposits/DDA, Lending, Trade Finance, Treasury) — explicit acknowledgment

**7. Hub Portfolio: Streams and Loops per Hub**

- At minimum: Credit Cards, Payments, CLM, Customer IAM, Merchant Acquiring
