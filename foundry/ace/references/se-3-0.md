# SE 3.0 — Situating ACE

> **Status: stub.** This document names the themes ACE engages with and the posture it takes relative to them. A curated bibliography and longer comparative discussion are intentionally deferred to a separate plan; this stub exists so readers know the conversation is being taken seriously and have a stable place to look for it.

## What the conversation is about

There is an active discussion in the software engineering community about a generational shift sometimes labeled **"Software Engineering 3.0" (SE 3.0)** — the period in which AI agents become first-class participants in the software development lifecycle, rather than auxiliary tools. The discussion spans academia, industry research, vendor positioning, and developer practice, and has not converged on a single definition.

Themes that recur across that discussion:

- **Agentic SDLC.** What does the SDLC look like when agents author requirements, designs, code, and tests, and not only humans?
- **Multi-agent orchestration.** How do multiple agents — possibly specialized — collaborate within a single development task or pipeline?
- **AI-augmented requirements engineering.** Can agents elicit, formalize, and trace requirements? With what guarantees?
- **Prompt programming as a discipline.** Treating prompts as first-class engineering artifacts: versioning, testing, reuse, evaluation.
- **Governance and trust models for AI-generated code.** Auditability, attribution, evidence, security posture for code where authorship is partly synthetic.
- **Human–agent team composition.** How are roles, skills, accountability, and review distributed across human and agent participants?

## ACE's posture

ACE takes a specific posture on these themes:

> Rather than treating AI as a layer to be added to a pre-existing SDLC, ACE redesigns the **unit of work**, the **station**, and the **interface** to make AI participation native.

Three concrete commitments illustrate the posture:

1. **The unit of work is a Scenario that decomposes into Tasks.** Tasks are entities, not free-form prompts. Provenance, ownership, and evidence are properties of the entity. (Compare with prompt-programming literature.)
2. **The station is a Workspace, not a generic IDE session.** Each Workspace has its own IDE context, its own scenario catalog, and its own repositories. (Compare with single-agent assistant patterns.)
3. **Governance is a property of every transition.** The Governance Workspace runs Scenarios on every handoff of Product Intent — handoffs are first-class events. (Compare with stage-gate audit models.)

These commitments are not unique to ACE in the abstract — components of each appear in the literature — but ACE's combination, and its insistence that all three are required *together* to employ agents effectively, is a stronger claim than the average paper makes.

## What ACE intends to contribute

If ACE has something to add to SE 3.0, it is the following:

- An explicit, working definition of an **environment** that turns three governing models (Product, Work, Operating) into delivery, rather than a model on paper.
- A repository taxonomy ([../repositories.md](../repositories.md)) tied to a formal information model (UPIM) so that "what gets recorded" is not left to ad-hoc convention.
- A **Product Evolution Cycle** ([../product-evolution-cycle.md](../product-evolution-cycle.md)) where parallelism, bidirectional edges, and optional return paths are first-class, not exceptions.
- A treatment of agents as **workforce members** rather than tools, with measurement parity to human peers.

These claims are intended to be backed by evidence over time — concrete platform behavior, measurable outcomes, comparative posture against published work.
