Rotation Model

Purpose

The rotation model defines how engineers move between Product Line Squads and Engagement squads: why we rotate, how long assignments last, how return is guaranteed, and how rotation supports career development. The goal is to balance platform quality (Product Line Squads retain expertise) with delivery (Engagement squads get capacity) and to preserve knowledge and build breadth.

Why Rotate

  • Knowledge preservation — When engineers return to Product Line Squads (or move to another Engagement), they bring back customer and solution context; knowledge is not locked in a single Engagement.
  • Breadth development — Engineers who rotate gain cross-platform and cross-Engagement experience; that supports EA and leadership paths.
  • Prevent burnout — Long stints on a single Engagement without option to rotate can lead to fatigue; rotation offers a path back to platform work or to a different Engagement.
  • Platform quality — Reserving some capacity in Product Line Squads and rotating Maintainers (see ) keeps platform expertise and governance strong.

Rotation does not mean everyone must rotate on a fixed clock; it means we have a model for how long people stay on Engagements and how they return or move.

Rotation Cadence Within Engagements

  • Not the whole team at once — Rotation is staggered so that the squad retains continuity. When one or two people rotate out, others stay (or new people rotate in).
  • Typical cadence — Individual rotation every 6–12 months (or per Engagement need). Exact cadence is set per Engagement and documented in staffing plan.
  • Key roles — Engineering Lead (EL), EA, and AVA may stay for the full Engagement; Product Line Engineers, Integration Engineers, Verification Squad engineers, and Product Line Analysts are more likely to rotate on a cadence.
  • Exception — Small Engagements or short delivery phases may have no rotation; the team stays until transition. Rotation is applied where Engagement duration is long (e.g. 1–2 years).

Return Guarantees to Product Line Squads

  • Commitment: When an engineer is assigned to an Engagement squad, their return to their Product Line Squad (or to another agreed role) is guaranteed and documented (e.g. return date or "return at end of Phase 3" or "return after N months").
  • Documentation: Staffing plan or Engagement charter includes return dates or rotation points for each assigned individual. Product Line Squad lead and EL agree before or early in the Engagement.
  • No indefinite assignment — We do not assign people without a defined return or rotation point. If the Engagement extends, return/rotation is renegotiated and documented.
  • Product Line Squad capacity — Product Line Squads reserve capacity so that returning engineers have meaningful platform work. We do not assign so many people that the Product Line Squad cannot function or that return is pointless.

EPM serves ELs on staffing and rotation coordination, ensuring visibility across squads and Engagements.

How Rotation Builds Career

  • Product Line depth — Engineers who spend most of their time in Product Line Squads (with limited or no Engagement rotation) build deep platform expertise; path to Staff/Principal Product Line Engineer. See .
  • Engagement breadth — Engineers who rotate through Engagements build solution and customer context; path to EA or Engineering Lead. Rotation is recognized as valuable experience.
  • Hybrid — Engineers who alternate between Product Line work and Engagement work can pursue Engineering Manager or Principal roles that span both.
  • Recognition — Inner source contributions and archetype contributions are recognized in performance and career discussions. Rotation is not a "pause" in career; it is a different kind of growth.

Rotation Planning and Visibility

  • Upfront — When squads are composed, rotation and return expectations are set and documented. Product Line Squad leads and ELs have visibility.
  • Ongoing — As the Engagement progresses, rotation dates are tracked (e.g. in staffing plan or resource tool). Product Line Squad leads use this for capacity planning; ELs use it for continuity planning (e.g. onboarding the next person before someone rotates out).
  • Forecasting — ERC Portfolio Program Manager (PPM) consolidates staffing demand across all Engagements and presents it to Product Line Squad leads, so that capacity and rotation can be planned ahead rather than managed reactively.

Exception Handling

  • Early return — If an engineer needs to return early (e.g. personal reason, platform emergency), Product Line Squad lead and EL agree; EL finds substitute or adjusts scope (EPM facilitates). Early return is not penalized.
  • Extended stay — If the Engagement needs someone to stay longer than originally planned, this is renegotiated with the engineer and Product Line Squad lead; return date is updated and documented. We avoid indefinite extension without agreement.
  • Multiple Engagements — An engineer may move from one Engagement to another (e.g. after rotation or return). That is still a "return" from the first Engagement's perspective; the second Engagement is a new assignment. Product Line Squad lead may agree to a direct move (Engagement A → Engagement B) if it fits capacity and career goals.
