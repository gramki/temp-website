Chapter 4: Enterprise Value Pools

Key Question: Where does enterprise transformation money actually sit?

This chapter maps the terrain of enterprise spending. Detailed value pool analysis for specific industries would require primary research and is beyond scope.

The Four Major Value Pools

Enterprise technology spending does not arrive in a single budget line. It flows through four distinct pools, each governed by different decision makers, different approval processes, and different competitive dynamics. Understanding which pool your revenue comes from is not an academic exercise — it determines your sales motion, your pricing architecture, your procurement path, and who you compete against.

IT Services Budgets

IT services budgets fund the people, processes, and vendors that keep enterprise technology running. This includes staff augmentation, application management, infrastructure operations, help desk services, and ongoing system maintenance. In large financial institutions, IT services budgets typically range from $500 million to $2 billion annually — the largest single category of technology spending.

Control sits with the CIO or CTO. Decisions are made through vendor panel agreements, master service contracts, and rate card negotiations. The competitive set is dominated by global systems integrators — Accenture, TCS, Infosys, Cognizant — and the evaluation criteria center on cost per resource, delivery reliability, and geographic coverage. Procurement is transactional: approved vendors bid on work packages, and the lowest qualified bidder wins.

This is the pool that funds "keeping the lights on." It is large, predictable, and fiercely price-competitive.

Software Platform Budgets

Software platform budgets fund the licensing, subscription, and maintenance fees for the technology products an enterprise uses — from core processing systems to middleware to analytics tools. In a large bank, software platform spending typically runs $200 million to $800 million annually, spread across hundreds of vendor relationships.

Control is shared between the CIO (for enterprise platforms), business line leaders (for domain-specific tools), and increasingly the CFO (who sees the aggregate cost). Decisions involve structured vendor evaluations, RFP processes, and multi-year contract negotiations. The competitive set is other platform vendors in the relevant category — Oracle vs. SAP, Temenos vs. Thought Machine, Salesforce vs. Microsoft Dynamics. Procurement is relationship-driven: vendor selection involves deep technical evaluation, reference checks, and contractual negotiation that can span twelve months or more.

This is the pool that SaaS-trained executives instinctively target. But in enterprise contexts, it represents only one of four pools — and not always the largest.

Outsourcing Budgets

Outsourcing budgets fund arrangements where an external provider operates an entire business function or technology domain on behalf of the enterprise. This includes BPO (business process outsourcing), infrastructure outsourcing, application outsourcing, and increasingly "as-a-service" managed operations. In banking, outsourcing budgets can range from $100 million to over $1 billion annually, depending on the institution's operating model.

Control sits with the COO or the head of the business function being outsourced, with heavy involvement from procurement and risk. Decisions are made through multi-year competitive tenders, often involving 6–12 month evaluation processes. The competitive set includes specialized outsourcing firms (Broadridge, FIS for financial services), global delivery providers (Wipro, HCL), and increasingly cloud platform providers offering managed services. Procurement is contract-intensive: outsourcing agreements are among the most complex commercial instruments in enterprise technology, with detailed SLAs, transition provisions, and exit clauses.

This pool is particularly important because it represents money that has already been extracted from internal operations. The enterprise has decided it does not want to do this work itself. The question is who does it.

Operational Expense Budgets

Operational expense budgets fund the day-to-day cost of running business functions — staffing, facilities, regulatory compliance activities, manual processes, and exception handling. These are not "technology" budgets in the traditional sense, but they represent enormous pools of spending that technology solutions can displace. A large bank's operations function — back-office processing, reconciliations, fraud investigation, compliance monitoring — can consume $1–5 billion annually.

Control sits with business line leaders, the COO, and functional heads. Decisions are made when a business case demonstrates that a technology solution can reduce operational headcount, eliminate manual processes, or improve throughput at lower cost. The competitive set is broad and often indirect — the solution competes not against another vendor but against the enterprise's own internal operations. Procurement follows the capital investment path: business case approval, executive committee sign-off, and multi-year funding allocation.

This is the least visible pool but often the largest. When an enterprise solutions company displaces manual operations with an automated platform, the revenue comes from operational budgets, not from IT.

The Critical Insight — Enterprise Money Is Not in SaaS Line Items

Enterprise technology spending is not organized the way a SaaS pricing page assumes. There is no "software subscription" line item in a bank's budget that a vendor can target with a per-seat-per-month offer. Enterprise money sits in structures that reflect how the institution thinks about its own transformation:

Transformation programs. The largest concentrations of enterprise spending are in named transformation programs — multi-year, board-sponsored initiatives with ring-fenced budgets. "Digital Banking Transformation," "Core Modernization," "Data Platform Consolidation" — these programs aggregate spending across IT services, software platforms, and operational change into unified budgets that can exceed $500 million over five years. The money is not in a software line item. It is in the program.

Strategic initiatives. Below the level of enterprise-wide transformation, strategic initiatives fund specific domain improvements: a new payments capability, a regulatory compliance program, a customer onboarding redesign. These initiatives draw from multiple budget pools and are governed by senior executive sponsors with dedicated funding.

Run-the-bank vs. change-the-bank allocations. Every large bank divides its technology spending into two fundamental categories: "run the bank" (keeping current systems operational, maintaining compliance, processing transactions) and "change the bank" (building new capabilities, modernizing infrastructure, launching new products). Typically, 65–75% of spending is run-the-bank and 25–35% is change-the-bank. Enterprise solutions companies must understand which side of this divide their revenue comes from — because the decision processes, budget authorities, and competitive dynamics are entirely different.

Vendor consolidation budgets. Large enterprises periodically undertake vendor rationalization — reducing the number of technology vendors to lower management overhead, negotiate better terms, and reduce integration complexity. These initiatives create their own budgets and their own competitive dynamics. A vendor consolidation program is not an opportunity to sell a new product. It is an opportunity to become the surviving vendor — which requires demonstrating breadth, reliability, and strategic alignment that a point-solution vendor cannot match.

Knowing which pool you are targeting determines four critical aspects of your business:

Sales motion. Transformation programs require executive-level consultative selling. SaaS line items require product demos and procurement compliance. The skills, seniority, and time investment are fundamentally different.

Pricing structure. Transformation budgets support value-based pricing tied to business outcomes. IT services budgets demand rate-card pricing. Operational expense budgets require TCO comparison against internal cost baselines. The pricing model must match the pool.

Procurement path. Transformation programs are procured through strategic vendor selection with C-level involvement. IT services are procured through rate-card competitions. Software platforms go through structured RFP processes. Each path has different timelines, gatekeepers, and evaluation criteria.

Competitive set. In transformation budgets, you compete against Accenture and Deloitte. In software platform budgets, you compete against other platform vendors. In outsourcing budgets, you compete against managed service providers. In operational expense budgets, you compete against the customer's own internal operations. Misidentifying your competitive set leads to misallocated sales effort and misdirected positioning.

In Banking

Banking technology budgets are structured around the run/change divide that governs all large financial institution spending. Understanding this structure is essential for any company selling into banks.

Run the bank absorbs 65–75% of total technology spending. This funds mainframe operations, legacy system maintenance, production support, regulatory reporting, cybersecurity operations, and the staff who keep current systems functioning. Run-the-bank budgets are governed by operational necessity — they are not discretionary. They are also intensely resistant to change, because any disruption to run-the-bank systems carries operational and regulatory risk.

Change the bank absorbs the remaining 25–35%. This funds new product development, digital transformation, core modernization, data platform initiatives, and regulatory change programs. Change-the-bank budgets are where enterprise solutions companies compete. They are governed by strategic priority, executive sponsorship, and business case justification.

Within change-the-bank, budgets are further organized by domain — payments, lending, cards, deposits, risk, compliance, data — and by vendor relationship. A bank's existing vendor relationships shape how new spending is allocated: a bank that has an enterprise agreement with a platform vendor will route new spending through that relationship before evaluating alternatives.

This structure explains a critical distinction that SaaS-trained teams often miss: "displacing a SaaS subscription" is fundamentally different from "winning a transformation budget allocation."

Displacing a SaaS subscription means competing for a software line item within an existing budget category. The evaluation is feature-to-feature, price-to-price, and the switching cost is the procurement effort plus data migration. The deal is typically $500K–$5M annually.

Winning a transformation budget allocation means becoming part of a named strategic program. The evaluation is outcome-to-outcome: which vendor can deliver the business transformation the program was created to achieve? The deal is typically $10M–$100M+ over the program's life. The competitive set is not other software vendors — it is other approaches to solving the strategic problem, including building internally, engaging an SI, or restructuring the program entirely.

Run-the-bank money is stable but margin-compressed. Vendors competing for run-the-bank budgets are evaluated on reliability, cost efficiency, and operational track record. Change-the-bank money is larger per engagement but episodic — it exists only as long as the strategic initiative is funded. Vendors competing for change-the-bank budgets are evaluated on transformation capability, domain expertise, and reference customers who have achieved comparable outcomes.

The banking technology company that understands which pool it is targeting — and designs its sales motion, pricing, and competitive positioning accordingly — has a structural advantage over one that approaches every bank with a product demo and a pricing page.

Enterprise money does not sit where SaaS instincts expect it. It sits in transformation programs, strategic initiatives, and operational budgets that are governed by business outcomes, not feature comparisons. The first strategic question for any enterprise solutions company is not "what should we charge?" but "which value pool are we targeting?" — because the answer to that question determines everything else.
