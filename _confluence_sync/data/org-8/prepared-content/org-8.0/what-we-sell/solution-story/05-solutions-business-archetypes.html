Chapter 5: Archetypes of Solutions Businesses

Key Question: What kind of solutions business are you building?

This chapter provides the taxonomy of solutions business archetypes. Each archetype's economics, scaling patterns, and organizational implications are explored in Chapters 9, 10, and 12.

Five Archetypes Within the Solutions Category

Chapter 0 introduced four business archetypes — Consumer Product, B2B SaaS, Enterprise Solutions, and SI/Consulting — to make visible the playbook confusion that occurs when instincts from one archetype are applied to another. But the "Enterprise Solutions" category is not monolithic. Within it, five distinct archetypes operate with different revenue models, talent requirements, and scaling logic. Conflating them is its own form of playbook confusion.

Capability Augmentation

The enterprise lacks a specific capability. You provide it. The solution fills a defined gap in the customer's technology or operational estate — adding something the enterprise does not have and cannot easily build.

Workday added human capital management to enterprises that had outgrown spreadsheets and on-premises HR systems. Snowflake added cloud data warehousing to organizations whose analytical infrastructure could not scale. In each case, the value proposition is precise: you do not have this capability; we provide it; it integrates into your existing environment. The capability is defined, bounded, and measurable. The engagement is focused on deployment, integration, and adoption — not on redefining how the enterprise operates.

Capability augmentation businesses scale well because the capability is standardized enough to be repeatable. The risk is that the capability becomes commoditized — once every vendor offers comparable data warehousing or HCM, differentiation erodes and pricing compresses.

Transformation Delivery

The enterprise needs a domain transformed end-to-end. You provide the methodology, the expertise, and the execution to achieve that transformation. The value is not in a product. It is in the outcome — the transformed state of the enterprise's operations, technology, or business model.

Accenture's industry practices and Deloitte Digital exemplify this archetype. When a bank engages Accenture to modernize its payments infrastructure, it is not buying software. It is buying the institutional capability to execute a complex, multi-year transformation involving strategy definition, vendor selection, system implementation, process redesign, change management, and operational transition. The deliverable is a transformed domain, not a deployed product.

Transformation delivery scales through methodology replication and talent leverage — the same transformation playbook applied across clients, executed by teams trained in the methodology. The risk is that each engagement is bespoke enough that knowledge does not compound, and the business cannot escape the linear relationship between headcount and revenue.

Product-Led Solutions

A product — proprietary technology IP — sits at the center, but the product alone is insufficient. Value is realized through implementation, configuration, integration, and ongoing optimization that adapts the product to the enterprise's specific environment. The product provides leverage; the services deliver the outcome.

Salesforce in its enterprise motion and SAP are the canonical examples. A bank does not "install Salesforce." It embarks on a multi-phase program to implement Salesforce Financial Services Cloud across its relationship management, advisory, and service functions — involving data migration, integration with core banking systems, workflow customization, user training, and organizational change management. The product is the nucleus. The implementation is the atom.

Product-led solutions businesses achieve the best unit economics when the product captures an increasing share of total contract value over time — when implementation effort decreases as the product matures and the implementation methodology becomes more efficient. The risk is the inverse: when every customer requires extensive customization, the product is a framework, not a platform, and the economics revert to services.

Strategic Intelligence Systems

The enterprise needs intelligence — analytical capability, decision-support infrastructure, or informational advantage — that it cannot build internally. The value is not in a workflow tool or a processing platform. It is in the ability to see, analyze, and act on information that would otherwise be invisible.

Palantir provides intelligence integration across fragmented data environments, enabling government agencies and commercial enterprises to perform cross-domain analysis that their internal systems cannot support. Bloomberg Terminal provides financial professionals with real-time data, analytics, and market intelligence that no single institution could aggregate independently. In both cases, the product is not a tool that automates a process. It is a system that creates an informational capability the enterprise could not otherwise possess.

Strategic intelligence systems command premium pricing because the value is tied to decision quality, not operational efficiency. The risk is proving that value — intelligence is inherently difficult to quantify, and buyers who cannot measure the ROI of better decisions may struggle to justify the investment.

Outcome/Managed Services

The enterprise decides it does not want to operate a function internally. You operate it on their behalf, delivering a defined outcome rather than a product or a project. The customer buys the result — payroll processed, transactions settled, reports filed — not the technology or the team that produces it.

ADP processes payroll for hundreds of thousands of companies. Paychex manages HR administration. Broadridge handles investor communications and securities processing for financial institutions. In each case, the customer has externalized a function, and the provider operates it as a service with defined SLAs and outcome-based pricing. The customer does not care how the work gets done. They care that it gets done — accurately, reliably, and at a cost lower than internal operation.

Outcome/managed services businesses scale through operational leverage — standardized processes, shared infrastructure, and automation that drives down the marginal cost of serving each additional customer. The risk is concentration: when you operate a critical function for an enterprise, a failure is not a product bug. It is an operational crisis for the customer, with regulatory, financial, and reputational consequences.

Most Companies Are Hybrids

In practice, few enterprise solutions companies inhabit a single archetype. SAP is product-led solutions at its core but offers transformation delivery through its consulting arm and managed services through SAP HANA Enterprise Cloud. Palantir is a strategic intelligence system that deploys through capability augmentation engagements. Accenture is primarily transformation delivery but has built product IP (such as its Avanade joint venture with Microsoft) and operates managed services at scale.

The hybrid reality is not the problem. The problem is confusion about which archetype is primary.

The primary archetype determines three things that cannot be blended:

Revenue model. A product-led solutions business prices around platform licensing with services attached. A transformation delivery business prices around time-and-materials or fixed-fee delivery. A managed services business prices around per-transaction or per-outcome fees. Each model requires different financial planning, different margin expectations, and different investor communication. A company that reports "platform ARR" but generates 60% of revenue from transformation delivery is telling one story while living another.

Talent model. Capability augmentation needs product engineers and implementation consultants. Transformation delivery needs industry consultants and program managers. Strategic intelligence systems need data scientists and domain analysts. Managed services need operations specialists and process engineers. When the primary archetype is unclear, the company hires for all five profiles, cannot develop a coherent career path for any of them, and struggles to build the deep expertise that differentiation requires.

Scaling logic. Product-led solutions scale by improving the product and reducing implementation effort. Transformation delivery scales by codifying methodology and training delivery teams. Managed services scale through operational automation and process standardization. These are different investment theses. A company that invests in product engineering when its revenue comes from transformation delivery is investing in the wrong scaling logic — building a better engine for a car it does not drive.

How to identify the primary archetype: look at where 60% or more of revenue originates, what the sales team actually sells in practice (not what the website says), and what capability the customer would miss most if the relationship ended. If the customer would miss the product, you are product-led. If they would miss the delivery expertise, you are transformation delivery. If they would miss the operational outcome, you are managed services.

The danger of archetype confusion is strategic incoherence. The company attempts to scale like a product business while generating revenue like a services business. It hires product managers when it needs engagement managers. It measures platform ARR when the growth engine is delivery revenue. Every organizational decision — compensation, structure, metrics, investment — pulls in a different direction because the company has not decided what it is.

In Banking

Banking technology companies map to these archetypes with instructive clarity — and the mapping explains their competitive positioning, growth trajectories, and strategic constraints.

Temenos — Product-Led Solutions. Temenos sells a core banking platform (Temenos Transact, Temenos Infinity) that sits at the center of multi-year implementation programs. The product is the nucleus: proprietary technology IP that processes banking transactions, manages accounts, and supports regulatory reporting. But no bank "installs Temenos" the way a team installs Slack. Every Temenos deployment involves twelve to thirty-six months of configuration, data migration, integration with the bank's existing systems, and organizational change. Temenos earns revenue from platform licensing and from the implementation ecosystem (both its own services arm and SI partners). Its strategic challenge is the product-led solutions archetype's defining risk: ensuring that product maturity reduces implementation effort over time, so that the product — not the services — drives margin expansion.

FIS/Fiserv — Outcome/Managed Services + Product-Led. FIS and Fiserv operate as hybrids, but their primary archetype is outcome/managed services. They process transactions, run card operations, manage settlement, and operate core banking functions on behalf of financial institutions. A community bank that uses FIS is not "using FIS software" in the way a team "uses Jira." The bank has outsourced its core processing to FIS, which operates the function and delivers the outcome — transactions processed, statements generated, regulatory reports filed. FIS and Fiserv also sell product platforms, but the managed services relationship is the economic center of gravity. This explains their competitive moat: displacing a managed services provider means the bank must either bring the function back in-house or transition to another provider — both of which are operationally risky and expensive.

Thought Machine — Capability Augmentation. Thought Machine provides a cloud-native core banking platform (Vault) that adds a specific capability banks lack: the ability to run core banking on modern, cloud-native infrastructure with real-time processing and configuration-driven product design. The value proposition is precise — Vault fills the architectural gap between what legacy cores can do and what modern banking requires. Thought Machine does not transform the bank's entire operation or operate the bank's core processing. It provides the platform capability and works with the bank (and SI partners) to deploy it. This positioning explains Thought Machine's growth pattern: it scales by proving the capability with reference deployments and expanding to banks with the same architectural gap.

Accenture Avanade — Transformation Delivery. When a bank engages Accenture for a digital transformation program, it is buying transformation delivery: the methodology, the delivery teams, the program management, and the industry expertise to execute a multi-year change. Accenture does not sell a proprietary banking platform. It sells the institutional capability to transform banking operations — often using third-party platforms (Microsoft, Salesforce, Temenos) as components within a delivery architecture that Accenture designs and executes. The competitive position is built on delivery track record, industry expertise, and the ability to staff large programs with credible teams. The strategic constraint is the transformation delivery archetype's defining risk: revenue scales linearly with headcount, and each engagement requires significant bespoke effort.

These mappings are not labels — they are strategic diagnostics. A banking technology company that believes it is product-led but generates most of its revenue from transformation delivery will make investment decisions (hiring product engineers, measuring platform ARR, cutting services headcount) that undermine its actual growth engine. The archetype must match the reality, not the aspiration.

Not all solutions businesses are alike, and the differences are not cosmetic. The primary archetype — capability augmentation, transformation delivery, product-led solutions, strategic intelligence, or outcome/managed services — determines revenue model, talent model, and scaling logic. The most dangerous strategic error is not choosing the wrong archetype. It is failing to choose at all — and building an organization that tries to scale five different ways simultaneously, excelling at none.
