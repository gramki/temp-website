Inputs Required for Full Zeta Solution Story Analysis

This document enumerates the information needed from Zeta leadership to complete a comprehensive assessment of Zeta's position in the enterprise solutions lifecycle. Each input is mapped to the relevant chapter of the Enterprise Solutions Playbook for context.

Why These Inputs Matter

The analyses in this directory are derived from two Mercury review decks (July 2025), Zeta's internal strategy documents, and public information. They reveal important signals — the HDFC revenue mix, the Pluxee protect posture, the India GTM pipeline, the contrasting US/India economic profiles. But they cannot answer the strategic questions that determine where Zeta sits on the solutions lifecycle and where it should go next.

The inputs below are organized by the playbook framework they inform.

Discovery & Problem Validation (Chapters 1–2)

1. Origin story of the compound banking problem.
How was the compound problem (systems gap + plumbing problem + modernization trap) originally discovered? Was it derived from HDFC engagement experience, from earlier Sodexo/Pluxee work, from the founders' prior experience, or from deliberate industry research? The discovery origin shapes whether this is a solutions-grade insight or a post-hoc rationalization.

2. Evolution timeline of the problem narrative.
When did Zeta's internal narrative shift from "we sell technology products" to "we solve compound enterprise problems"? Was this a gradual evolution or a specific strategic inflection point? Understanding the timeline reveals whether the organization has internalized the solutions identity or is still in transition.

3. Customer validation of the problem framing.
Have HDFC, the second-largest private bank prospects, or other target institutions explicitly validated the compound problem framing? Do they describe their own problem in these terms, or is this Zeta's diagnostic imposed on the customer's reality? The distinction matters for the consultative discovery motion.

Deal History & Win/Loss (Chapters 6–7)

4. Chronological deal history.
A timeline of major engagements — won, lost, abandoned, and in-progress — with key milestones: first contact, discovery/POC, proposal, negotiation, close, go-live. This reveals the actual sales cycle dynamics and how they compare to the book's enterprise buying dynamics framework.

5. Win/loss attribution.
For each completed deal cycle: what was the entry point (technology conversation, business conversation, or problem-led)? Which stakeholders championed, which blocked? What was the deciding factor — price, capability, trust, reference, or strategic fit? For lost deals: was the loss at discovery, deal-shaping, or delivery stage?

6. Exploration investment history.
The business model narrative describes a pre-commitment "Exploration" phase ($100K–$200K for regional banks, up to $1M for larger banks). How many exploration investments has Zeta made? What is the conversion rate from exploration to signed engagement? Is this viewed internally as deal-shaping (strategic) or as cost of sales (overhead)?

Economics & Revenue Evolution (Chapter 9)

7. Historical revenue mix by engagement.
SaaS vs. Studio vs. Non-SaaS revenue for each major engagement (HDFC PayZapp, HDFC Pixel, Pluxee, US/FIS, others) over the past 3 years. The trajectory matters more than the snapshot: is the SaaS proportion growing, stable, or declining in each engagement?

8. Unit economics per engagement.
What is the fully loaded cost of a studio team vs. the margin on platform SaaS revenue? At what deal size does the platform economics start to dominate the studio economics? This determines whether scale produces operational leverage or just proportional cost growth.

9. Target revenue mix.
Does the organization have an explicit target for SaaS-to-services ratio? If so, what is the target and timeline? If not, is the current mix considered acceptable, or is it a source of strategic tension?

10. Revenue quality assessment.
The Non-SaaS component includes passthrough costs (AWS), customer support, SRE, and project revenue. What proportion of total revenue is true platform margin vs. pass-through or cost-plus services? This determines whether the revenue is "solutions-grade" or "services-grade."

Delivery & Engagement Engineering (Chapter 8)

11. Engagement lifecycle completion rate.
How many engagements have completed the full Engagement Engineering lifecycle (Initiate → Discover → Build → Transfer → Complete)? What happens after "Transfer" — does the customer operate the platform independently, or does Zeta remain embedded?

12. Studio reduction dynamics.
Is the 25% studio reduction at HDFC being driven by HDFC (cost optimization / insourcing) or by Zeta (deliberate shift toward platform-only engagement)? Is this pattern expected to repeat in other engagements?

13. Inner-source velocity.
What percentage of engagement-specific work has been contributed back to Product Lines through the inner-source model? Is the flow from Engagement Engineering back to Product Lines actually working, or is it aspirational?

14. Engagement Engineering internal perception.
Is Engagement Engineering treated as a core competency or as a cost center? Are studio engineers seen as first-class contributors or as secondary to product engineers? The cultural signal determines whether solutions delivery is sustainable.

Scaling & Reference Dynamics (Chapter 10)

15. Reference customer inventory.
Which customers will publicly reference Zeta today? Beyond HDFC (Celent award, press), who else speaks on Zeta's behalf? What is the reference depth — a quote, a case study, a conference appearance, or a peer-to-peer conversation?

16. Reference risk from HDFC.
If HDFC proceeds with insourcing CS and operations, does this affect the reference value of the engagement? Does a relationship that is simultaneously deepening (more products) and contracting (fewer people) send a positive or mixed signal to prospects?

17. Competitive positioning in active deals.
How does Zeta position against competitors (Fiserv, TSYS, Pismo, Sarvatra, JusPay) — as a cheaper replacement, a modern-architecture alternative, or a compound-problem solver? The positioning reveals which archetype Zeta operationally embodies in sales.

18. India vs. US scaling models.
The India GTM targets top-10 banks with $5M+ ARR deals through direct consultative engagement. The US operates at least two distinct motions: the Axon/FIS channel (embedded platform distribution through an incumbent, reaching 680+ FIs) and direct enterprise relationships (Optum and others, where Zeta owns the customer). Are these understood as three different scaling strategies with different economics, or is one model intended as the primary growth engine? What is the revenue mix for the direct US business — does it resemble the Axon SaaS profile or the India services profile?

Platformization & Architecture (Chapter 11)

19. Platform consumption model.
Is Zeta's platform consumed primarily by Zeta's own engagement/studio teams, or are customers and partners building on it independently? The answer determines whether Zeta is in Phase 2 (productization — Zeta assembles) or Phase 3 (platform strategy — customers assemble) of the platformization curve.

20. Hub Way adoption.
Do product managers and engineers understand the Hub Way as a solutions-thinking framework (modeling work, not features), or do they treat it as a technical architecture? Is the Hub Way used in sales conversations to frame the customer's problem, or only in engineering?

21. Five-layer leverage actualization.
The five-layer model (Infrastructure → Integration → Frameworks → Product Lines → Engagement Engineering) is well-articulated. In practice, which layers are fully built and deployed vs. aspirational? Where are the gaps between the architecture diagram and production reality?

Strategic Identity (Chapter 12)

22. Self-description.
When Zeta describes itself to new hires, candidates, investors, and partners — which archetype does the description most closely resemble? Product company, platform company, solutions company, or something else?

23. Intended end-state.
From the book's four identity end-states (consulting firm, technology platform, industry infrastructure provider, managed services operator) — which does Zeta's leadership aspire to? Is there alignment across the leadership team, or do different leaders pull toward different end-states?

24. Archetype consciousness.
Is the split identity (India = 75% services, US = 78% SaaS) a known strategic reality that leadership is managing, or is it an unexamined consequence of different market entry strategies?

Organizational & Cultural (Prologue)

25. Anti-pattern prevalence.
The prologue identifies specific anti-patterns. Preliminary confirmation exists for several (PMs defining features instead of shaping solutions, GTM running funnel instead of consultative discovery, pricing as tiers instead of value-based). How deep do these run — are they individual habits, team-level culture, or org-level incentive structures?

26. Root cause.
Is the consumer/SaaS reflex a training problem (people don't know the solutions playbook), a hiring problem (people were hired for consumer/SaaS skills), or a cultural problem (the organization rewards consumer/SaaS behaviors)? The answer determines the intervention.

27. Success metrics alignment.
What does the organization measure today as success — and does it align with enterprise solutions metrics (value delivered, deal expansion, referencability, platform attachment rate) or SaaS metrics (MAU, DAU, NPS, NRR, engagement)?

Priority Ranking

If only five inputs could be obtained, these are the most diagnostic:

Priority Input Why
1 Historical revenue mix by engagement (#7) Reveals whether the trajectory is toward platform economics or services dependency
2 Intended strategic identity end-state (#23) Determines what all other decisions should optimize for
3 Studio reduction dynamics (#12) Reveals whether Zeta is driving or reacting to its own scaling transition
4 Win/loss attribution (#5) Shows which archetype actually wins deals vs. which is aspired to
5 Platform consumption model (#19) Determines platformization phase — the most important structural question
