Career Paths in the Operating Model

Purpose

This document describes career paths across the Engagement Operating Model. It is organized around functions (permanent career homes) and Engagement roles (temporary assignments). All paths follow the : career progression is evaluated on the functional axis; execution-axis leaders provide performance input for each Engagement.

Note: This document is a candidate for a future org-8.0-level "Talent Model" guide. For now, the Engagement Operating Model guide is the right home because career paths are defined by the roles this operating model establishes.

1. The Function-Role Model

Functions are permanent career homes on the functional axis. They own designation ladders, career progression, and people management. The functions are Engineering, Architecture, Product Management, Program/Delivery Management, SRE/Operations, and Account Management (or a broader Commercial function, depending on organizational structure).

Engagement roles are temporary assignments on the execution axis. Client Partner, CPA, EA, AVA, EL, EPM, EPO, SRE Lead, Squad PM, Scrum Master, and EO are roles you play during an Engagement (or, for Client Partner, per-client) — they are not designations. ERC assigns people from functions to Engagement-level roles based on Engagement complexity and individual designation level.

  • Your function determines who manages your career — your functional-axis leader sees your full Engagement history and evaluates growth across assignments.
  • Your Engagement role determines what you do right now — the execution-axis leader (EO, EPM) evaluates your performance within the current Engagement and provides input to your functional-axis leader.

This is universal — it applies to all functions, not just Engineering.

2. Management Spine

All functions follow the same structural spine:

Individual → Lead → Manager → Sr. Manager → Director → Sr. Director → VP

  • VP (or Director / Sr. Director if the org does not warrant a VP) owns the track. Each track has at most one VP.
  • If a track is led by a Director or Sr. Director, they report to another VP.
  • The people-management transition — who manages other people's careers — occurs at the Manager level (or Director level in Product and Program tracks; see below).

Title convention in Product and Program tracks: The Product and Program tracks carry "Manager" in functional titles at Individual and Lead levels (e.g. Product Manager, Program Manager). This is a naming convention, not an indication of people management. In these tracks, the people-management transition is at the Director level.

3. Functional Tracks

3a. Engineering

Designation ladder: Junior Engineer → Engineer → Senior Engineer → Staff Engineer (PE-1) → Principal Engineer

  • Principal Engineer is a terminal destination — deep technical IC spanning product line concerns. Not a waypoint to Architect.
  • Engineering Management branch: Senior Engineer → Engineering Lead (EL) → Engineering Manager → Sr. Engineering Manager. Engineering Managers manage engineers on the functional axis across PL and Engagement contexts.

One engineering ladder — engineers work across Product Line, Customer Product, Studio, and Verification contexts through rotation. There are no separate "Platform" and "Engagement" tracks; there are no QA, Test Engineer, or SDET titles. All engineers do engineering work (code, tests, code review, verification infrastructure) as context demands.

Engagement roles supplied: EL, Engineers. Engineers at Staff/Principal level may also play EA or AVA on smaller Engagements without changing their functional home — they remain in the Engineering function.

What builds seniority: Depth in a platform, breadth across Engagement contexts, cross-context experience (PL + Engagement), multi-Engagement experience. See .

3b. Architecture

Architect is a cross-functional designation — it requires synthesis of technical, functional, commercial, and operational contexts. Architecture shapes product lines; it is not "senior engineering." It is a distinct discipline with its own body of knowledge.

Designation ladder: Architect → Senior Architect → Principal Architect

  • Architecture Management branch: Architect → Architecture Manager → Sr. Architecture Manager (manages architects on the functional axis).

Engagement roles supplied: EA, AVA. ERC draws from the Architecture function's bench, matching architect seniority to Engagement complexity. For smaller Engagements, the EA or AVA role may be played by a Staff/Principal Engineer from the Engineering function; for complex Engagements, an Architect from the Architecture function is assigned.

What builds seniority: Multi-Engagement architecture experience, product line shaping, archetype and variability contributions, certification track record (for AVA).

3c. Product Management

Designation ladder: Associate Product Manager → Product Manager → Senior Product Manager

  • "Product Manager" is an IC title. The people-management transition is at the Director level.
  • Product leadership: Director of Product → Sr. Director of Product → VP of Product

Engagement roles supplied: EPO, Squad PM. Squad PM is typically at PM or Senior PM level; EPO at Senior PM or Director level, depending on Engagement scale.

What builds seniority: Customer discovery depth, requirements quality, cross-squad thinking, multi-Engagement product experience.

3d. Program / Delivery Management

Designation ladder: Program Coordinator → Program Manager → Senior Program Manager

  • "Program Manager" is an IC title. The people-management transition is at the Director level.
  • Delivery leadership: Director of Delivery → Sr. Director of Delivery → VP of Delivery

Engagement roles supplied: EPM, Scrum Master. Scrum Master is typically at Program Manager or Senior Program Manager level; EPM at Senior Program Manager or Director level.

What builds seniority: Process facilitation skill, multi-squad experience, multi-Engagement delivery record, cross-functional coordination.

3e. SRE / Operations

Designation ladder: SRE → Senior SRE → SRE Lead (designation) → SRE Manager

  • SRE leadership: Director of SRE → Sr. Director of SRE → VP of SRE

Engagement roles supplied: SRE Lead (the Engagement role). The same designation "SRE Lead" may be used for both the functional level and the role name.

What builds seniority: Operating model breadth (Fully Managed, Co-Managed, Customer-Operated), multi-Engagement operational experience, release coordination and operational handover quality.

4. Cross-Track Jumps

A cross-track jump is a deliberate career transition from one function to another. It requires demonstrating capability in the destination function's dimensions — it is not a promotion within a track.

Engineering → Architecture

  • From: Staff Engineer (PE-1), Principal Engineer, or Engineering Manager
  • Brings: Technical depth
  • Must develop: Functional, commercial, and synthesis breadth; product line shaping thinking

Product → Architecture

  • From: Senior Product Manager or Director of Product
  • Brings: Customer, domain, and commercial understanding
  • Must develop: Technical depth and system-level architecture thinking

Engineering Management → Architecture

  • From: Engineering Manager or Sr. Engineering Manager
  • Brings: Delivery breadth, team dynamics, cross-squad perspective
  • Must develop: Architecture thinking, commercial awareness, system design

Important: Principal Engineer is not a waypoint to Architect. Director of Product is not a waypoint to Architect. These are legitimate terminal destinations in their own tracks. Architect is a different destination — entering the Architecture function is a cross-track jump, not a promotion.

Playing an EA or AVA role in an Engagement is not a cross-track jump. A Staff Engineer playing EA remains in the Engineering function. The jump is when you change your functional home to the Architecture function.

6. Convergence at EO

EO is the most senior per-Engagement role. It is a role, not a function — the EO's functional home may be Architecture, Delivery, or Engineering.

EO draws from:

  • Architecture heritage — Architects who have guided multiple Engagement lifecycles
  • Delivery heritage — EPMs who have delivered multiple complex Engagements
  • Senior Engineering leadership — ELs or Engineering Managers who have demonstrated broad program leadership

EO requires both delivery leadership and technical/architectural judgment. The EO must understand both what is being built and how it is being delivered.

7. Dual-Axis Career Tracking

Career progression is evaluated on the functional axis — your function's leadership evaluates growth across Engagements:

Function Functional-axis home What the axis tracks
Engineering Engineering leadership Multi-Engagement delivery record, technical growth, inner source contributions
Architecture Architecture leadership Multi-Engagement architecture record, certification track record, archetype contributions
Product Product leadership Multi-Engagement product record, customer discovery depth, requirements quality
Program / Delivery Delivery leadership Multi-Engagement delivery record, process maturity, cross-squad effectiveness
SRE / Operations SRE / Platform Ops leadership Multi-Engagement operational record, operating model breadth
Account Management AM leadership Client relationship depth, multi-Engagement client portfolio, commercial alignment

How it works:

  • The functional-axis leader (VP or Director of the function) sees the full Engagement history and evaluates growth across assignments.
  • The execution-axis leader (EO, EPM) evaluates performance within the current Engagement and provides input to the functional-axis leader.
  • Multi-Engagement track record is the primary seniority signal for all functions.

Each functional track is owned by a VP (or Director/Sr. Director, reporting to another VP, if org size does not warrant a VP per track).

8. Recognition Mechanisms

Inner Source Contributions

  • Quality and impact of inner source PRs (merged to Product Line platforms) are considered in performance reviews. Engagement squad members who contribute back are recognized; Product Line Squads acknowledge the contribution.
  • Inner source contributions support progression on the Engineering ladder and are valued across functions. See .

Archetype Contributions

  • Archetype ownership and archetype update proposals are part of Architecture function performance and career progression. Maintaining blueprints, cookbooks, playbooks, and variability documentation is valued. See .
  • Engagement feedback that leads to archetype improvements is acknowledged; decision logs and retrospective input that feed PAC pattern extraction are valued.

Engagement Delivery Recognition

  • Successful Engagement delivery — on-time, on-scope, high customer satisfaction — is a key performance signal for EPM, EO, ELs, EA, and AVA.
  • Adoption metrics and customer outcomes (Engagement Success) are explicitly tracked and attributed. See .
  • Multi-Engagement track records are valued for progression to senior designations and for EO candidacy.

Verification and Certification Recognition

  • AVA certification track record — number of Engagements certified, certification quality (few post-certification defects), verification module reuse across Engagements.
  • Verification module quality — completeness, automation level, environment definition maturity. See .

Cross-Engagement Knowledge Sharing

  • Pattern extraction and coaching newer role-holders are recognized. Engineers and architects who share Engagement learnings (via retrospectives, PAC Practice Mode, mentoring) contribute to the organization's maturity.
  • Formal recognition: PAC may acknowledge cross-Engagement pattern contributions in Practice Mode sessions.
