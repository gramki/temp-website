Client Partner — Role Coaching Guide

|

The Client Partner is the senior-most per-client role, owning the strategic client relationship across one or more Engagements. The role is scoped per-client (not per-Engagement). The Client Partner sits at the top of the execution-axis hierarchy — EO reports to Client Partner. The functional home is Account Management.

1. Empowerment

  • Client-facing decisions. You decide on stakeholder management, commercial alignment, and relationship strategy. When delivery decisions affect client commitments, EO recommends — you decide.
  • Client governance. You own client-side governance meetings, executive briefings, and escalation to client leadership.
  • Commercial alignment. You ensure delivery and contract terms stay in sync. You surface misalignment to Account Management; AM negotiates.
  • What you cannot override. You cannot override EO on internal delivery authority. You cannot override AVA's release-block authority — this is non-negotiable, even under extreme client pressure. If you disagree with a release block, the path is: EPM mediates → EO decides. An EO override is a documented governance event.

2. Assignment

ERC assigns the Client Partner at Stage 1 (). If the client already has existing Engagements, a Client Partner may already be in place. ERC ensures at least one Client Partner Associate (CPA) is staffed alongside the Client Partner. For complex multi-Engagement clients, multiple CPAs may be assigned.

Scope: Per-client. One Client Partner per client; may span multiple Engagements with that client.

3. Ambition

First 90 Days (or First Engagement with a New Client)

  • Learn the client's organisation. Map every stakeholder who influences Engagement outcomes — decision-makers, budget holders, technical counterparts, change sponsors. Understand the client's internal politics and governance cadence.
  • Align with EO(s). Meet each Engagement Owner within your client scope. Establish the authority boundary early — you own client-facing decisions; EO retains full internal delivery authority. Agree on communication protocols: what the client hears from you directly, what they hear from EPM, and how you stay in sync.
  • Brief your CPA(s). Set expectations for governance preparation, stakeholder tracking, and cross-Engagement coordination. The CPA is your operational backbone — invest in the relationship.
  • Understand the commercial context. Review contract terms, scope, and any existing delivery-commercial misalignment. Meet with Account Management leadership to understand the broader commercial relationship.

What Great Looks Like

  • The client has a single, trusted point of strategic contact who speaks with authority about the relationship, commercial alignment, and Engagement direction.
  • EOs can focus on internal delivery because they trust the Client Partner to manage client expectations, governance, and escalation.
  • Cross-Engagement coordination (for multi-Engagement clients) happens through you and your CPA, not through ad-hoc EO-to-EO conversations.
  • Governance meetings are well-prepared (CPA prepares), well-run (you lead), and result in clear actions (you follow up).
  • Commercial and delivery are in sync — no surprises for Account Management or the client.
  • You anticipate client concerns before they become escalations. The client sees you as a partner, not a messenger.

4. Commitments

Dimensions

Dimension Client Partner SLA
Fitment Client expectations match what is being delivered. Scope-need alignment is validated at every major milestone. Gap between client expectation and Engagement output is surfaced before it becomes a dispute.
Cost Delivery scope and commercial terms remain in sync. Scope changes are assessed for commercial impact and flagged to AM before they accumulate. Budget burn rate and commercial alignment are tracked through the CPA.
Predictability Client-facing milestones are communicated accurately. The client is never surprised by delays — early warning is delivered before the deadline, not after. Governance meetings provide a reliable view of Engagement health.

Contractual Connection — Tier 1 (Direct)

The Client Partner interfaces directly with the client on contract-level commitments. When SLA trends indicate risk to a contractual milestone, the Client Partner coordinates with EO and EPM to surface it to the client with a remediation plan — not a surprise. Commercial misalignment is flagged to Account Management for negotiation.

5. Collaboration

Key Relationships

CPA Governance preparation, stakeholder tracking, cross-Engagement status consolidation Clear delegation, feedback on quality, career development investment

Tensions and Navigation

Client Partner ↔ EO. You want to maximise client satisfaction and say "yes" to client requests. EO owns delivery feasibility and may say "not possible in this timeline" or "not ready for release." Both positions are legitimate — the client relationship matters and engineering integrity matters.

  • When delivery decisions affect client commitments → EO recommends; you decide. Ensure you understand the technical rationale before overriding.
  • When client demands affect delivery feasibility → bring to EO; EO assesses. Do not commit to the client before the assessment.
  • Never override AVA release-block authority, even under extreme client pressure. AVA's certification is independent. If you disagree with a release block, escalate through EPM → EO. An EO override is a governance event.

Client Partner ↔ EPM. Both of you are customer-facing, but with different scopes. EPM owns delivery communication and the "what's happening" narrative. You own relationship strategy and the "what it means for our partnership" narrative. Overlap is inevitable.

  • Agree early on the communication split: EPM leads delivery status; you lead strategic and commercial conversations. Both attend client governance, but with defined roles.
  • If a client escalation arrives to both of you, coordinate before responding. Contradictory messages destroy client trust faster than a delayed response.

Client Partner ↔ Account Management. AM owns the commercial relationship and contract. You own the client relationship at the Engagement level. Scope changes, contract amendments, and renewal conversations can create friction if you and AM are not aligned.

  • You surface commercial misalignment; AM negotiates. Do not negotiate contract terms directly with the client — flag to AM and coordinate.
  • Ensure AM has visibility into delivery progress that affects commercials. Surprises at renewal are failures of coordination, not of delivery.

6. Management

Dual-Axis Reporting

  • Execution axis: Assigned by ERC. EO(s) and EPM(s) provide input on your effectiveness within specific Engagements.
  • Functional/career axis: Account Management leadership evaluates your career growth across client assignments — client relationship depth, commercial alignment quality, multi-Engagement coordination.

Performance Input

Request explicit feedback from EOs after each Engagement milestone (go-live, Transfer, Complete). EPM feedback on customer-facing communication quality is particularly valuable. AM leadership evaluates your trajectory across clients, not just within one.

7. Accountability

Escalation: Dos and Don'ts

Do:

  • Escalate CP ↔ EO disagreements to ERC — ERC assigned both roles and is the designated escalation endpoint ().
  • Before escalating: document the issue, what was tried (direct conversation with EO), options, and your recommended path.
  • Escalate client-side risks to EO and EPM early — giving them time to assess and recommend, rather than presenting a fait accompli.

Don't:

  • Go directly to Engineering leadership, Architecture leadership, or PAC to override EO's delivery decisions. The execution axis goes through EO, not around EO.
  • Override AVA release-block authority. If you believe a release block is wrong, the path is: EPM mediates → EO decides. An EO override is a documented governance event.
  • Commit delivery timelines to the client before EPM and EO have confirmed feasibility. Saying "yes" to the client and then asking the delivery team to make it work is a trust-destroying pattern.
  • Escalate the same issue to AM leadership and ERC simultaneously without clarity on what each body should decide.

When Commitments Are Missed

Client Partner commitments are primarily commitment-based (per the accountability framework): governance timeliness, commercial alignment accuracy, and early warning delivery are binary — done or not done. When a Fitment or Predictability SLA is missed (e.g. the client is surprised by a delay you should have surfaced), the response is immediate transparency, impact containment, and a revised communication protocol — not a retrospective on learning. The client's trust in the organisation depends on your reliability.

8. Objectives in Perspective

Your immediate objectives — client satisfaction, commercial alignment, governance quality — serve a broader purpose. The Engagement Operating Model exists to deliver customer-specific product instantiations from a shared platform. Your role ensures that the client sees a coherent, trustworthy partner, while the internal team has the space to build with engineering integrity.

When you manage client expectations well, you protect the EA's inner source bar, the AVA's certification authority, and the EPM's ability to deliver realistic timelines. When you manage poorly — making commitments without feasibility checks, or pressuring the team to skip quality gates — you undermine the model that makes delivery sustainable across multiple Engagements.

The Client Partner's success is measured not just by the current client's satisfaction, but by whether the Engagement's delivery model remains healthy enough to serve the next client, and the one after that.
