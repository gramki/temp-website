Career Guides — Coaching for Functions and Roles

Purpose

This folder contains coaching-style guides for practitioners in the Engagement Operating Model. It answers the question: "How do I succeed in my function and in my current role?"

This is not a reference for what functions and roles are — that lives in (functions, ladders, dual-axis model) and (role definitions, authority, reporting). These coaching guides assume you have read or can reference those documents and focus instead on how to build craft, navigate interactions, and grow.

Audience

Anyone working within the Engagement Operating Model who wants to understand what is expected at their level, how to succeed in their current role, and how to prepare for the next step.

Two Types of Guides

Function Coaching (functions/)

One doc per functional track. Organised by seniority level (from the designation ladder in ). For each level:

  • Required craft, skills, competencies — what you must be able to do at this level, grounded in the operating model.
  • Roles you are expected to play and challenges to be ready for — which Engagement roles are typically staffed from this level, and what those roles demand.
  • How to learn — practical mechanisms in this model (rotation, dual-axis feedback, inner source, PAC Practice Mode, multi-Engagement exposure) plus curated external reading.

Role Coaching (roles/)

One doc per Engagement role. Each doc opens with a brief Introduction (purpose, scope, position in hierarchy, functional track) making it nearly self-sufficient, followed by eight sections:

  1. Empowerment — what authority the role carries, what decisions it can make, what it cannot override.
  2. Assignment — how the role is assigned (ERC / EO / PPM), at which formation stage, typical scope.
  3. Ambition — first 90 days, what "great" looks like, career trajectory.
  4. Commitments — SLA-based delivery commitments across applicable dimensions (Quality, Predictability, Velocity, Fitment, Cost), with contractual connection tiered by role.
  5. Collaboration — key relationships and interaction tensions with navigation guidance.
  6. Management — dual-axis reporting, how performance input flows.
  7. Accountability — escalation model, dos and don'ts, what happens when commitments are missed.
  8. Objectives in Perspective — how the role's objectives fit within the broader Engagement and organisational context.

Plus Further Learning — internal links and curated external references.

Authoring Rules

All coaching documents must follow these rules:

  1. Grounded in the Engagement Operating Model. Every section must reference concrete constructs: Engagement, lifecycle phases, formation stages, ERC, dual-axis reporting, squad types, artifact groups, inner source, verification/certification, Client Partner → EO authority boundary, escalation paths, and governance bodies. Do not write advice that could apply to any company.

  2. Grounded in business context. Explain why this model exists and why the behaviour matters: assembly from Product Lines (not bespoke projects), extend don't fork, platforms as accelerators, Engagement Success (adoption and value, not just delivery), governed lifecycle.

  3. No platitudes or broad generalizations. Every piece of advice must be tied to a specific interaction, decision, artifact, or governance path. "Be a strong communicator" is not acceptable; "Summarise squad progress against the solution architecture and known gaps (inner source vs custom) so the EPM can report to the Client Partner without chasing multiple threads" is.

  4. Learning and Further reading in both function and role docs. Internal links (roles.md, career-paths.md, governance.md, engagement-formation.md, etc.) plus external references from the curated set. Each external entry must have a stable link and a one-line "why relevant to this model" note.
