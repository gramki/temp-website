Program / Delivery Management — Function Coaching Guide

|

Purpose

This guide helps delivery practitioners at every level understand what the Engagement Operating Model expects of them and how to grow within the Program / Delivery function. This function supplies the EPM and Scrum Master roles. It also supplies CPA (when the CPA comes from a delivery background) and Stream Delivery Leads in scaled Engagements.

The delivery function in this model is distinctive: it exists at the intersection of customer-facing accountability (Engagement Success — readiness, adoption, value), multi-squad coordination, dual-axis feedback, and commercial alignment with Account Management.

Program Coordinator

Required Craft, Skills, Competencies

  • Craft: Track and consolidate squad-level progress into the integrated view that EPM needs. Maintain dependency maps, risk registers, and action item logs. Prepare governance meeting materials for the EPM.
  • Skills: Use the dual-axis reporting model correctly — understand that ELs report to EPM on the execution axis, but career evaluation happens in their Engineering function. Consolidate status from multiple ELs without chasing — design a reporting cadence that ELs can sustain alongside their squad work.
  • Competencies: Distinguish process coordination from process imposition. The Scrum Master facilitates squad-level process; the Program Coordinator (and later EPM) coordinates across squads. Respect the boundary — don't prescribe how squads run their sprints; coordinate what they deliver and when.

Roles Expected to Play and Challenges

  • Scrum Master on simpler Engagements (1-2 squads). May assist EPM with coordination tasks on complex Engagements.
  • Challenge: Working in a temporary team assembled from different Product Line contexts. Establishing a coordination cadence quickly matters — the squad was not together last month and may not be together next month.
  • Challenge: Understanding that status consolidation is a service to EPM, not a bureaucratic exercise. If EPM has to re-ask ELs for information you already collected, the consolidation failed.

How to Learn

  • On the job: Serve as Scrum Master on a 2-squad Engagement. Shadow an EPM during the Discover-to-Build transition to understand how the integrated view is constructed from squad inputs.
  • Feedback: EPM provides execution-axis input. Delivery leadership evaluates growth across Engagements.
  • Knowledge sharing: Observe how experienced EPMs run cross-squad coordination meetings. Note what they track vs. what they delegate.
  • External reading: Nexus Framework (Bittner et al.) — scrum.org/resources/scaling-scrum — multi-team Scrum coordination that maps to the EPM's cross-squad orchestration challenge.

Program Manager

Required Craft, Skills, Competencies

  • Craft: Own the integrated progress view across 2-4 squads. Run cross-squad coordination — dependency management, risk escalation, and cadence alignment. Manage scope changes by ensuring Account Management is aware of any scope changes that affect commercial terms (scope-commercial alignment). Serve as the primary process facilitator across multiple squads when playing Scrum Master.
  • Skills: Drive the staffing coordination loop — submit staffing requests to PPM, track commitments, manage rotation planning with ELs. Build the EPM-level risk and dependency view from squad-level inputs. Communicate the integrated view to EO in a way that enables EO decision-making without requiring EO to attend every squad standup.
  • Competencies: Manage the tension between EPM's process consistency expectations and EL's squad-level flexibility. Process consistency across squads enables the integrated view; squad autonomy enables delivery velocity. Neither is optional — the Program Manager must navigate both daily.

Roles Expected to Play and Challenges

  • Scrum Master across 2-3 squads. EPM on smaller Engagements.
  • Challenge: Serving multiple squads with different ELs, different cadences, and different maturity levels. Each squad needs adaptation, not uniformity.
  • Challenge: Being process-oriented in a delivery-pressure environment. When delivery is behind, ceremonies and process feel like overhead — but they are how the squad recovers. Advocate for process without becoming rigid.

How to Learn

  • On the job: Take a Scrum Master assignment spanning 3 squads with diverse contexts (CP, Studio, PL). Seek an EPM assignment on a small Engagement after at least two Scrum Master assignments.
  • Feedback: Dual-axis — EPM rates cross-squad coordination; Delivery leadership evaluates process maturity and multi-Engagement track record.
  • Knowledge sharing: Contribute a coordination pattern (e.g. how to run a dependency review across 4 squads in 30 minutes) to PAC Practice Mode or a delivery community of practice.
  • External reading: Agile and Lean Program Management (Rothman) — jrothman.com — scaling collaboration across multiple teams; directly applicable to the EPM's multi-squad coordination in this model.

Senior Program Manager

Required Craft, Skills, Competencies

  • Craft: Own the full EPM scope — integrated progress view, risk and dependency management, commercial alignment, Engagement Success (readiness, adoption, value delivery), and customer-facing communication. Serve ELs on staffing needs and rotation coordination. Drive scope change management and ensure delivery scope remains aligned with contract terms.
  • Skills: Own the Engagement Success function — readiness, adoption, and value delivery beyond engineering completion. This is not a handoff to a separate team; the EPM drives it. Work with EPO on customer training and enablement planning. Communicate progress and risks to Client Partner and EO, calibrating detail level for each audience. Coordinate with Account Management on commercial alignment without becoming a commercial negotiator.
  • Competencies: Synthesise status from multiple squads into a coherent picture — squads operate at different cadences with different blockers; the "integrated view" is harder than it sounds. Manage scope creep at the contract-delivery intersection — customers always want more; contracts constrain; delivery teams have capacity limits. The EPM sits at the intersection of all three.

Roles Expected to Play and Challenges

  • EPM on complex Engagements (4+ squads). May serve as Stream Delivery Lead in scaled Engagements under a senior EPM.
  • Challenge: Being the single customer-facing contact without having direct control over squads. The EPM depends on ELs and EA for information, but the customer expects answers from the EPM.
  • Challenge: Driving Engagement Success (adoption) when it depends on customer actions the EPM can influence but not control. Customer training, change management, and operational readiness are adoption drivers — but the customer must execute.
  • Challenge: Client Partner coordination — the Client Partner owns client-facing decisions; the EPM owns delivery communication. The boundary can be ambiguous. Align early on who says what to the customer and when.

How to Learn

  • On the job: Lead a complex Engagement through the full lifecycle (Discover → Complete). Own the Engagement Success plan, not just the delivery plan.
  • Feedback: EO rates integrated view quality and customer-facing effectiveness. Delivery leadership evaluates multi-Engagement delivery record and Engagement Success outcomes.
  • Knowledge sharing: Mentor Scrum Masters and Program Managers. Contribute Engagement Success patterns to the delivery community.
  • External reading: Practical Customer Success Management (Adams) — link — customer success framework applicable to the Engagement Success function that EPM owns.

Delivery Leadership (Director → VP)

Director of Delivery / Sr. Director / VP

  • Craft: Own the Delivery function's career path. Evaluate delivery practitioners across Engagements. Shape delivery practice, process standards, and Engagement Success methodology.
  • Key model construct: Participate in ERC — advise on EPM and Scrum Master designation-to-complexity matching, advocate for Engagement Success as a delivery function (not a separate team). Resolve cross-Engagement delivery conflicts when multiple Engagements compete for the same delivery practitioners.
  • Challenge: Providing meaningful career guidance when your reports' daily work is embedded in Engagements you do not lead. Build relationships with EOs to ensure execution-axis feedback flows reliably.
  • Challenge: Defining and evolving the Engagement Success methodology. "Success" means different things for different operating models (Fully Managed vs. Co-Managed vs. Customer-Operated); the Delivery VP must ensure EPMs are equipped for all variants.
