## 1. Success in the Role

### First 90 Days (or First Client Assignment)

- **Map the landscape:** Absorb the client's organisational structure, stakeholder relationships, ongoing Engagements, and governance cadence. Review the Client Partner's stakeholder map and update it with your observations. Understand which Engagements are active, who the EOs and EPMs are, and what lifecycle phase each Engagement is in.
- **Establish the prep cycle:** Build a repeatable governance preparation workflow — agenda drafts, data collection from EPMs and EOs, executive briefing templates, action item tracking. The Client Partner should not have to tell you what to prepare; you should anticipate it.
- **Align with EPMs:** Introduce yourself to each Engagement's EPM. You will be collecting status, consolidating cross-Engagement views, and coordinating logistics. Establish a reporting cadence that EPMs can sustain without extra overhead.
- **Understand the commercial baseline:** Review contract terms for each Engagement. Know what scope changes have been flagged and what the commercial alignment status is.

### What "Good" Looks Like

- Governance meetings are prepared, on time, and the Client Partner walks in with all context needed.
- Cross-Engagement status is consolidated and current — the Client Partner can answer "how is this client doing overall?" without chasing EOs and EPMs individually.
- Stakeholder maps are maintained and accurate. Relationship changes (new stakeholders, departures, reorganisations) are captured promptly.
- Commercial alignment is tracked — scope change impacts are flagged to the Client Partner before they become disputes.
- Internal coordination (ERC reporting, AM coordination) happens without the Client Partner's direct involvement in logistics.

### Key Relationships

| Role | What You Need from Them | What They Need from You |
|------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Client Partner | Clear delegation, feedback on quality, career investment, decision context | Governance preparation, stakeholder tracking, cross-Engagement consolidation, commercial tracking |
| EPMs (across Engagements) | Timely status, risk updates, scope change notifications | Efficient information requests, minimal overhead, clear purpose for each data point |
| EOs (across Engagements) | Context on Engagement-level decisions, escalation awareness | Coordination support, aligned logistics for cross-Engagement events |
| Account Management | Commercial context, contract terms, renewal/expansion timeline | Scope-commercial alignment tracking, early flags on misalignment |
| ERC | Client portfolio decisions, role assignment context | Reporting input, formation feedback |

### Performance Input and the Dual Axis

You report to the **Client Partner** on the execution axis. Your functional home is either **Account Management** or **Program/Delivery**, depending on your background. Your functional-axis leader evaluates career growth across client assignments. The Client Partner provides input on your execution effectiveness — preparation quality, coordination timeliness, stakeholder management. Request explicit feedback from the Client Partner monthly, not just at assignment end.

## 2. Expected Interactions, Tensions, and How to Navigate

### CPA ↔ Client Partner

**Tension:** The Client Partner delegates operational work to you, but the boundary between "preparation" and "decision" can be ambiguous. The Client Partner may expect you to handle conversations that are actually decision-making, or may take over preparation work that erodes your accountability.

**How to navigate:**
- Clarify delegation boundaries early: "I prepare and present options; you decide." If you find yourself making decisions the Client Partner should make, flag it.
- If the Client Partner takes over preparation details, ask what you should focus on instead — the role is defined by its operational accountability, and if that erodes, the role loses value.

### CPA ↔ EPMs (across Engagements)

**Tension:** You need information from multiple EPMs regularly. EPMs are busy with their own Engagements and may see your requests as overhead. From their perspective, they already report to EO; reporting to CPA feels like double-reporting.

**How to navigate:**
- Design your information requests to piggyback on EPM's existing reporting. If the EPM already produces a weekly status for EO, ask for a copy rather than a separate report.
- Explain the purpose — the Client Partner needs a cross-Engagement view; the CPA constructs it. If EPMs understand why, compliance improves.
- Consolidate requests. Don't ask five times in a week; ask once with a clear format.

### CPA ↔ ERC

**Tension:** ERC expects reporting on the client portfolio. You may not have all the information (delivery details are EO/EPM scope) and may need to coordinate across multiple Engagements to produce a coherent view.

**How to navigate:**
- Build the reporting cadence into your preparation workflow. Don't produce ERC reports as a separate activity; produce them as a by-product of the cross-Engagement consolidation you already do for the Client Partner.

## 3. Escalation: Dos and Don'ts

**Do:**

- Escalate to the **Client Partner** when you encounter coordination issues you cannot resolve (e.g. an EPM who consistently does not provide status, a stakeholder who bypasses the governance process).
- Escalate to **AM leadership** (your functional home, if AM) when career-related concerns arise or when client portfolio decisions are needed that exceed the Client Partner's scope.
- Before escalating: document the issue, what you tried, and your recommended action.

**Don't:**

- Escalate directly to EO or ERC without the Client Partner's awareness. You support the Client Partner; escalation goes through or with them, not around them.
- Make client-facing decisions that are the Client Partner's authority — even under time pressure, defer and brief the Client Partner rather than acting unilaterally.
- Treat the CPA role as "waiting for instructions." The role is proactive — anticipate what the Client Partner needs and have it ready before they ask.
