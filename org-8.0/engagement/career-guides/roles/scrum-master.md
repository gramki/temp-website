# Scrum Master — Role Coaching Guide

[← Back to Career Guides](../README.md) | [Role Definition](../../roles.md#scrum-master)

---

The Scrum Master is a **process facilitation role** serving 1-3 squads within an Engagement. The Scrum Master owns ceremonies, impediment removal, cadence, and team health — enabling ELs to focus on engineering leadership and delivery. The role is scoped **per-squad** (1-3 squads). The Scrum Master reports to EPM on the execution axis. The functional home is **Program / Delivery Management**.

---

## 1. Empowerment

- **Process facilitation.** You own how the sprint runs — ceremonies (standups, sprint planning, retrospectives, demos), cadence, and timebox management. The Squad PM decides *what* to build; the EL decides *how* to build it; you decide *how the process runs*.
- **Ceremony ownership.** You facilitate all squad ceremonies. When delivery pressure mounts, ceremonies are not overhead to be cut — they are how the squad recovers and sustains delivery.
- **Impediment identification and escalation.** You identify impediments, categorise them (squad-level, cross-squad, Engagement-level), and escalate to the right person — EL for squad-level, EPM for cross-squad, EO for Engagement-level.
- **Team health monitoring.** You notice workload imbalance, collaboration friction, or morale issues before they become crises and surface them to EL and EPM.
- **What you cannot do.** You do not direct engineering work — that is EL's domain. You do not make product decisions — that is Squad PM's domain. You do not set process expectations for the Engagement — that is EPM's domain. Your authority is in facilitation and process quality.

**Note:** The Verification Squad does not have a Scrum Master. AVA directs through architectural authority. If you are assigned to squads that include a Verification Squad, that squad is outside your scope.

---

## 2. Assignment

Scrum Masters are staffed through PPM at **Stage 3** ([engagement-formation.md](../../engagement-formation.md#stage-3--squad-staffing-through-ppm-at-discover)). Scrum Masters are drawn from the Program/Delivery function.

**Scope:** 1-3 squads within an Engagement. One Scrum Master may serve multiple squads.

---

## 3. Ambition

### First 90 Days (or First Increment)

- **Assess your squads.** You may serve 1-3 squads. Each squad has a different context (CP, Studio, PL), a different EL, different team dynamics, and may be at a different maturity level. Before imposing any process, observe each squad's current cadence and working style. Adaptation to context is more valuable than uniform process.
- **Establish ceremonies.** Set up sprint planning, standups, retrospectives, and demos for each squad. Agree with each EL on ceremony cadence and format. Be prepared to adapt.
- **Align with EPM.** EPM sets process expectations for the Engagement. Understand what minimum coordination EPM requires. You are the process link between EPM and the squads.
- **Learn the product-process boundary.** You facilitate process; the Squad PM manages product; the EL manages engineering and delivery. All three authorities operate within the same squad — your success depends on respecting these boundaries.

### What Great Looks Like

- Ceremonies happen on cadence and are effective — sprint planning produces clear commitments; retrospectives produce actionable improvements; standups surface blockers early.
- Impediments are identified and escalated promptly. You categorise (squad-level vs. cross-squad vs. Engagement-level) and escalate to the right person.
- Team health is monitored. You notice workload imbalance, collaboration friction, or morale issues before they become crises.
- Process is consistent enough across your squads that EPM can maintain the integrated view, but flexible enough that each squad works effectively in its own context.
- When serving multiple squads, you surface common impediments and alignment opportunities to EPM — you are the cross-squad process radar.

---

## 4. Commitments

### Dimensions

| Dimension | Scrum Master SLA |
|---|---|
| **Predictability** | Ceremonies happen on cadence. Sprint commitments are well-formed (clear scope, acceptance criteria, capacity-matched). Process consistency enables EPM to maintain the integrated view. When process breaks down (ceremonies skipped, cadence lost, impediments unaddressed), early warning is delivered to EPM. |
| **Velocity** | Impediments within the squad are identified and either resolved or escalated promptly. Ceremony effectiveness enables the squad to sustain delivery — sprint planning is focused, retrospectives produce action, standups surface blockers before they stall work. When serving multiple squads, common impediments are consolidated and escalated to EPM once, not solved separately in each squad. |

### Contractual Connection — Tier 3 (Roll-up)

The Scrum Master does not interface with the contract. Scrum Master SLAs aggregate upward through EL and EPM. When process is effective, squads commit accurately, deliver predictably, and surface impediments before they cascade — enabling the EPM to forecast reliably and the Engagement to meet its milestones.

---

## 5. Collaboration

### Key Relationships

| Role | What You Need from Them | What They Need from You |
|------|------------------------|------------------------|
| EL(s) | Engineering leadership, delivery commitments, cooperation on process | Process facilitation, ceremony management, impediment identification, team health monitoring |
| Squad PM(s) | Product input for ceremonies, backlog context | Process facilitation (sprint planning, review), cadence management |
| EPM | Process expectations, cross-squad alignment context, Engagement-level impediment resolution | Cross-squad process observations, impediment escalation, team health signals |
| Engineers | Participation in ceremonies, impediment reporting | Effective facilitation, protected ceremony time, impediment follow-through |

### Tensions and Navigation

**Scrum Master ↔ EL.** You want process consistency and ceremony discipline. EL wants squad flexibility and may see ceremonies as overhead under delivery pressure.

- When delivery is behind, the answer is rarely "skip the retro." It is usually "use the retro to find and fix the bottleneck." Advocate for process as a delivery enabler, not bureaucratic compliance.
- Each EL has a different style. Adapt your facilitation to the EL's squad without abandoning the minimum coordination requirements EPM has set.
- If an EL consistently resists process, discuss directly first. If unresolved, escalate to EPM.

**Scrum Master ↔ Squad PM.** Product decisions (what to build, priority) and process decisions (how the sprint runs) overlap in practice, especially during sprint planning.

- You facilitate; Squad PM provides content. In sprint planning, the Squad PM presents the prioritised backlog; you manage the discussion, the timebox, and the commitment.
- If a product-process disagreement arises, discuss the trade-off and escalate to EPM if needed.

**Scrum Master across multiple squads.** Serving 2-3 squads with different ELs, different cadences, and different maturity levels requires context-switching and adaptation.

- Stagger ceremonies so you are fully present for each.
- Surface common patterns across squads to EPM — if two squads share the same impediment, raising it once at the EPM level is more effective than solving it twice at squad level.
- Be transparent with each squad about your multi-squad assignment.

**Scrum Master ↔ EPM.** EPM sets process expectations for the Engagement. You implement them at squad level. EPM may push for consistency; you may push for squad-specific adaptation.

- Understand the minimum coordination requirements and meet them consistently. Beyond the minimum, adapt to each squad's context.
- Use your cross-squad perspective as a contribution to EPM's integrated view. You see process health, team dynamics, and impediment patterns; EPM sees scope, timeline, and risk. Together, the view is more complete.

---

## 6. Management

### Dual-Axis Reporting

- **Execution axis:** Reports to **EPM**. EPM provides execution-axis input on your process effectiveness within the Engagement.
- **Functional/career axis:** **Program / Delivery leadership** evaluates your career growth across Engagements — process facilitation skill, multi-squad experience, cross-functional coordination.

### Performance Input

Request feedback from ELs on facilitation quality and from engineers on ceremony usefulness. EPM provides the overall process effectiveness assessment. Delivery leadership evaluates your trajectory across Engagements — the ability to facilitate increasingly complex multi-squad configurations and contribute to the integrated view.

---

## 7. Accountability

### Escalation: Dos and Don'ts

**Do:**

- Escalate impediments that squads cannot resolve internally to **EPM** — EPM coordinates across squads and has Engagement-level authority.
- Escalate team health concerns (workload imbalance, collaboration friction, morale) to **EL** first, then to **EPM** if EL cannot address.
- Escalate process disagreements with ELs to **EPM** — EPM sets process expectations for the Engagement ([governance.md](../../governance.md#escalation-model)).

**Don't:**

- Direct engineering work. You facilitate process; EL directs engineering.
- Skip escalation to fight impediments you cannot solve yourself. Your value is in identification and escalation, not in solving every blocker personally.
- Impose uniform process across squads without regard for context. A PL Squad maintaining a stable platform needs different ceremony intensity than a CP Squad integrating a new customer deployment.
- Escalate directly to EO without going through EPM first. EPM is your execution-axis reporting line and the first escalation point for process and coordination issues.

### When Commitments Are Missed

Scrum Master commitments are primarily **commitment-based** (per the [accountability framework](../../people-and-culture/accountability.md)): ceremony cadence, impediment escalation timeliness, and process consistency are binary — either ceremonies happen on schedule or they don't; either impediments are escalated promptly or they aren't. When process breaks down (ceremonies skipped, impediments unaddressed, cadence lost), the response is immediate correction and communication to EPM.

However, team health improvement and ceremony effectiveness are **outcome-based**: a squad that has never worked together will not have perfect retrospectives in sprint one. The Scrum Master's judgement on adapting process to context is evaluated by trajectory — are ceremonies getting more effective? Are impediments surfacing earlier? Is the team's delivery improving?

---

## 8. Objectives in Perspective

The Scrum Master's immediate objectives — ceremony facilitation, impediment removal, process consistency — seem procedural, but they are the mechanism that makes multi-squad delivery work.

Without effective process, squads accumulate hidden problems — commitments are made without capacity checks, blockers fester, retrospectives produce no action. These squad-level process failures compound into Engagement-level delivery failures that EPM and EO cannot diagnose because the signal was never surfaced.

The Scrum Master is the squad's process immune system. When process is healthy, the squad detects and responds to problems early. When process is weak, problems grow silently until they become crises.

The Scrum Master who keeps ceremonies running on time has done scheduling. The Scrum Master who makes ceremonies effective — sprint planning that produces realistic commitments, retrospectives that produce real improvement, standups that surface blockers before they stall work — has done facilitation that sustains delivery.
