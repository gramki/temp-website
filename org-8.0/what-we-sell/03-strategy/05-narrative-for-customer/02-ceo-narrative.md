# Chapter 05.02: The Bank You Are Becoming — A Narrative for the CEO

---

## The Question Behind the Question

Every bank board is asking its CEO the same question this year: *What is our AI strategy?*

It is the wrong question — or rather, it is a question that invites the wrong answer. The wrong answer is a list: a fraud triage agent, a document summarization tool, a chatbot, a copilot for the contact center. Every bank can produce this list. Most already have. And after two or three years of producing it, the honest CEOs notice what the list never contains: an application retired, a cost curve bent, a category of work that the bank no longer pays humans to coordinate, a product the bank could not sell before. AI was added. Nothing changed shape.

The better question — the one we believe boards will be asking within two years, and the one a CEO can get ahead of now — is this:

*What happens to our bank when AI is on both sides of the counter?*

---

## Both Sides of the Counter

On the bank's side of the counter, the story is familiar by now. AI can resolve disputes, process applications, reconcile accounts, investigate alerts, draft and file and route. Done well, it is the largest operating-leverage event in a generation. Done as a portfolio of pilots, it is a new layer of cost on top of all the old ones.

The other side of the counter is the part most strategies miss.

Your customers are acquiring AI too. Not from you — from their phone, their software vendors, their assistants. Within a few years, a meaningful share of the interactions your bank receives will not come from customers. They will come from customers' *agents*: software acting under delegated authority, comparing your savings rate against the market every night, filing the dispute the moment the charge posts, moving the idle balance the day a better yield appears, evaluating your products against competitors' with perfect patience and zero loyalty.

Consider what that does quietly, before it does anything loudly. A substantial share of retail banking profit has always rested on customer inertia — deposits that don't chase rates, balances that don't refinance, product holdings that are close enough, fees that go unexamined. None of that inertia survives contact with a competent agent. The erosion will not announce itself with a press release. It will show up as a deposit beta that won't come down, an attrition number that won't stay put, a back book that reprices itself one agent-assisted customer at a time.

Here is the uncomfortable symmetry: **the same technology that can transform how your bank works is simultaneously transforming what your customers demand and what your margins can withstand.** A strategy that addresses only the first half is half a strategy.

## The Bank You Could Not Afford to Be

But read the same change from the other direction, because the possibilities are larger than the threats.

Every banker knows what a private-banking client receives: someone who watches the relationship, notices the cash sitting idle against the card balance, calls before the life event becomes a financial mistake, makes the products fit the person. And every banker knows why the other 98% of customers don't receive it: the economics of human attention. That constraint — not ambition, not imagination — has always defined which bank you could afford to be.

The constraint is breaking. When advisory, product optimization, and relationship care can be delivered by governed agents at a fraction of the cost of a human relationship manager, the question changes from *which customers deserve attention* to *which attention you are willing to govern*. Concretely, that means a bank that can:

- **Give every customer the private-banking experience.** An agent that notices the customer paying 24% on a revolving balance while cash idles in a checking account, and starts the conversation your best banker would start — for the whole portfolio, not the top decile.
- **Be the part-time CFO for every small business you bank.** Receivables watched, cash flow forecasted, the credit line draw pre-approved before the shortfall the owner hasn't seen yet. The most underserved and most profitable expansion segment in banking, served at software economics.
- **Manufacture products for segments of one.** When the cost of serving and the cost of operating a product both collapse, the product catalog stops being a compromise across millions of customers and starts being a fit to each one.
- **Meet your customers' agents on your own terms.** The bank that treats inbound agents as a threat will be arbitraged by them. The bank that serves them deliberately — verified identity, clear delegation boundaries, products an agent can read and act on, every action auditable — turns the most dangerous channel of the next decade into a distribution advantage.

This is the growth half of the answer your board is actually asking for. Defense and growth are not two programs. They are one capability viewed from two sides.

## Why You Cannot Get There by Projects

If these possibilities could be bought as projects, every bank would already be buying them. They can't, for one structural reason that our [full thesis](../../02-the-thesis/README.md) develops in depth and that can be said briefly here:

**Your bank does not have a model of its own work.** It has systems, and processes wrapped around systems, and people who carry in their heads everything the systems and processes don't cover — which, in most domains, is a great deal. Nobody can say, for any given domain: here is all the work, here is who resolves each piece of it today, here is what governs it. And what you cannot see, you cannot transform. You cannot move work to AI when you don't know its full extent. You cannot retire an application when you don't know what invisible work leans on it. You cannot tell your board what fraction of a domain AI now handles when you have never known the denominator.

This is why every bank's AI portfolio produces successes and no transformation. Each project wires itself to the systems it needs, builds its own governance, freezes at its initial scope. The fiftieth agent costs what the first one did. Nothing compounds — and at enterprise scale, *whatever doesn't compound, decays*.

The alternative is to model the work itself — the commitments a domain fulfills, the disciplines that keep it healthy — and let that model, not the systems, be the stable foundation. Then who resolves the work (a person, an agent, a combination) becomes a governed, reversible decision rather than an engineering project. Then each domain modeled makes the next one cheaper. Then the question your regulator will ask — *what is your AI doing, and on whose authority?* — has a structural answer instead of fifty project-shaped ones.

And the same model answers the other side of the counter. The only bank that can safely expose itself to customers' agents is the bank whose products, permissions, and commitments are explicit enough for a machine to engage — under governance, with every decision recorded. The work model is not one prerequisite among several. It is the single prerequisite that defense, growth, efficiency, and regulatory confidence share.

## The Fork Every Bank Will Face

One more thing belongs in a CEO narrative, because it is a decision only a CEO can make.

As agentic intermediaries grow — consumer assistants, treasury platforms, embedded finance surfaces — every bank faces a fork. One path: fight to keep the customer relationship by being more proactive, more personal, and more useful than the intermediaries, with agents of your own. The other path: deliberately become the best manufacturing and balance-sheet utility behind other people's interfaces, and win on reliability, speed, and unit economics.

Both are legitimate strategies. What is not legitimate is drifting into the second while narrating the first — which is precisely what happens to banks that cannot evolve fast enough to compete on relationship. And here is the practical convergence: **both paths require the same foundation** — explicit products, modeled work, governed agents, machine-consumable interfaces, auditable decisions. You do not have to resolve the fork today. You have to build the bank that can take either path deliberately.

## What You Should Demand From Any Partner

We would rather arm you to evaluate everyone — including us — than ask to be taken on faith. Whoever you engage for this decade, demand five things:

1. **Make the work visible first.** If a partner's proposal starts with software and not with a map of your domain's work — all of it, including what lives in spreadsheets and experienced heads — they are selling you the fifty-first project.
2. **Leave the intelligence with you, in a form you can read.** Your operational knowledge should be expressed as explicit, examinable specifications — not buried in code or configurations that only the vendor's engineers understand. When you change vendors (and you will), the knowledge must move with you.
3. **Governance at machine speed, by construction.** Who decided, on what information, under whose authority, with what outcome — answerable across every agent in a domain, replayable for an examiner, not reconstructed per project after the fact.
4. **Treat agents as counterparties, not just employees.** Identity, delegated authority, and accountability for AI agents — yours *and your customers'* — modeled as first-class concerns. Very few providers can even articulate this requirement today. It will define the decade.
5. **Prove the compounding, in numbers.** The second domain must cost demonstrably less than the first, and the partner should commit to instrumenting and reporting that delta to your board. If each engagement is priced and scoped as if it were the first, nothing is compounding — except their revenue.

## How This Starts

Not with a transformation program. With one domain and ninety days.

Pick a domain that matters — servicing, disputes, an operations function. We model its work: every commitment, every discipline, who resolves what today, what depends on what. You receive something no bank we have met currently possesses: a complete, board-presentable map of one domain's work, with the erosion exposures quantified and the places where AI can responsibly absorb work made explicit — each move's outcome measurable against a denominator you can finally see.

If the map persuades you, it becomes the foundation of the first domain deployment — nothing is thrown away. If it doesn't, you own an artifact that makes every other vendor conversation sharper. Either way, you will have replaced the question *what is our AI strategy* with the one your board will eventually ask anyway — *what fraction of our bank's work have we made visible, governable, and ready to evolve* — and you will be the first bank in the room with an answer.

The banks that win this decade will not be the ones with the most AI projects. They will be the ones that made their work visible, kept their intelligence in their own hands, learned to govern machines at machine speed, and met their customers' agents with agents of their own — while their competitors were still counting pilots.

That is the bank you are becoming. We would like to build it with you.
