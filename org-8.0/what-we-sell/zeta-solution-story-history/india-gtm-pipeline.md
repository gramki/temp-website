## GTM Strategy Pillars

The Matrix India GTM strategy rests on four pillars:

| Pillar | Description |
|---|---|
| **Target Large Banks and FIs** | Engage top 7-10 FIs — Banks, NBFCs, large corporates with financial services ambition |
| **Large-Scale Transformation** | Focus on digital transformation and solutions; target opportunities with ARR potential of $5M+ |
| **Strategic Partnerships** | Collaborate with consulting firms (BCG, McKinsey) and major marketplaces (Google, Amazon) |
| **Build Strong Brand Presence** | Top-of-mind recall through thought leadership, events, and workshops |

**Current penetration claim**: Credit cards — 2 out of top 10 banks. UPI Stack — 2 out of top 3 banks.

## Pipeline Assessment — Book Framework Lens

### What the Pipeline Reveals

**1. Entry motion is predominantly RFP/product-led, not problem-led.**

The active deals at the second-largest private bank are RFP-driven competitive displacements. Zeta is responding to the bank's defined requirements, competing against established processors (Fiserv, TSYS, Pismo, Sarvatra, JusPay). This is the product-company GTM motion — not the solutions-company discovery motion the book describes.

The consultative/workshop-led entry in the Commercial Cards deal is closer to the solutions motion, but it remains within a single product category.

**2. Deal sizes are appropriate for enterprise solutions.**

$5M+ ARR targets per engagement are consistent with enterprise solutions economics (Chapter 9). These are not SaaS per-seat deals.

**3. The strategic partnership pillar is underdeveloped in the pipeline data.**

The strategy names BCG, McKinsey, Google, and Amazon as partnership targets, but the pipeline shows no deals sourced through these partnerships. The consulting-firm partnership model (Chapter 10 — partner ecosystems) is declared but not yet producing pipeline.

**4. The marketplace/corporate segment is a different business.**

TATA Pay, JIO Pay, AdaniOne, Google, Amazon — these are platform/checkout opportunities. They represent a B2B SaaS sale, not an enterprise solutions engagement. They may be valuable revenue, but they require a different GTM motion, different deal dynamics, and different success metrics. Mixing them in the same pipeline creates archetype confusion (the core problem the book identifies).

**5. The reference leverage from HDFC is implied but not explicitly structured.**

The India GTM presumably relies on HDFC as the reference engagement. But the pipeline data does not show how the reference is being deployed — in proposals, in prospect conversations, in industry events. A $43M ARR reference customer is a powerful asset, but only if the reference story is actively managed (Chapter 10).

### Summary Observation

The India GTM pipeline is **product-led with consultative elements** — closer to the B2B SaaS sales motion than the solutions discovery motion the book advocates. The $5M+ deal sizes are solutions-grade, but the entry motion (RFPs, feature comparisons, competitive shortlists) is product-grade. This is the gap the book's Chapter 6 identifies: enterprise deal sizes with SaaS sales reflexes.

The compound-problem narrative that Zeta articulates so well in its strategy documents (`problems.md`, `the-hub-way/`) does not yet appear in the sales pipeline. The bank prospects are buying UPI switches, credit card processors, and payment gateways — individual products, not compound solutions. Whether this is because the market is not ready for the compound narrative, or because the GTM team defaults to the product pitch, is a critical question for the full analysis.
