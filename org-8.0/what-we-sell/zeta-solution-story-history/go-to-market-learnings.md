# Go-to-Market Learnings — How Banks Buy Structural Change

*Observations from Zeta's engagement history about when and how banks are ready for structural action. These learnings were originally embedded in the thesis documents and extracted here as Zeta-specific strategic intelligence. The [thesis](../the-thesis/README.md) itself is maintained as neutral thought leadership; this document captures what Zeta has learned from operating inside the pattern.*

## Zeta's Direct Experience of the Problem

Across the banking domains Zeta works in, we see the [compound structural problem](../the-thesis/problems.md) in every bank we engage with. The specifics vary — different domains, different vendors, different debt — but the underlying pattern is the same. This is not an inference from industry reports. It is a firsthand observation from diagnostic engagement with banks, repeated across institutions of different sizes, geographies, and technology maturities.

This direct experience — seeing the same structural pattern from the inside, across multiple banks — is what gives Zeta's thesis its conviction. The thesis describes a problem Zeta has observed, not one it has hypothesized.

## The Market Opportunity Is Structural

The [thesis](../the-thesis/thesis.md) establishes four core concerns and argues that the approaches the industry has tried — ESBs, API gateways, cloud migrations, digital transformation programs — have not touched the root cause. Billions invested, root cause untouched.

From Zeta's perspective, this means: **the root problem persists and the market opportunity remains open.** No vendor has addressed the structural issue. The banks that have invested in transformation programs have improved their channels and moved workloads to cloud, but the compound problem — operational intelligence locked in bespoke code, architecture that punishes evolution — continues to worsen. The opportunity is not to compete with existing approaches but to address a problem that existing approaches have not reached.

## How Banks Buy — The Episodic Window

Banks cope. The compound problem grows slowly and the institutional immune system maintains the status quo. Banks don't lose customers easily. Product differentiation among incumbents has not significantly swayed market fortunes. Competitive responses — a peer launches a feature, a neobank enters the market — produce incremental reaction, not structural change. The bank ships the feature, adds more plumbing, and deepens the structural problem while creating the illusion of progress.

What actually forces structural action is different. These are the events that create real programs, real budgets, and real mandates:

**Regulatory mandates are non-optional.** When the regulator demands operational resilience reporting, AI explainability, real-time surveillance, or cross-border compliance, the bank must act regardless of how tangled its architecture is. The compliance cost is directly proportional to the structural mess.

**M&A forces architectural confrontation.** When a bank acquires another bank, it inherits a second compound problem overnight. When a vendor the bank depends on is acquired, or sunsets a product, or changes pricing, the bank is forced to migrate — and confronts the full weight of its integration dependence. These events trigger the largest technology programs in banking.

**Leadership change opens a window.** A new CTO, CDO, or COO arrives — often from a more modern environment — with a mandate and a 2–3 year window of political capital. They see the structural problems their predecessor had normalized. They want to leave a lasting mark on the institution. Without this person, the institutional immune system holds. With them, there is a brief window for structural action before the organization reverts.

**Contract renewal and vendor end-of-life create decision points.** Major vendor contracts come up for renewal every 5–7 years. Vendor products get sunsetted. These are predictable, recurring moments where the bank evaluates alternatives — and confronts the question: "If we switch, what breaks?"

**Cost pressure surfaces the hidden burden.** When margins compress and the board asks "why is our cost-to-income ratio not improving despite all this technology spend?", the enormous recurring cost of integration maintenance — systems integrator contracts, middleware licenses, headcount to keep integrations running — becomes visible.

**Major incidents create sudden political will.** A catastrophic outage traced to brittle integration — a batch job that failed silently, a compensating layer that corrupted data, a shadow architecture that nobody understood — creates willingness for structural change that did not exist the day before.

**AI changes the calculus.** Every previous technology wave was a channel innovation. AI is different: it is a work execution innovation that changes who resolves the work itself. Banks cannot deploy AI agents at enterprise scale without a model of the work they participate in. This is the first technology wave that requires a structural model rather than tolerating its absence — and every bank is under board-level pressure to show AI results.

## The Buying Moment Dynamic

Transformational needs are opportunistic, not seasonal. The compound problem grows slowly, but the buying moments are sudden and time-limited:

- A new leader has 18–24 months of political capital.
- A regulatory mandate has a compliance deadline.
- An M&A integration has a board-mandated timeline.
- A major incident creates willingness that fades within quarters.

**When the door opens, we must be ready with an answer** that works domain by domain, at each bank's own pace, without demanding enterprise-wide transformation. The answer must be credible at the scale of a domain — not a point solution, not a multi-year program, but a structural response that can start in one domain and expand as evidence accumulates.

This has direct implications for Zeta's go-to-market posture:

1. **The thesis must exist before the window opens.** The structural argument cannot be built during a sales cycle. It must be ready — intellectually complete, credible to a CIO, and expressible in the bank's own language — so that when a trigger event occurs, Zeta can engage with an answer, not a discovery process.

2. **Domain-by-domain entry is essential.** The buying moment is usually domain-specific: a payments migration, a card platform renewal, a regulatory mandate in compliance. The answer must work for that domain, at that bank's starting point, without requiring enterprise-wide commitment.

3. **The first domain must compound.** If the first domain engagement doesn't demonstrably produce the compounding effect — lower cost for the next change, richer model, more AI leverage — the expansion never happens. The first Hub must prove the trajectory, not just deliver a project.

4. **Each bank's configuration is unique.** Different domains, different gaps, different debt, different starting points. The approach must be adaptable to any starting point, not prescriptive about a single migration path.
