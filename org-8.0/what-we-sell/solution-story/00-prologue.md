# Prologue: The Playbook Confusion

---

## The Invisible Playbook

Every executive carries a playbook. It is rarely written down, seldom examined, and almost never questioned. It is the accumulated instinct of a career — the reflexes formed by watching what worked, what failed, and what was rewarded in the companies where that executive grew up.

An executive who built their career at a consumer technology company — a social network, a streaming service, a marketplace — carries a playbook that says: understand users, ship features, measure engagement, iterate fast, grow virally, optimize funnels. These instincts served them well. They are not wrong. They are the survival reflexes of a particular species of technology company.

An executive who grew up in B2B SaaS carries a different playbook: build a product that solves a workflow problem, acquire customers through content and inbound marketing, convert free trials to paid subscriptions, drive adoption through product-led growth, measure success in ARR, NRR, and the Rule of 40. These instincts are equally valid — for the environment in which they evolved.

The problem arises when these executives — talented, experienced, successful in their previous context — join an enterprise solutions business. They bring their playbooks with them. They apply consumer product instincts or SaaS growth heuristics to a business that operates by fundamentally different rules. And because their instincts feel universal ("good product thinking is good product thinking"), the misapplication is invisible. It does not feel like a mistake. It feels like common sense.

It is not. It is the most dangerous kind of error: the kind that looks like wisdom.

## The Four Archetypes

This book distinguishes four business archetypes. They are not a taxonomy of all technology companies — the real world is more complex. They are strategic species: business models that have evolved distinct survival strategies, organizational structures, economic models, and cultural reflexes.

### Consumer Product Companies

Companies that build products for individual users and scale through adoption. Think of Apple, Netflix, Spotify, Instagram. The product is the business. The user is the buyer. The relationship is direct, transactional, and high-volume. Success is measured by users, engagement, retention, and network effects. The organizational culture prizes design, speed, experimentation, and user empathy. The product manager is the central strategic role — the person who understands what users want and translates that into features that drive adoption.

**Defining characteristic**: The user and the buyer are the same person. The product sells itself through the experience it delivers.

### B2B SaaS Companies

Companies that build software products for business teams and departments, sold through a combination of self-serve adoption and sales-assisted conversion. Think of Slack, Zoom, HubSpot, Atlassian in its early days. The product solves a specific workflow problem. Adoption often starts with a team, grows within a department, and expands through the organization. Pricing is per-seat or per-usage, structured in standardized tiers. Success is measured by ARR, net revenue retention, CAC/LTV ratio, and the Rule of 40.

**Defining characteristic**: The product is standardized. Growth is driven by the product's ability to spread within and across organizations with minimal customization.

### Enterprise Solutions Companies

Companies that discover complex enterprise problems, build technology-based solutions to those problems, and deliver value through structured engagements that configure, extend, and integrate the solution into the customer's environment. Think of Palantir, ServiceNow (in its enterprise motion), Veeva Systems, Workday, and — in banking — companies like Temenos, FIS, and Thought Machine. The customer is not a user. It is an institution — with buying committees, procurement processes, regulatory requirements, integration landscapes, and political dynamics. Sales cycles are measured in quarters or years. Delivery is a discipline, not an afterthought. Revenue comes from a blend of platform licensing, implementation services, and ongoing operational support.

**Defining characteristic**: The problem is discovered before the product exists. Value is realized through delivery, not adoption.

### Systems Integrators and Consulting Firms

Companies that sell expertise and delivery capacity to enterprises. Think of Accenture, Deloitte, Infosys, Wipro. They employ armies of consultants and engineers who execute projects defined by client needs. Revenue is directly proportional to headcount. There is limited product IP — the value is in the people, the methodology, and the relationships. Each engagement starts largely from scratch. What compounds is the firm's reputation and talent base, not its technology.

**Defining characteristic**: Revenue scales with headcount. Nothing technological compounds from one engagement to the next.

## Why the Distinction Matters

These four archetypes are not points on a single spectrum. They are different species that share a genus. A lion and a dolphin are both mammals, but they do not hunt the same way, organize in the same social structures, or thrive in the same environments. Applying lion strategies to dolphin problems produces confusion, not capability.

The same is true for business archetypes. A consumer product manager who joins an enterprise solutions company and insists on "shipping features fast" is not wrong about the value of speed. They are wrong about what speed means in this context. In consumer products, speed means deploying to production and measuring results. In enterprise solutions, speed means accelerating the time between problem discovery and value realization for a specific customer — which may involve months of diagnostic work, deal shaping, architecture design, and phased delivery.

The instinct is right. The application is wrong. And because the instinct feels universal, the executive cannot see the mismatch. They see a company that "isn't moving fast enough" and prescribe the remedies that worked in their previous context — more features, shorter cycles, self-serve adoption, engagement metrics. Each remedy makes the problem worse.

This book exists to make the mismatch visible.

## The Cost of Misapplication

When consumer product or B2B SaaS playbooks are applied to an enterprise solutions business, the damage follows predictable patterns. These are not hypothetical — they are observable in enterprise solutions companies around the world, including companies in banking technology, healthcare IT, industrial platforms, and government technology.

### Product managers define features instead of shaping solutions

In consumer products, the product manager's job is to understand users and define features that improve their experience. The unit of work is the feature. The measure of success is adoption. This instinct, applied to enterprise solutions, produces product managers who maintain feature backlogs disconnected from enterprise problems. They build capabilities that no buying committee asked for, because they are solving for the user (who does not buy) rather than the enterprise (which does). The roadmap becomes a collection of features rather than a sequence of solutions to named enterprise problems.

**The correction**: In enterprise solutions, the product manager's job is to understand the enterprise problem — across multiple customers — and shape a solution that addresses it. The unit of work is the solution capability, not the feature. The measure of success is whether a customer's business outcome improved, not whether a user clicked a button.

### Go-to-market teams build funnels instead of consultative discovery motions

In B2B SaaS, the go-to-market machine runs on content marketing, lead scoring, free trials, and conversion optimization. The funnel is the organizing metaphor: attract visitors, convert to leads, qualify leads, close deals. This works when the buyer can evaluate the product through self-serve exploration — sign up, try it, see the value, purchase.

Enterprise solutions do not work this way. The buyer cannot evaluate a core banking modernization platform through a 14-day free trial. The evaluation involves months of diagnostic discovery, architectural assessment, vendor risk review, and reference checking. The "funnel" metaphor breaks down because there is no self-serve entry point. The motion is consultative: identify an enterprise with the problem, engage its leadership in a diagnostic conversation, demonstrate understanding of their specific situation, shape a solution, navigate procurement, and earn the right to deliver.

When GTM teams apply funnel thinking to enterprise solutions, they optimize for lead volume instead of relationship depth, produce content designed to generate downloads instead of demonstrate domain expertise, and measure success by pipeline metrics that have no correlation with deal outcomes.

**The correction**: Replace the funnel with the diagnostic. The enterprise solutions GTM motion starts with problem expertise, not product awareness. The salesperson's first job is to understand the customer's situation better than the customer does — not to demonstrate features.

### Pricing is modeled as subscription tiers instead of value-based deal architecture

SaaS pricing is elegant in its simplicity: Starter, Professional, Enterprise. Per seat per month. Annual contract for a discount. This simplicity is a feature of SaaS — it reduces friction, enables self-serve evaluation, and creates predictable revenue.

Enterprise solutions pricing is not simple, and attempts to make it simple destroy value. When a bank modernizes its card processing infrastructure, the value is not measured in seats. It is measured in cost avoided (replacing legacy maintenance contracts), revenue enabled (launching new card products faster), risk reduced (meeting regulatory requirements), and operational efficiency gained (automating manual processes). The value of that outcome to a large bank may be tens or hundreds of millions of dollars over the contract term.

Pricing this as "$X per seat per month" captures a fraction of the value created. Value-based deal architecture — pricing tied to the business outcome, structured in phases, with risk shared between vendor and customer — captures the value appropriately and aligns incentives. But it requires deal-by-deal design, not a pricing page on the website.

**The correction**: Enterprise solutions pricing is deal architecture, not product packaging. Each deal is designed around the specific value it creates for the specific customer. The pricing conversation is inseparable from the architecture conversation and the delivery conversation.

### Teams measure success with the wrong scoreboard

Perhaps the most insidious misapplication is in how success is measured. Consumer product teams measure daily active users, session duration, feature adoption, and NPS. B2B SaaS teams measure ARR growth, net revenue retention, CAC payback period, and logo churn. These are the right metrics for those archetypes.

When enterprise solutions teams adopt these metrics, they create a scoreboard that systematically undervalues what the business actually does well and overvalues what it should not be optimizing for. An enterprise solutions company that measures "feature velocity" (how many features shipped per quarter) is optimizing for output, not outcomes. One that measures "user engagement" is measuring a proxy for value that is 3-4 levels removed from the actual customer relationship.

The result is organizational frustration. Teams feel they are "not doing well" because the scoreboard says so — even when customers are renewing contracts, expanding scope, and providing references. The scoreboard is wrong, not the performance.

**The correction**: Enterprise solutions success metrics center on customer outcomes: value delivered (measurable business impact), customer health (renewal, expansion, reference willingness), deal economics (margin, attach rate, platform revenue mix), and delivery excellence (on-time, on-scope, knowledge transfer completion). These are not as clean or as frequent as daily active users. They are accurate.

### Teams resent not "building for users"

This is the subtlest and most human cost. Product engineers and designers who grew up in consumer or SaaS companies derive professional satisfaction from building things that users love — beautiful interfaces, delightful interactions, products that spread because they are wonderful to use. This is a legitimate and admirable professional identity.

In enterprise solutions, the end user of the system is often not the buyer, not the decision maker, and not the economic beneficiary. A bank operations team will use the platform daily, but the purchase was made by a CIO, approved by a risk committee, and justified by a business case written for the CFO. The product must work well for users — that is table stakes — but the purchase decision, the renewal decision, and the expansion decision are made at levels that never log in.

Engineers and designers in enterprise solutions companies sometimes feel that their craft is undervalued — that the company doesn't care about "the user." This is a misdiagnosis. The company cares about the user. It also cares about the buyer, the evaluator, the risk officer, and the procurement committee — because without them, there is no user. The professional challenge in enterprise solutions is building something that satisfies a matrix of stakeholders, not optimizing for one.

**The correction**: Redefine the craft. In enterprise solutions, great product work means building systems that are excellent for users, compelling for buyers, defensible for risk committees, and transformative for the enterprise. This is harder than building for users alone. It should be celebrated as the more difficult achievement, not mourned as a compromise.

## How to Read This Book

This book follows the natural lifecycle of an enterprise solutions business, from discovering the opportunity to achieving strategic positioning. Twelve chapters, organized in four parts:

**Part I: Finding the Problem** (Chapters 1–4) covers how enterprise problems are identified, how opportunities are discovered, how markets are sized, and where enterprise money actually sits.

**Part II: Designing the Business** (Chapters 5–7) covers the different types of solutions businesses, how enterprises purchase solutions, and how large deals are designed.

**Part III: Delivering Value** (Chapter 8) covers how enterprise solutions are actually delivered and implemented — the discipline that separates solutions businesses from product companies.

**Part IV: Building the Machine** (Chapters 9–12) covers the economics of solutions businesses, how to scale, the path from solutions to platforms, and long-term strategic positioning.

### The Four-Column Contrast

Every chapter includes a contrast table showing how the chapter's core question is answered differently across the four business archetypes: Consumer Product, B2B SaaS, Enterprise Solutions, and SI/Consulting. This is the book's recurring pedagogical device. It is designed to make the invisible playbook visible — to show executives exactly where their instincts apply and where they do not.

### Deep and Concise Chapters

Six chapters receive deep treatment: Opportunity Discovery (Ch. 2), Enterprise Buying Dynamics (Ch. 6), Deal Shaping (Ch. 7), Delivery Models (Ch. 8), Economics (Ch. 9), and Platformization (Ch. 11). These are the topics where playbook confusion causes the most damage and where detailed treatment creates the most value.

Six chapters are treated more concisely: Problem Archetypes (Ch. 1), Opportunity Sizing (Ch. 3), Enterprise Value Pools (Ch. 4), Solutions Business Archetypes (Ch. 5), Scaling (Ch. 10), and Strategic Positioning (Ch. 12). Each concise chapter notes its scope explicitly — not because these topics lack importance, but because they can be introduced structurally without the depth that the other six require.

### Industry Context

The frameworks in this book are industry-agnostic. The examples draw primarily from banking technology — an industry where the solutions business model is particularly clear because the problems are complex, the buyers are sophisticated, the regulatory environment is demanding, and the delivery stakes are high. Where relevant, examples from healthcare IT, industrial platforms, and government technology are included to demonstrate that the patterns are structural, not accidental to any single industry.

### Named References

Two intellectual debts should be acknowledged at the outset:

**Clayton Christensen's "jobs to be done" framework** applies to enterprise solutions, but differently than to consumer products. In consumer products, the "job" is something an individual needs to accomplish — and the product is hired to do it. In enterprise solutions, the "job" is an institutional transformation — and the solution is hired to deliver it. The hiring process involves committees, not individuals. The evaluation is measured in business outcomes, not user satisfaction. The "interview" takes months, not minutes.

**Geoffrey Moore's "Crossing the Chasm"** is the canonical text on technology adoption, but it was written for products — specifically, for products that must cross from early adopters to mainstream markets. Enterprise solutions face a different adoption challenge: not crossing from visionaries to pragmatists, but earning the trust of risk-averse institutions that evaluate vendors through procurement processes, regulatory lenses, and multi-year track records. The chasm is not between market segments. It is between "impressive demo" and "trusted production partner."
