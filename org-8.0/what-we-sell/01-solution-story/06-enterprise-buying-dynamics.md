# Chapter 06: Enterprise Buying Dynamics

---

**Key Question**: *How do enterprises actually purchase solutions?*

This is the second most misunderstood area in enterprise solutions businesses — after problem discovery. Consumer and SaaS veterans expect a familiar arc: users adopt, usage proves value, purchase follows. The product demonstrates its worth through experience, and the buying decision is a natural consequence of the product being good.

Enterprise purchasing is fundamentally different. The people who use the product do not buy it. The people who buy it do not use it. The people who evaluate it apply criteria that have nothing to do with the product's quality. The people who can veto it may never attend a demo. And the timeline is governed not by enthusiasm but by budget cycles, procurement calendars, and regulatory review schedules that were set before anyone knew your company existed.

Understanding enterprise buying dynamics is not optional knowledge for a solutions business. It is the operating system on which everything else runs. A team that builds a brilliant solution but does not understand how enterprises buy will watch that solution sit on a shelf — admired but unpurchased — while less capable competitors who understand the buying machine will win the contracts.

---

## Buying Committees, Not Buyers

The most persistent myth in enterprise sales is the myth of "the decision maker." Teams ask: *Who is the decision maker? Get us in front of the decision maker.* The question reveals a consumer instinct — the assumption that one person evaluates, decides, and purchases.

In enterprise purchasing, there is no single decision maker. There is a buying committee — a shifting, informal, and often unacknowledged group of 6–10 stakeholders who collectively determine whether a purchase proceeds. Each member evaluates the solution through a different lens, applies different criteria, and possesses different forms of veto power.

The typical enterprise buying committee includes:

**The Executive Sponsor** — a C-level or SVP who owns the strategic initiative the solution supports. This person cares about business outcomes: revenue growth, cost reduction, competitive positioning, or regulatory compliance. They rarely evaluate the technology directly. They ask: *Will this solve my problem? Can I stake my reputation on it? Will it deliver results on a timeline that matters?*

**The Technology Leadership** — CIO, CTO, or VP of Engineering — who evaluates architectural fit, integration complexity, operational burden, and technical risk. Their concerns are control and capability: *Does this fit our technology strategy? Can we operate it? Does it introduce dependencies we cannot manage? Will it work with our existing systems?*

**The Business Line Leaders** — heads of the business units that will be affected by the solution. They evaluate through the lens of outcomes and disruption: *Will this make my team more effective? How much change does it require? What is the transition risk? Will it slow us down before it speeds us up?*

**The Risk and Compliance Function** — Chief Risk Officer, information security, regulatory compliance teams. They evaluate through the lens of evidence and assurance: *Does this vendor meet our security requirements? How does this affect our regulatory posture? What happens if the vendor fails? Where does the data reside?*

**The Procurement Organization** — sourcing, vendor management, contract negotiation. They evaluate through the lens of process and precedent: *Is this vendor on our approved list? Have we followed the required evaluation process? Are the commercial terms within policy? What are the exit provisions?*

**The Operations Teams** — the people who will actually operate, maintain, and support the solution in production. They ask practical questions: *Can we run this? Do we have the skills? What does the support model look like? How does this affect our on-call burden?*

**The End Users' Management** — middle managers whose teams will interact with the solution daily. They care about adoption difficulty, training requirements, and workflow disruption.

Each of these stakeholders has a different evaluation framework, a different definition of "good," and — critically — a different form of power. The executive sponsor can champion a purchase but cannot override a risk committee veto. The technology leadership can approve architectural fit but cannot commit business budget. Procurement can delay or block a deal indefinitely through process requirements but cannot initiate one.

The practical implication is stark: winning enthusiastic support from one stakeholder while neglecting another does not advance the deal — it creates the illusion of progress while the neglected stakeholder quietly prepares a veto.

## The Three Conversations

Every enterprise purchase involves three parallel conversations, each with a different audience, a different language, and a different definition of success. Solutions businesses that recognize and deliberately conduct all three conversations close deals. Those that conduct only one or two watch deals stall, derail, or die in silence.

### Conversation 1: Technology Leadership — Control and Capability

The conversation with technology leadership is about architecture, integration, operations, and risk. The CIO or CTO is asking: *Can I control this? Will it work within my estate? What does it demand of my team?*

This conversation requires deep technical credibility. Technology leaders are evaluating not just whether the solution works, but whether it fits — into their architecture principles, their integration landscape, their operational model, their security posture, and their talent base. A solution that is technically excellent but architecturally incompatible is not a solution; it is a liability.

What this audience cares about: architectural diagrams, integration patterns, deployment models, security certifications, operational runbooks, technology roadmap alignment. They want to see evidence that the vendor understands enterprise-grade operations — not startup-grade innovation.

### Conversation 2: Business Leadership — Outcomes and Speed

The conversation with business leadership is about results. The business line head or P&L owner is asking: *What problem does this solve for me? How fast will I see results? What is the business case?*

This conversation requires domain expertise. Business leaders do not evaluate technology — they evaluate outcomes. They want to understand how similar institutions have used this solution to achieve measurable results: cost reductions quantified, revenue growth demonstrated, compliance achieved, operational efficiency gained. They want case studies, reference customers, and a credible narrative about time-to-value.

What this audience cares about: business cases with quantified outcomes, reference customers in comparable institutions, implementation timelines with realistic milestones, proof that the vendor understands their specific business context.

### Conversation 3: Risk, Procurement, and Governance — Evidence and Assurance

The conversation with the risk and procurement apparatus is about proof. This audience is asking: *Can we trust this vendor? Have we followed our process? What happens if this goes wrong?*

This is the conversation that SaaS-trained teams most frequently neglect or resent. It involves vendor risk assessments, security questionnaires, compliance certifications, financial stability reviews, business continuity plans, data residency documentation, contractual protections, and reference checks. It is not glamorous. It is not about the product. It is about whether the institution can safely enter into a multi-year dependency on this vendor.

What this audience cares about: SOC 2 reports, penetration test results, regulatory compliance documentation, financial stability evidence, insurance coverage, contractual SLAs with teeth, escalation procedures, exit provisions, and references from institutions of comparable regulatory complexity.

### How the Three Conversations Interact

These conversations proceed in parallel but are not independent. The technology leader may endorse the solution, but if the risk team flags unresolved security findings, the deal stops. The business leader may champion the business case, but if procurement cannot negotiate acceptable terms within the budget cycle, the deal slips a year. The risk team may clear the vendor, but if the technology leadership finds architectural incompatibility, the solution is disqualified.

Winning one conversation while losing another kills the deal. The solutions business must staff and manage all three conversations simultaneously — with different materials, different expertise, and different cadences. The salesperson who can present a compelling demo but cannot navigate a vendor risk assessment is equipped for SaaS, not for enterprise.

## Political Dynamics

Enterprise purchases are political acts. They allocate budget, create dependencies, establish vendor relationships, and — most importantly — they put individual executives' reputations at stake. The executive who sponsors a failed implementation carries that failure for years. The one who sponsors a successful transformation builds career capital. Every enterprise purchase is, at its core, a bet placed by a human being with a career to protect.

This reality introduces political dynamics that product-focused teams often find uncomfortable or illegible.

### Champions vs. Mobilizers

The most important distinction in enterprise political dynamics comes from CEB/Gartner research, detailed in Brent Adamson, Matthew Dixon, Pat Spenner, and Nick Toman's *The Challenger Customer* (2015). The research identifies a critical difference between two types of internal supporters:

**Champions** (or "Talkers") are enthusiastic advocates for the solution. They love the product, attend every demo, provide generous feedback, and assure the sales team that "this is going to happen." They are visible, engaged, and encouraging. They are also, frequently, powerless. Champions tend to be individuals who respond to the quality of the product itself — they see its value, they want it to succeed, and they communicate that enthusiasm freely. But they often lack the organizational authority, political capital, or cross-functional relationships needed to drive a purchase through the buying committee.

**Mobilizers** are different. They may be less visibly enthusiastic, but they possess something champions lack: the ability to build consensus across organizational silos, navigate internal politics, and drive collective action. Mobilizers are not motivated by the product's features — they are motivated by the business outcome the solution enables. They will challenge the vendor, push back on assumptions, demand evidence, and ask hard questions. They do this not because they are hostile, but because they are building the internal case that will survive scrutiny from every member of the buying committee.

### Identifying Mobilizers vs. Talkers

The CEB research found that the stakeholder who is most enthusiastic in early meetings is frequently the least effective at driving the purchase to completion. Identifying mobilizers requires looking for different signals:

- **Talkers** say: "This is great. I love it. When can we start?" They focus on features and capabilities.
- **Mobilizers** say: "This could work, but I need to understand how it addresses [specific concern of another stakeholder]. What evidence can you give me for [specific objection that will arise in committee]?" They focus on building a cross-functional case.

Talkers describe personal enthusiasm. Mobilizers describe organizational readiness. Talkers agree easily. Mobilizers challenge constructively. Talkers attend demos. Mobilizers attend steering committees.

The practical implication is counterintuitive: the person who seems most supportive may be the least strategically important. Solutions businesses must learn to look past enthusiasm to identify the individuals who can actually move the organization — and then equip those mobilizers with the evidence, business cases, and reference materials they need to win the internal battle that no vendor will ever see.

## Budget Cycles and Timing

Enterprise budgets are not flexible pools of capital waiting to be allocated to good ideas. They are planned 6–18 months in advance through structured annual planning processes that involve finance, business line leadership, technology leadership, and executive committees.

A brilliant solution that arrives mid-cycle without a budget allocation will wait. It does not matter how compelling the demo is, how enthusiastic the champion is, or how acute the problem is. If the money was allocated to other priorities in last year's planning process, there is no budget — and without budget, there is no purchase.

### The Annual Planning Cycle

Most large enterprises follow a planning cycle that looks roughly like this:

**Months 1–3 (Q1 of the fiscal year)**: Strategy setting. Executive leadership defines strategic priorities for the coming year. Business lines identify their major initiatives. Technology leadership aligns capacity against strategic themes.

**Months 4–6 (Q2)**: Budget formulation. Business cases are developed for major initiatives. Technology investments are sized and prioritized. Portfolio trade-offs are made. Vendor evaluations for known needs may begin.

**Months 7–9 (Q3)**: Budget finalization and approval. Finance consolidates requests, applies constraints, and submits the budget for board or executive committee approval. By the end of this period, the money is allocated.

**Months 10–12 (Q4)**: Execution preparation. Approved initiatives are staffed, vendors are engaged (or RFPs issued), and delivery planning begins.

### Aligning with the Cycle

Solutions businesses must map their engagement cadence to this planning cycle — not to their own product release cycles. The critical insight is that the window for influencing budget allocation is narrow and early. By the time budgets are finalized, it is too late to introduce a new initiative.

This means:

- The relationship-building and diagnostic conversations that create demand should begin 12–18 months before the expected contract start.
- The vendor must be present during the strategy-setting and budget-formulation phases — not arriving with a solution after the money has already been committed elsewhere.
- Solutions businesses that time their outreach to their own product launch dates rather than to the customer's budget cycle will consistently arrive too late.

The SaaS instinct — "ship it, let them try it, they'll buy it when they see the value" — collapses against this reality. The enterprise cannot buy it when it sees the value if the value becomes visible in month 8 and the budget was locked in month 6.

## Procurement Processes

Enterprise procurement is not a transaction. It is an institutional process designed to protect the organization from vendor risk, ensure competitive pricing, maintain regulatory compliance, and create contractual protections that will endure for years. The process typically includes:

**Request for Information (RFI)**: A broad market scan to identify potential vendors and understand the solution landscape. Often used to shortlist vendors for deeper evaluation.

**Request for Proposal (RFP)**: A structured solicitation that defines requirements, evaluation criteria, and submission formats. Responses are evaluated by a cross-functional team against weighted criteria. RFPs in enterprise environments can run 50–200 pages and require weeks of effort to complete.

**Vendor Risk Assessment**: A parallel process conducted by information security, compliance, and vendor management teams. This includes security questionnaires (often hundreds of questions), penetration test reviews, SOC 2 report analysis, financial stability assessment, and business continuity plan evaluation.

**Proof of Concept (POC)**: A structured evaluation in which the shortlisted vendor demonstrates the solution against specific use cases in the customer's environment. Enterprise POCs are not free trials — they are planned, resourced, and evaluated against predefined success criteria.

**Reference Checks**: Direct conversations with the vendor's existing customers, typically institutions of comparable size and complexity. Reference checks in enterprise purchasing are substantive — not perfunctory calls, but detailed discussions about implementation experience, vendor responsiveness, production stability, and realized value.

**Contract Negotiation**: Enterprise contracts are negotiated, not signed. Master Service Agreements, Statements of Work, Service Level Agreements, data processing agreements, liability provisions, termination clauses, intellectual property assignments, and escrow arrangements are all individually negotiated. This process alone can take 2–4 months.

The total elapsed time from initial engagement to signed contract in an enterprise deal is typically 6–18 months. For large-scale platform purchases, 12–24 months is common.

The SaaS instinct — "let them try it and they'll love it" — fails here because procurement requires months of due diligence before a trial can begin. The free trial that a SaaS company offers with a credit card is, in enterprise terms, a structured POC that requires procurement approval, security review, data handling agreements, and executive sponsorship. By the time the enterprise is ready to "try it," they have already invested six months of evaluation effort.

## The "Unhappy Teams" Problem

In enterprise solutions companies that employ product managers, engineers, and designers trained in consumer or SaaS environments, a predictable tension emerges. These professionals feel frustrated. They express it in recognizable ways:

*"We're not building for users."*
*"Nobody cares about the product experience."*
*"We're just doing whatever the customer asks."*
*"Sales drives the roadmap, not product."*

This frustration is legitimate. It reflects a genuine professional instinct — the instinct to build things that delight users, to see people enjoy what you have created, to measure your impact through adoption and engagement. That instinct is not wrong. It is the instinct of a craftsperson who cares about their work.

But it is an instinct calibrated to the wrong context.

In enterprise solutions, the "user" in the consumer sense — the individual who interacts with the product daily — is three to four levels removed from the buyer. A bank's operations team uses the platform every day. They have opinions about the interface, the workflow, the performance. Those opinions matter — a product that frustrates its users will erode the customer relationship over time. User quality is table stakes.

But the purchase decision — the decision to spend millions of dollars over multiple years — is made by executives who will never log in. They evaluate the solution based on business outcomes, vendor credibility, reference customer testimony, regulatory compliance posture, and strategic alignment. They do not evaluate the button placement on screen three of the workflow.

The measurement of success in an enterprise solutions business must reflect this reality. The metrics that matter are enterprise outcomes: cost reduction achieved, compliance goals met, transformation milestones delivered, operational efficiency gained. These are not as emotionally satisfying as watching a daily active user count climb. They are, however, the metrics that determine whether the business survives.

The empathetic response to unhappy teams is not to dismiss their instinct but to reframe the challenge. Building a product that satisfies a buying committee of ten stakeholders with competing concerns while simultaneously working well for end users is *harder* than building for users alone. It is a more complex design problem, a more demanding engineering challenge, and a more sophisticated product management discipline. It should be recognized as the greater achievement — not mourned as a lesser one.

The direct response is also necessary: in an enterprise solutions business, the team's job is to build something that an institution will buy, deploy, operate, and renew. If the institution does not buy, there are no users. User delight without enterprise purchase is a hobby, not a business. The sequence is non-negotiable: earn the institution's trust, win the purchase, deliver the solution, enable the users. Not the other way around.

## The Misapplication

Enterprise solutions companies staffed with consumer and SaaS veterans exhibit predictable anti-patterns in how they approach enterprise buying dynamics. Each represents a legitimate instinct applied to the wrong context.

### Demoing features to users instead of demonstrating value to buyers

The product team builds an impressive demo showing the user interface, the workflow, the real-time analytics dashboard. They present it to the customer's operations team, who respond enthusiastically. The sales team reports strong interest. But the demo never reached the CIO, the risk officer, or the CFO — the people who actually control the purchase. The operations team's enthusiasm creates the illusion of momentum. The deal stalls because no one demonstrated how the solution reduces operational cost by 30%, meets the bank's data residency requirements, or integrates with the existing core banking platform.

### Expecting the product to "sell itself" through quality

"If we build a great product, customers will buy it." This is the foundational belief of consumer product culture, and it is true — for consumers. Enterprise purchasing does not work by product quality alone. An enterprise will choose a less technically elegant solution from a vendor with stronger references, deeper domain expertise, a proven implementation methodology, and a more credible long-term commitment to the market. Product quality is necessary but radically insufficient.

### Treating procurement as an obstacle instead of a conversation to win

Product-trained teams experience procurement as bureaucratic friction — an obstacle between their brilliant solution and the customer who wants it. They resent the security questionnaires, the vendor risk assessments, the contract negotiations. They view procurement as something to endure, not something to excel at. This attitude is visible to procurement teams and it damages the vendor's credibility. The enterprise solutions mindset treats procurement as a conversation to win — an opportunity to demonstrate operational maturity, security rigor, contractual reasonableness, and institutional reliability.

### Ignoring the risk/governance conversation

"We're a technology company, not a compliance company." This statement, heard frequently in product-led organizations, reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of enterprise sales. In regulated industries — banking, healthcare, government, insurance — the risk and governance conversation is not a side discussion. It is often the primary gating factor. A vendor that cannot demonstrate regulatory compliance, data security, operational resilience, and contractual protections will not reach the shortlist, regardless of how good the technology is. The risk conversation is not an obstacle to the technology conversation. It is a prerequisite.

### Confusing a champion's enthusiasm with organizational commitment

The champion is excited. They attend every meeting, provide early feedback, facilitate introductions, and tell the sales team that "we're definitely going to do this." The sales team forecasts the deal as highly probable. Six months later, the deal is dead — killed by a risk committee finding, a budget reallocation, or a change in executive sponsorship. The champion's enthusiasm was real but had no bearing on the organization's readiness to buy. The team confused individual enthusiasm with institutional commitment — a category error that produces chronically inaccurate forecasts and wasted pursuit investment.

## Case Studies

### Salesforce's Enterprise Evolution

Salesforce launched in 1999 as the antithesis of enterprise buying dynamics. Marc Benioff's founding insight — documented in his 2009 memoir *Behind the Cloud* — was that enterprise software purchasing was broken. CRM systems from Siebel and SAP required million-dollar licenses, year-long implementations, and executive committee approvals. Salesforce's disruption was to bypass all of that: sign up online, pay per user per month, start using it immediately. The product sold itself to salespeople. Users adopted, usage proved value, purchase followed.

This worked spectacularly — for small and mid-market deals. But as Salesforce pursued larger enterprise accounts, the model hit the very dynamics it had been designed to circumvent. Large enterprises could not adopt Salesforce through a credit card signup. They required vendor risk assessments, data residency guarantees, integration with existing ERP and data warehouse systems, contractual SLAs, and executive-level business case justification. Salesforce's annual reports from the 2010s document the deliberate construction of an enterprise sales organization: industry-specific solutions, professional services capabilities, strategic account teams, and C-level relationship management. By the time Salesforce acquired MuleSoft (2018) and Tableau (2019), it had become the kind of enterprise solutions company it once defined itself against — not because it abandoned its product-led instinct, but because enterprise buying dynamics demanded it.

The lesson is not that product-led growth fails. It is that product-led growth addresses one segment of the market. Accessing the enterprise segment requires understanding and respecting enterprise buying dynamics — buying committees, three conversations, budget cycles, procurement processes — regardless of how good the product is.

### Epic Systems in Healthcare

Epic Systems, the dominant electronic health record (EHR) vendor in the United States, has never had a self-serve motion. There is no free trial, no product-led growth, no sign-up page. Every Epic sale is a multi-year, committee-driven enterprise decision involving hospital system CEOs, CMOs, CIOs, CFOs, clinical leadership, and board members. The evaluation cycle typically spans 12–24 months. The implementation spans 2–5 years.

Epic's sales methodology, documented extensively in healthcare IT literature including studies from KLAS Research and analyses in journals such as *Health Affairs*, relies on three pillars that map directly to enterprise buying dynamics. First, reference customers: Epic maintains an extraordinary network of reference customers who host site visits for prospective buyers. Hospital system executives fly to existing Epic customers to observe the system in production, speak with clinical staff, and evaluate the transformation outcomes. Second, executive relationships: Epic's founder, Judy Faulkner, and the senior team maintain direct relationships with health system executives, engaging at the strategic level rather than the product level. Third, transformation credibility: Epic does not sell software. It sells the institutional transformation from fragmented clinical systems to an integrated digital health record — and it demonstrates the credibility to deliver that transformation through reference outcomes and implementation track record.

Epic's product is excellent — consistently rated highest in clinical usability by KLAS. But the product does not sell itself. The sale is won through enterprise buying dynamics: buying committee navigation, three parallel conversations (clinical, technical, financial), budget cycle alignment, and procurement process mastery.

## In Banking

Banking technology purchases are the purest expression of enterprise buying dynamics, intensified by regulatory scrutiny, systemic risk consciousness, and institutional conservatism that is both culturally ingrained and regulatorily mandated.

### The Cast of Decision Makers

A banking technology purchase of any significance involves, at minimum:

- **The CIO/CTO**, evaluating technology architecture, integration with the bank's existing estate, and the operational burden of the solution.
- **The business line leader** (head of cards, head of retail banking, head of payments), evaluating business outcomes, time-to-market for new products, and competitive positioning.
- **The Chief Risk Officer** and their team, evaluating vendor risk, data security, operational resilience, regulatory compliance, and concentration risk.
- **Information Security/ISG**, conducting technical security assessments, penetration testing reviews, and data handling evaluations.
- **Procurement/Vendor Management**, ensuring competitive evaluation, contractual protections, commercial reasonableness, and compliance with the bank's sourcing policies.
- **The CFO or Finance**, evaluating the business case, total cost of ownership, and alignment with the bank's capital allocation priorities.
- **Regulatory Affairs**, assessing whether the solution introduces regulatory risk or supports regulatory compliance.

In large banks, add the enterprise architecture board, the data governance committee, and the operational resilience team. The buying committee for a significant banking technology purchase can exceed fifteen stakeholders.

### The 12–18 Month Evaluation Cycle

A bank that "loves your demo" in January may not sign a contract until the following year. The intervening months are consumed by a structured evaluation that proceeds through recognizable phases:

**Phase 1 — Discovery and Shortlisting (2–3 months)**: The bank identifies the need, issues an RFI to the market, evaluates responses, and creates a shortlist of 3–5 vendors.

**Phase 2 — Structured Evaluation (3–4 months)**: Shortlisted vendors complete detailed RFPs, present to evaluation committees, and participate in technical deep-dives. Vendor risk assessments begin in parallel.

**Phase 3 — Proof of Concept (2–3 months)**: One or two finalists conduct a structured POC against predefined criteria, typically in a sandboxed environment with the bank's data (anonymized) and integration requirements.

**Phase 4 — Reference Checks and Due Diligence (1–2 months)**: The bank speaks with the vendor's existing banking customers — typically institutions of comparable size, regulatory jurisdiction, and complexity. Reference checks in banking are substantive, probing implementation experience, production stability, regulatory findings, and vendor responsiveness.

**Phase 5 — Commercial Negotiation and Contract (2–4 months)**: Legal and procurement negotiate the full contractual apparatus: MSA, SOW, SLA, DPA, escrow, liability, termination, transition assistance. In banking, contracts include regulatory audit rights, data sovereignty provisions, and operational resilience requirements that add layers of complexity.

**Phase 6 — Regulatory and Board Approval (1–2 months)**: For material vendor relationships, the bank's board or executive committee must approve. In some jurisdictions, regulatory notification or approval may be required.

### Why "Loved the Demo" Takes 18 Months

Each phase of this cycle is necessary. Banks are regulated institutions that bear fiduciary responsibility to depositors, shareholders, and the financial system. A technology vendor failure in a bank is not a product outage — it is a systemic risk event that can trigger regulatory intervention. The evaluation process reflects this reality.

The implication for solutions businesses is that patience, process mastery, and sustained engagement are not optional — they are the cost of entry. A solutions business that cannot maintain engagement, provide consistent information, respond to hundreds of diligence questions, produce reference customers on request, and staff a deal team for 12–18 months is not equipped for banking.

### The Decisive Role of Reference Customers

In banking, reference customers from comparable institutions are frequently the single most influential factor in the final decision. A bank evaluating a new card processing platform will want to speak with another bank of similar size, similar regulatory jurisdiction, and similar product complexity that has already deployed the solution in production. No amount of demo excellence, technical architecture elegance, or commercial competitiveness substitutes for a reference customer who says: "We deployed it, it works, the vendor delivered what they promised, and the regulators are satisfied."

Building a portfolio of referenceable banking customers is the highest-leverage investment a banking technology solutions business can make. Each successful implementation creates not just revenue but sales infrastructure — a proof point that makes the next deal easier to win.

---

*Enterprise buying dynamics are not obstacles to be overcome. They are the operating environment in which enterprise solutions businesses exist. A team that resents the buying committee, ignores the three conversations, misreads the political dynamics, misses the budget cycle, and treats procurement as friction is not a team that will win enterprise deals — regardless of how good the product is. The buying machine is the terrain. The solutions business must learn to navigate it, not wish it away.*
