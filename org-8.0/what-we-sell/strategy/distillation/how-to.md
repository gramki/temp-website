# RTP-RTW

## In this lens

- **Right to Play**: Do we have a legitimate basis to enter and participate in this market?  
  What evidence shows this is a meaningful, viable, strategically relevant market for us to enter?
- **Right to Win**: Even if we can enter, do we have a credible path to outperform alternatives and build durable advantage?  
  What evidence shows we can create superior value and sustain an advantaged position against competition?

---

### 1) Right to Play — key questions

These questions test whether the opportunity is even worth pursuing and whether you are a plausible participant.

#### Market attractiveness

- Is the problem real, painful, and important enough?
- How large is the opportunity today?
- How fast is it growing?
- Is it strategic for us, or merely adjacent?
- Is the timing right now?

#### Customer need and willingness

- Who exactly is the customer and buyer?
- What job are they trying to get done?
- How acute is the pain?
- Are they already spending money to solve it?
- How much are they willing to pay?
- Is the budget obvious and accessible?

#### Fit with who we are

- Is this aligned with our mission, brand, and long-term direction?
- Does it fit our existing customer base, channels, and relationships?
- Can we leverage existing capabilities, assets, or distribution?
- Does entering this space create meaningful synergies with our current business?

#### Feasibility of entry

- Can we build, buy, partner, or otherwise assemble the required offering?
- Do we understand the product, technology, operating, and regulatory requirements?
- Can we credibly get to market in a reasonable timeframe?
- Is the investment required acceptable relative to our resources?
- Are there major barriers to entry that make participation unrealistic?

#### Economic viability

- Is there a plausible business model here?
- Are the unit economics potentially sound?
- Can we acquire customers at a reasonable cost?
- Is there enough margin pool in the category?
- Is the payback period acceptable?

#### Risk and constraints

- Are there regulatory, legal, compliance, or trust hurdles?
- Are there ecosystem dependencies that could block us?
- Could this distract from more important priorities?
- What must be true for this to work at all?

A good summary question for Right to Play is:

> **Do we have a justified reason to be in this market at all?**

---

### 2) Right to Win — key questions

These questions test whether you can create superior value and defend it.

#### Differentiated value proposition

- Why would customers choose us over incumbents or alternatives?
- What would we do meaningfully better, faster, cheaper, safer, or simpler?
- Is our value proposition clear and compelling?
- Does our offer solve the problem in a way others do not?

#### Competitive advantage

- What unique assets do we bring?
- Do we have technology, data, IP, brand, trust, distribution, relationships, or cost advantages?
- Are these advantages real or just internal optimism?
- Are they durable or easily copied?

#### Ability to execute better

- Do we have the talent, leadership, and operating model to win here?
- Can we deliver consistently at the required quality and scale?
- Are we better positioned than competitors on product, GTM, service, or partnerships?
- Can we cross the capability gap quickly enough?

#### Route to market advantage

- Do we have privileged access to customers?
- Can we sell through existing channels, installed base, ecosystem, or partnerships?
- Do we have lower CAC or higher conversion than likely competitors?
- Can we land and expand efficiently?

#### Economic superiority

- If we win, will the economics be attractive?
- Can we reach better margins, retention, or lifetime value than others?
- Is there a path to compounding advantage as we scale?
- Does scale improve the business meaningfully?

#### Defensibility

- What prevents competitors from matching us?
- Are there network effects, switching costs, data advantages, process advantages, regulatory moats, or ecosystem lock-in?
- Will our advantage strengthen over time or erode?
- Are we likely to become a feature inside someone else’s platform?

#### Strategic control

- Do we control critical parts of the value chain?
- Are we dependent on powerful partners, platforms, or suppliers?
- Could incumbents retaliate effectively?
- Can we shape the category, or only participate in it?

A good summary question for Right to Win is:

> **Assuming we enter, why should we believe we can win rather than merely participate?**

---

### 3) The practical distinction

A simple way to keep the two separate:

**Right to Play asks:**
- Is the market attractive?
- Is the problem real?
- Is entry feasible?
- Is there a viable business here for us?

**Right to Win asks:**
- Why us?
- Why now?
- Why will customers choose us?
- Why can’t competitors neutralize us?

---

### 4) A compact executive checklist

**Right to Play**
- Is the market worth being in?
- Is there real customer demand?
- Is the business model viable?
- Can we credibly enter?
- Is it strategically aligned?

**Right to Win**
- Do we have a sharp advantage?
- Is our advantage relevant to customer choice?
- Can we execute better than rivals?
- Is the advantage durable?
- Can we build an attractive, defensible position?

---

### 5) One useful decision rule

You generally want:

- Strong Right to Play + Strong Right to Win → pursue
- Strong Right to Play + Weak Right to Win → likely avoid, unless you have a clear path to build advantage
- Weak Right to Play + Strong Right to Win → usually suspect; your “advantage” may not matter if the market or fit is poor
- Weak on both → reject
