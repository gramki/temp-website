## United States

### OCC Heightened Standards (12 CFR Part 30, Appendix D)

The OCC's [Heightened Standards](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/12/appendix-D_to_part_30), finalized in 2014 with [technical amendments through December 2025](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/30/2025-23986/occ-guidelines-establishing-heightened-standards-for-certain-large-insured-national-banks-insured), establish risk governance requirements for banks with ≥$50 billion in total consolidated assets.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **Three lines of defense** structure: front-line units own risk; independent risk management provides oversight; internal audit provides assurance
- Board must require an effective risk governance framework, provide active oversight, exercise independent judgment, and include independent directors
- Risk appetite statement with concentration limits and breach escalation processes
- Risk data aggregation and reporting capabilities sufficient for timely, accurate risk reporting
- Talent management programs aligned with risk management objectives

**Cloud and platform operations relevance:** The three-lines-of-defense requirement directly shapes how cloud operations teams must be organized. Cloud platform teams (front-line) must own risk identification; an independent risk function must oversee cloud concentration, vendor dependency, and operational resilience; internal audit must provide assurance over cloud control effectiveness. Risk data aggregation requirements demand consolidated observability across multi-cloud environments.

**Penalty:** Supervisory action (MRAs, consent orders, cease-and-desist orders). No fixed statutory fine — the penalty is operational: noncompliance triggers examination findings that constrain business activities.

> Sources: [12 CFR 30, Appendix D](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/12/appendix-D_to_part_30), [OCC NR 2014-117](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2014/nr-occ-2014-117.html), [Federal Register — Technical Amendments (Dec 2025)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/30/2025-23986/occ-guidelines-establishing-heightened-standards-for-certain-large-insured-national-banks-insured)

### FFIEC IT Examination Handbook and Cybersecurity Assessment

The [FFIEC IT Examination Handbook](https://ithandbook.ffiec.gov/it-booklets) is the examination standard used by all five federal financial institution regulators (OCC, FDIC, Fed, NCUA, CFPB) plus state regulators. Key booklets relevant to cloud operations:

**Information Security booklet:**
- Requires documented information security programs with board-approved policies
- Risk assessment covering all information assets and systems
- Access controls, encryption, vulnerability management, monitoring
- Third-party service provider oversight integrated into the security program
- Business continuity considerations embedded in security architecture

**Business Continuity Management booklet:**
- Board and senior management governance responsibilities
- Business impact analysis identifying critical functions and interdependencies
- Resilience strategies covering change management, telecommunications, third-party providers, and personnel
- Scenario-based testing integrating information security incident response

**Cloud operations relevance:** Examination procedures evaluate whether institutions have adapted their information security and business continuity programs for cloud-hosted workloads. Examiners assess cloud-specific risks: shared responsibility models, data residency, vendor lock-in, and multi-tenancy. Institutions that cannot demonstrate cloud-aware security and continuity programs face adverse examination findings.

> Sources: [FFIEC IT Booklets](https://ithandbook.ffiec.gov/it-booklets), [Information Security Booklet](https://ithandbook.ffiec.gov/it-booklets/information-security), [Business Continuity Management](https://ithandbook.ffiec.gov/it-booklets/business-continuity-management)

### FFIEC Joint Statement on Cloud Computing (2020)

Issued April 30, 2020 ([OCC Bulletin 2020-46](https://occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2020/bulletin-2020-46.html)), the joint statement addresses cloud risk management for financial institutions. It does not impose new regulatory expectations but clarifies how existing expectations apply to cloud.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- Due diligence before cloud adoption: evaluate provider security posture, compliance capabilities, financial stability
- Clearly defined shared responsibility models documented in contracts
- Encryption, data tokenization, and identity/access management controls
- Security configuration management and vulnerability management
- Change management controls for cloud environments
- Business resilience and recovery plans that account for cloud-specific failure modes
- Ongoing monitoring of cloud provider compliance and performance
- Incident response coordination between institution and cloud provider

**Infrastructure investment forced:** Banks must build cloud governance capabilities including cloud security posture management (CSPM), identity federation and privileged access management across cloud environments, cloud-native monitoring and observability, and cloud-specific business continuity testing. The guidance effectively mandates a cloud center of excellence function.

> Sources: [OCC Bulletin 2020-46](https://occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2020/bulletin-2020-46.html), [FDIC FIL-2020](https://content.govdelivery.com/accounts/USFDIC/bulletins/2894702)

### Federal Reserve SR 11-7 (Model Risk Management)

[SR 11-7](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm), issued jointly by the Federal Reserve and OCC in 2011, is the foundational framework for model risk management in US banking.

**Application to AI-driven operational decisions:**
- All models used for decision-making require governance: development documentation, independent validation, ongoing monitoring
- Extended to AI/ML systems: explainability requirements, data quality assessment, hyperparameter documentation, adversarial robustness testing
- Fair lending and bias testing for credit-related AI models
- Ongoing monitoring: data drift detection, concept drift monitoring, retraining triggers
- Board-level reporting for material AI models

**Cloud and platform operations relevance:** As banks deploy AI/ML for operational decisions — capacity planning, incident prediction, automated remediation, security threat detection — SR 11-7 applies to these operational AI systems. AIOps and ML-driven platform management tools require the same model governance rigor as credit models. This creates a compliance overhead for platform engineering teams that may not have model risk management expertise.

**Emerging tension:** [GARP analysis (February 2026)](https://www.garp.org/risk-intelligence/operational/sr-11-7-age-agentic-ai-260227) documents that SR 11-7's assumptions of periodic review and stable model form break down for agentic AI systems. The Federal Reserve is actively reassessing the framework's applicability to autonomous, continuously learning systems.

**Cross-reference:** Covered in the digital identity and trust research at `org-8.0/what-we-sell/strategy/_research/digital-identity-and-trust/s2-regulatory-landscape.md` (Section: OCC/FDIC/Fed Joint Guidance — AI in Banking). That document covers the fair lending and bias testing angle; this document focuses on the operational AI and platform management implications.

> Sources: [SR 11-7](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm), [GARP — SR 11-7 and Agentic AI](https://www.garp.org/risk-intelligence/operational/sr-11-7-age-agentic-ai-260227), [OCC Bulletin 2025-26](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html)

### Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Risk Management (2023)

The [final interagency guidance](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2023/nr-ia-2023-53.html), issued June 6, 2023 by the OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve, replaces each agency's prior third-party risk management guidance with a unified standard.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- Full lifecycle management of all third-party relationships (planning → due diligence → contract → monitoring → termination)
- Risk-based approach: more rigorous oversight for critical activities (those that could cause significant harm if the provider fails)
- Due diligence covering financial condition, business experience, legal/regulatory compliance, risk management practices, information security, and operational resilience of the third party
- Contract provisions addressing performance standards, confidentiality, business continuity/disaster recovery, audit rights, subcontracting, and termination
- Ongoing monitoring: performance against SLAs, financial condition changes, security incidents, compliance status

**Cloud and multi-cloud governance implications:** This guidance is the regulatory foundation for cloud vendor management in US banking. It directly drives:
- Cloud provider risk assessment programs (applied to AWS, Azure, GCP, and specialized cloud providers)
- Multi-cloud governance frameworks addressing concentration risk across hyperscalers
- Cloud exit strategy planning (portability, data migration, service continuity)
- Subcontracting oversight (cloud provider use of sub-processors)
- Board-level reporting on critical cloud dependencies

The guidance explicitly covers fintech relationships, making it applicable to cloud-native banking-as-a-service providers, embedded finance platforms, and SaaS vendors.

> Sources: [OCC NR 2023-53](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2023/nr-ia-2023-53.html), [Full Guidance Text (PDF)](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2023/nr-ia-2023-53a.pdf), [Fed SR 23-4](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr2304.htm)

### SEC Cybersecurity Disclosure Rules (2023)

The SEC [adopted final rules](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-139) on July 26, 2023 (Release No. 33-11216), requiring public companies to disclose material cybersecurity incidents and describe their cybersecurity risk management.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **Material incident detection and assessment:** Determine materiality of cybersecurity incidents "without unreasonable delay" — requires mature incident detection, triage, and escalation processes
- **Form 8-K Item 1.05 disclosure:** Report material incidents within 4 business days of materiality determination — describe nature, scope, timing, and material impact
- **Annual Form 10-K Item 106 disclosure:** Describe processes for assessing, identifying, and managing material cybersecurity risks; board oversight of cybersecurity risk; management's role and expertise

**Infrastructure investment forced:**
- Real-time security monitoring and incident detection (SOC/SIEM capabilities)
- Incident materiality assessment workflows integrated with legal/compliance
- Board-level cybersecurity risk reporting dashboards
- Cybersecurity governance documentation and evidence management
- Rapid incident forensics and impact assessment capabilities

**Cloud operations relevance:** For banks operating on cloud infrastructure, the 4-business-day materiality disclosure requirement demands that cloud security incidents are detected, triaged, escalated, and assessed for materiality at speed. This drives investment in cloud-native security monitoring, cloud workload protection platforms, and integrated incident management across cloud and on-premises environments.

> Sources: [SEC Press Release 2023-139](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-139), [Final Rule (PDF)](https://www.sec.gov/files/rules/final/2023/33-11216.pdf), [Federal Register](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/08/04/2023-16194/cybersecurity-risk-management-strategy-governance-and-incident-disclosure)

### NIST Cybersecurity Framework 2.0

[NIST CSF 2.0](https://nist.gov/news-events/news/2024/02/nist-releases-version-20-landmark-cybersecurity-framework), released February 2024, is the first major update to the framework since 2014. While voluntary, CSF is referenced extensively by banking regulators in examination guidance.

**Key changes relevant to cloud operations:**
- **Govern function (new):** Establishes cybersecurity strategy, expectations, and policies at the organizational level. Includes organizational context, legal/regulatory requirements, critical objectives, and external dependencies. This is the governance layer that frames all other security functions.
- **Supply chain risk management (GV.SC):** Explicit C-SCRM category requiring organizations to establish supply chain risk management capabilities, define supplier requirements, and monitor third-party risk. Supported by [SP 800-161r1](https://www.nist.gov/publications/cybersecurity-supply-chain-risk-management-practices-systems-and-organizations) (updated November 2024).
- **Expanded scope:** Applicable to all organizations regardless of size, sector, or maturity — not just critical infrastructure.

**Cloud operations relevance:** The Govern function creates a framework for cloud governance that maps directly to cloud platform operations: organizational context (what cloud services support which business functions), supply chain risk (cloud vendor dependency, sub-processor risk), and policy alignment (cloud security policies within enterprise governance). Banking regulators increasingly reference CSF 2.0 categories in examination procedures.

> Sources: [NIST CSF 2.0 Release](https://nist.gov/news-events/news/2024/02/nist-releases-version-20-landmark-cybersecurity-framework), [NIST SP 1305 — C-SCRM Quick-Start Guide](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/1305/final), [RiskRecon — CSF 2.0 Supply Chain](https://blog.riskrecon.com/nist-csf-2.0-updated-third-party-supply-chain-risk-management)

## EU / UK

### DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) — Regulation (EU) 2022/2554

DORA is the most directly relevant regulation for cloud and platform operations in banking. It became fully applicable on **January 17, 2025** with no transitional period.

**Scope:** 20+ categories of financial entities (credit institutions, investment firms, payment institutions, insurance, crypto-asset service providers) and — critically — their ICT third-party service providers, including cloud providers.

**Five pillars of compliance:**

**1. ICT Risk Management (Articles 5–15):**
- Board-approved digital operational resilience strategy with explicit ICT risk tolerance
- Documented ICT risk management framework reviewed at least annually
- Appropriate budget allocation for ICT security, training, and resilience
- Regular board training on ICT risk topics
- ICT change management with pre-deployment testing
- Asset and dependency mapping across internal systems and all third-party providers

**2. ICT Incident Reporting (Articles 17–23):**
- Standardized incident classification taxonomy
- Major incident reporting to competent authorities using prescribed templates and timelines
- Annual reporting on new ICT service arrangements
- Notification requirements for planned arrangements supporting critical or important functions

**3. Digital Operational Resilience Testing (Article 24+):**
- Periodic testing of ICT systems including scenario-based simulations and tabletop exercises
- Threat-Led Penetration Testing (TLPT) for larger institutions — first notifications expected 2026
- Testing must cover critical ICT systems supporting important business functions

**4. ICT Third-Party Risk Management (Article 28+):**
- Comprehensive register of all ICT third-party service provider contracts (Register of Information)
- First submission deadline: April 30, 2025
- Contractual standards including business continuity, subcontracting arrangements, SLAs, data locations, criticality assessments
- Concentration risk monitoring across the sector

**5. Information Sharing:**
- Collaborative threat intelligence exchange networks among financial entities

**Critical Third-Party Provider (CTPP) oversight:**
- ESAs published list of [19 critical ICT third-party providers on November 18, 2025](https://www.morganlewis.com/blogs/sourcingatmorganlewis/2025/11/dora-eu-regulators-announce-list-of-critical-ict-third-party-providers)
- Designation criteria: systemic impact potential, importance of dependent entities, concentration of reliance, substitutability
- CTPPs must designate an EU coordination point and pay annual oversight fees
- ESAs conduct direct oversight through examinations and incident assessments
- Non-compliant CTPPs: ESAs may make noncompliance public and require financial entities to suspend or terminate use of services
- Penalty: up to **€5 million** for non-compliant CTPPs

**Cloud and platform operations investment forced:**
- ICT asset register and dependency mapping (automated discovery across cloud environments)
- Cloud-native incident detection, classification, and reporting workflows
- Resilience testing infrastructure (chaos engineering, disaster recovery testing in cloud)
- Third-party register management system with contractual metadata
- Multi-cloud concentration risk monitoring and reporting
- TLPT capability (red team testing of cloud infrastructure)
- Board-level ICT risk reporting dashboards

**Cross-reference:** DORA is covered at a high level in the digital identity research at `org-8.0/what-we-sell/strategy/_research/digital-identity-and-trust/s2-regulatory-landscape.md` (DORA section). That document focuses on identity infrastructure implications. This document provides the comprehensive cloud and platform operations analysis.

> Sources: [DORA Regulation Text (EUR-Lex)](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2554), [EBA — DORA](https://www.eba.europa.eu/regulation-and-policy/single-rulebook/interactive-single-rulebook/17716), [DORA GRC — 2026 Compliance Checklist](https://doragrc.com/blog/dora-compliance-checklist-2026), [Morgan Lewis — CTPP List](https://www.morganlewis.com/blogs/sourcingatmorganlewis/2025/11/dora-eu-regulators-announce-list-of-critical-ict-third-party-providers), [DORA Register of Information Update](https://www.regulation-dora.eu/blog-content/article?slug=dora-register-information-compliance-update-2025)

### EBA Guidelines on ICT and Security Risk Management

The [EBA Guidelines on ICT and Security Risk Management](https://www.eba.europa.eu/regulation-and-policy/internal-governance/guidelines-on-ict-and-security-risk-management), first issued in 2019 and updated for DORA alignment, provide detailed implementation guidance for ICT risk management in financial institutions.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- ICT governance with sound internal governance and control frameworks
- ICT strategy development aligned with business strategy
- Independent control functions and internal audit for ICT
- Information security and cybersecurity management

**Cloud outsourcing governance (EBA Cloud Outsourcing Recommendations):**
- Security of data and systems in cloud environments
- Data location and data processing jurisdiction controls
- Access and audit rights over cloud providers
- Chain outsourcing (sub-processor) oversight
- Contingency plans and exit strategies for cloud arrangements

**DORA alignment:** In January 2024, the EBA finalized Regulatory Technical Standards (RTS) under DORA harmonizing ICT risk management tools, methods, processes, and policies. The amended ICT risk and security management guidelines have a **compliance deadline of May 20, 2025**.

**Cloud operations relevance:** The EBA guidelines operationalize DORA's requirements at the implementation level. Banks must demonstrate that their cloud outsourcing arrangements comply with EBA expectations on data sovereignty, audit rights, exit planning, and chain outsourcing — driving investment in cloud governance platforms, multi-cloud management tools, and vendor risk management systems.

> Sources: [EBA Guidelines](https://www.eba.europa.eu/regulation-and-policy/internal-governance/guidelines-on-ict-and-security-risk-management), [EBA Cloud Outsourcing Recommendations](https://www.eba.europa.eu/publications-and-media/press-releases/eba-issues-guidance-use-cloud-service-providers-financial), [EBA DORA RTS (Jan 2024)](https://www.eba.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2024-01/bf5a2976-1a48-44f3-b5a7-56acd23ba55c/JC%202023%2086%20-%20Final%20report%20on%20draft%20RTS%20on%20ICT%20Risk%20Management%20Framework%20and%20on%20simplified%20ICT%20Risk%20Management%20Framework.pdf)

### PRA SS1/21 — Operational Resilience (UK)

The PRA's [Supervisory Statement SS1/21](https://bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2021/march/operational-resilience-impact-tolerances-for-important-business-services-ss) establishes the UK's operational resilience framework for banks, building societies, PRA-designated investment firms, and insurers.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **Identify important business services:** Services whose disruption could harm consumers, threaten market integrity, jeopardize firm viability, or destabilize the financial system
- **Set impact tolerances:** Maximum tolerable level of disruption, measured primarily by time duration plus other metrics, set at the point where further disruption risks firm safety/soundness or financial stability
- **Map dependencies:** Document people, processes, technology, facilities, and information supporting each important business service — including third-party dependencies
- **Scenario testing:** Regular testing against severe-but-plausible disruption scenarios, focusing on recovery and response (not prevention), evaluating both availability and integrity of resources
- **Invest in resilience:** Make necessary investments to operate consistently within impact tolerances

**Timeline:**
- March 31, 2022: Identify important business services and set impact tolerances
- **March 31, 2025: Full compliance — mapping, testing, and investment completed**

**Cloud and platform operations relevance:** The important business service framework requires banks to map which cloud services underpin which business services, set recovery time objectives per cloud dependency, test cloud failure scenarios (region outage, provider outage, data corruption), and invest in multi-region/multi-cloud resilience where impact tolerances demand it. The March 2025 compliance deadline means UK banks should now have demonstrable cloud resilience capabilities.

> Sources: [PRA SS1/21](https://bankofengland.co.uk/prudential-regulation/publication/2021/march/operational-resilience-impact-tolerances-for-important-business-services-ss), [PRA Rulebook — SS1/21](https://www.prarulebook.co.uk/guidance/supervisory-statements/ss01-21---operational-resilience-impact-tolerances-for-important-business-services)

### FCA PS21/3 — Building Operational Resilience

[FCA PS21/3](https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/policy-statements/ps21-3-building-operational-resilience) mirrors the PRA framework for FCA-regulated firms, applying to a broader scope including payment services providers, electronic money institutions, and enhanced SMCR firms.

**Operational capability demanded:** Substantially identical to PRA SS1/21:
- Important business service identification
- Impact tolerance setting
- Resource mapping (people, processes, technology, facilities, information)
- Scenario testing against severe-but-plausible disruptions
- Resilience investment

**Timeline:** Same as PRA — rules in force March 31, 2022; **full compliance by March 31, 2025**.

**Additional scope:** Includes payment service providers and electronic money institutions, meaning the operational resilience mandate extends beyond deposit-taking banks to the broader payments ecosystem.

> Sources: [FCA PS21/3](https://www.fca.org.uk/publications/policy-statements/ps21-3-building-operational-resilience), [FCA Operational Resilience](https://fca.org.uk/firms/operational-resilience)

### ECB SREP ICT Risk Assessment

The ECB's [Supervisory Review and Evaluation Process (SREP)](https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/activities/srep/2024/html/ssm.srep202412_operationalandictriskmethodology.en.html) includes a dedicated ICT risk assessment methodology applied to all significant institutions (SIs) under ECB direct supervision.

**Assessment methodology:**
- **Quantitative phase:** Assess inherent ICT risk level using bank data and supervisory indicators
- **Qualitative phase:** In-depth assessment of management and control frameworks using supervisory judgment and bank-specific factors
- ICT risk and operational risk are assessed separately but reported together

**Key definitions driving assessment:**
- **ICT Risk:** Loss related to network and information systems use that could compromise system security, technology-dependent processes, operations, or service provision
- **Operational Resilience:** Ability to deliver critical operations through disruption — identifying threats, protecting, responding, adapting, recovering

**DORA integration (from 2025):** The ECB has integrated DORA requirements into the SREP methodology, harmonizing ICT risk assessment with DORA's five pillars. Reported cyber incidents to the ECB by significant banks have doubled in recent years, driving heightened supervisory attention.

**Cloud operations relevance:** The ECB's ICT risk assessment directly evaluates cloud infrastructure risk management. Banks with weak cloud governance, inadequate resilience testing, or poor incident management will receive lower SREP scores, potentially resulting in Pillar 2 capital add-ons — a direct financial consequence of cloud operations deficiencies.

> Sources: [ECB SREP Operational & ICT Risk Methodology (2024)](https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/activities/srep/2024/html/ssm.srep202412_operationalandictriskmethodology.en.html), [ECB Blog — Operational Resilience in the Digital Age (Jan 2025)](https://www.ssm.europa.eu/press/blog/2025/html/ssm.blog20250117~32b79d4efa.pl.html), [2025 SREP Aggregated Results](https://www.bankingsupervision.europa.eu/activities/srep/2025/html/ssm.srep202511_aggregatedresults2025.en.html)

## India

### RBI Master Direction on IT Governance, Risk, Controls and Assurance Practices (2023)

The RBI's [comprehensive IT governance direction](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_ViewMasDirections.aspx?id=12562), issued November 2023 and effective April 1, 2024, consolidates IT governance requirements for all regulated entities.

**Scope:** Scheduled commercial banks, small finance banks, payments banks, NBFCs, credit information companies, all-India financial institutions.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **IT Governance:** Board-level oversight mandate; CIO role with defined responsibilities; IT Strategy Committee composition requirements; strategic alignment of IT investments with business objectives
- **Risk Management:** IT and cybersecurity risks integrated into enterprise risk management; periodic review by risk management committee (minimum yearly); risk assessment methodology; emerging technology risk evaluation; defined risk appetite
- **IT Control Framework:** Control documentation standards, testing methodology, implementation guidelines, deficiency remediation processes across:
  - Change management
  - Configuration management
  - Data classification
  - Access controls
  - Patch management
- **IT Assurance:** Continuous monitoring, control maturity assessment, independent assurance mechanisms, internal IT audit requirements
- **Specific controls:** Data migration policies (integrity assurance), audit trails for critical applications, cryptographic controls, segregation of duties, privileged access management, incident response with root cause analysis

**Cloud and platform operations relevance:** This direction mandates the governance structure within which cloud operations must function. It requires board-level accountability for IT risk (including cloud risk), formal change management for cloud infrastructure changes, privileged access management across cloud environments, and internal audit coverage of cloud controls. Banks cannot treat cloud as an IT function outside governance — it must be integrated into the enterprise IT risk framework.

> Sources: [RBI Master Direction](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/BS_ViewMasDirections.aspx?id=12562), [Aanetic Analysis](https://aanetic.com/rbi-master-direction-on-information-technology-governance-risk-controls-and-assurance-practices-2023/), [ET BFSI — RBI IT Governance Directions](https://bfsi.economictimes.indiatimes.com/news/policy/rbi-issues-comprehensive-directions-on-it-governance-in-banks-nbfcs/105053059)

### RBI Master Direction on Outsourcing of IT Services (2023)

The [RBI IT outsourcing direction](https://taxguru.in/rbi/master-direction-outsourcing-information-technology-services.html), effective October 1, 2023, establishes the governance framework for IT outsourcing including cloud services.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- Board-approved IT outsourcing policy
- Third-party service provider compliance obligations documented in contracts
- Full RBI access to data, systems, and premises of outsourced service providers for audit and regulatory oversight
- **Data localization mandate:** Core banking operations and payment system data must be stored and processed exclusively in India — no cross-border storage or duplication without exceptional approval
- Exit strategies and contingency plans for all IT outsourcing arrangements
- Risk assessment and ongoing monitoring of outsourced service providers
- Sub-contracting oversight with prior notification/approval requirements

**Cloud-specific implications:**
- Cloud providers serving Indian banks must enable data residency in India for regulated data
- RBI audit access requirements apply to cloud provider data centers and operations
- Banks must maintain exit strategies for cloud provider relationships — data portability, service migration plans
- Sub-contracting by cloud providers (e.g., managed services, support) requires oversight

**Cross-reference:** The data localization dimension is covered in `market-study/regulatory-landscape-payments-infrastructure.md` (Section 2.2 on tokenization) and the IFS Cloud initiative. This document focuses on the operational governance requirements for cloud outsourcing.

### RBI IFS Cloud / Cloud Computing Framework

The RBI is launching the **Indian Financial Services (IFS) Cloud** in 2025–26, developed by IFTAS (a wholly owned RBI subsidiary).

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **Data sovereignty:** Core banking and payment system data must reside within India (directive since 2018, reinforced in 2023 outsourcing direction)
- Community cloud designed specifically for regulated financial entities
- Reduced dependency on foreign cloud vendors (AWS, Azure, GCP)
- Support for core banking functions on domestic cloud infrastructure

**Infrastructure investment implications:**
- Banks using foreign hyperscalers must ensure India-region data residency for all regulated data
- Potential migration of workloads to IFS Cloud as it matures
- Dual-cloud architecture considerations: IFS Cloud for regulated workloads + hyperscaler for non-regulated/innovation workloads
- Investment in data classification systems to distinguish regulated from non-regulated data for cloud placement decisions

### SEBI Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience Framework (CSCRF)

[SEBI issued the CSCRF](https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/circulars/aug-2024/cybersecurity-and-cyber-resilience-framework-cscrf-for-sebi-regulated-entities-res-_85964.html) on August 20, 2024, establishing a comprehensive cybersecurity standard for all SEBI-regulated entities.

**Scope:** Five tiers of regulated entities based on operational scale:
- Market Infrastructure Institutions (MIIs) — most stringent requirements
- Qualified REs
- Mid-size REs
- Small-size REs
- Self-certification REs

**Operational capability demanded:**
- **Cybersecurity functions:** Governance, Identify, Detect, Protect, Respond, Recover
- **Cyber resilience goals:** Anticipate, Withstand, Contain, Recover, Evolve
- Board-approved cybersecurity policies
- Cybersecurity accountability at the organizational level
- Clear roles and responsibilities for cybersecurity functions
- Continuous cyber risk management frameworks (for MIIs, Qualified, and Mid-size REs)

**Deadlines:**
- Entities with existing cybersecurity frameworks: **January 1, 2025**
- All other regulated entities: **April 1, 2025**

**Cloud operations relevance:** While SEBI primarily regulates securities market participants (not banks directly), the CSCRF applies to entities that banks interact with in capital markets operations. The framework's six cybersecurity functions map closely to cloud security operations requirements and establish the expected security baseline for market infrastructure.

> Sources: [SEBI CSCRF Circular](https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/circulars/aug-2024/cybersecurity-and-cyber-resilience-framework-cscrf-for-sebi-regulated-entities-res-_85964.html), [Mondaq — CSCRF Overview](https://www.mondaq.com/india/security/1511068/strengthening-cybersecurity-and-resilience-an-overview-of-sebis-new-cscrf-framework), [SEBI Clarifications (Dec 2024)](https://www.sebi.gov.in/legal/circulars/dec-2024/clarifications-to-cybersecurity-and-cyber-resilience-framework-cscrf-for-sebi-regulated-entities-res-_90401.html)

### CERT-In Directions — 6-Hour Incident Reporting

[CERT-In Directions under Section 70B](https://cert-in.org.in/Directions70B.jsp) of the Information Technology Act, issued April 28, 2022, mandate the most aggressive incident reporting timeline globally.

**Operational capability demanded:**
- Report cyber incidents to CERT-In within **6 hours** of noticing or being informed of the incident
- 20 reportable incident types (expanded from 10), including:
  - DDoS attacks
  - Ransomware
  - Data breaches and data leaks
  - Intrusions into computer systems and websites
  - Attacks on critical infrastructure
  - Unauthorized access to social media accounts
- Maintain logs of all ICT systems for a rolling 180-day period and provide to CERT-In on demand
- Synchronize system clocks with NTP servers (NIC or NPLI)
- Designate a point of contact for CERT-In communication

**Penalty:** Imprisonment up to 1 year and/or fine up to ₹1 lakh under the IT Act.

**Cloud operations relevance:** The 6-hour reporting window is the most demanding globally (compared to GDPR's 72 hours, SEC's 4 business days, DORA's defined timelines). This mandates:
- Real-time cloud security monitoring and alerting
- Automated incident detection and classification
- Pre-drafted incident report templates for rapid submission
- 180-day log retention across all cloud environments (significant storage and observability investment)
- Clock synchronization across cloud infrastructure (trivial technically but must be verified)

**Comparison to global standards:**

| Jurisdiction | Incident Reporting Timeline |
|---|---|
| India (CERT-In) | **6 hours** |
| EU (DORA) | Initial notification within hours; intermediate report within 72 hours; final report within 1 month |
| EU (GDPR) | 72 hours to supervisory authority |
| USA (SEC) | 4 business days for material incidents |
| Singapore (PDPA) | 3 calendar days |

> Sources: [CERT-In Directions (PDF)](https://cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_Directions_70B_28.04.2022.pdf), [CERT-In — Directions page](https://cert-in.org.in/Directions70B.jsp), [Mondaq — 6 Hours Analysis](https://www.mondaq.com/india/it-and-internet/1270332/6-hours-to-report-cyber-incidents-to-cert-in), [LiveLaw — Compliance Analysis](https://www.livelaw.in/columns/indian-computer-emergency-response-team-cert-in-information-technology-act-cert-in-rules-202834)

## Key Findings

- **DORA is the single most consequential regulation for cloud and platform operations in banking.** It is the first regulation globally to mandate a comprehensive, enterprise-wide digital operational resilience framework that directly governs cloud infrastructure — from board-level ICT risk strategy through third-party provider registers to threat-led penetration testing. The January 2025 effective date with no transition period means compliance is being actively supervised now. The designation of 19 critical third-party providers (November 2025) and up to €5M penalties signal that enforcement has structural teeth.

- **The UK's PRA/FCA operational resilience framework (SS1/21 / PS21/3) reached full compliance on March 31, 2025.** UK banks must now demonstrate that they have identified important business services, set impact tolerances, mapped all technology and third-party dependencies, tested against severe disruption scenarios, and invested to operate within tolerances. This is the world's most mature operational resilience regime and sets the benchmark for what "good" looks like.

- **India's CERT-In 6-hour incident reporting mandate is the world's most aggressive.** Combined with the RBI's IT governance direction (board-level IT oversight, mandatory controls framework) and IT outsourcing direction (data localization, RBI audit access to cloud providers), India has created a regulatory environment that demands significant investment in cloud security monitoring, incident management, log retention (180 days), and domestic data residency.

- **The US regulatory approach is fragmented but comprehensive in aggregate.** There is no single US equivalent to DORA, but the combination of OCC Heightened Standards (three lines of defense for large banks), FFIEC cloud guidance (shared responsibility), interagency third-party risk management guidance (lifecycle vendor management), SEC cybersecurity disclosure (4-day incident reporting), SR 11-7 (AI model governance), and NIST CSF 2.0 (governance and supply chain risk) creates an overlapping web of expectations that is arguably more demanding than DORA in practice — because compliance must be demonstrated separately to each examiner against each framework.

- **Third-party/cloud vendor risk management is regulated in every jurisdiction.** The 2023 US interagency guidance, DORA's third-party oversight regime, EBA's cloud outsourcing recommendations, PRA/FCA's dependency mapping requirements, and RBI's IT outsourcing direction all converge on the same investment mandate: lifecycle cloud vendor management with exit planning, concentration risk monitoring, sub-contracting oversight, and audit rights.

- **Incident reporting timelines are compressing globally** — from CERT-In's 6 hours to DORA's hours-to-days framework to the SEC's 4 business days. This compression drives investment in real-time security monitoring, automated incident classification, and pre-staged reporting workflows. Banks operating across jurisdictions must build incident management that satisfies the most demanding timeline (6 hours) and then adapt reporting for each jurisdiction.

- **The ECB's integration of DORA into SREP creates a direct capital consequence for cloud operations deficiencies.** Poor ICT risk management scores in the SREP can result in Pillar 2 capital add-ons — meaning a bank's cloud operations maturity directly affects its cost of capital. This is the strongest financial incentive for cloud operations investment in any jurisdiction.

- **AI/ML governance for operational decisions is an emerging but under-specified requirement.** SR 11-7 applies to AIOps and ML-driven platform management, but the framework's assumptions (periodic review, stable model form) strain against autonomous, continuously learning systems. Banks deploying AI for incident prediction, capacity management, or automated remediation must build model governance that regulators have not yet fully specified — creating both compliance risk and first-mover opportunity.

- **India's IFS Cloud initiative introduces a potential sovereign cloud mandate.** If the IFS Cloud becomes the expected standard for regulated financial data, banks will need to architect for dual-cloud environments (IFS for regulated workloads, hyperscaler for others) — a structural investment in cloud platform design.

## Regulatory Convergence: Cloud Operations Investment Mandate

Across all three geographies, the regulations converge on five infrastructure capabilities:

### 1. Cloud Governance and Risk Framework
Every jurisdiction requires board-level accountability for technology/ICT risk, documented risk management frameworks, and risk appetite statements that encompass cloud:
- USA: OCC Appendix D (three lines of defense); NIST CSF 2.0 (Govern function)
- EU/UK: DORA (board-approved ICT risk strategy); PRA SS1/21 (important business service identification)
- India: RBI IT Governance Direction (board oversight, IT Strategy Committee, CIO role)

### 2. Third-Party / Cloud Vendor Lifecycle Management
Universal requirement for structured oversight of cloud provider relationships:
- USA: Interagency guidance 2023 (full lifecycle management); FFIEC cloud guidance (shared responsibility)
- EU/UK: DORA (Register of Information, CTPP oversight); EBA (cloud outsourcing recommendations)
- India: RBI IT outsourcing direction (audit access, exit strategies, sub-contracting oversight)

### 3. Incident Detection, Response, and Reporting
Accelerating reporting timelines force investment in detection, classification, and reporting:
- USA: SEC (4 business days for material incidents)
- EU: DORA (hours-to-days for major incidents); GDPR (72 hours)
- India: CERT-In (6 hours); RBI (incident response with root cause analysis)

### 4. Operational Resilience Testing
Testing against severe-but-plausible disruption scenarios:
- USA: FFIEC (business continuity testing); OCC Appendix D (risk data aggregation)
- EU/UK: DORA (TLPT, scenario simulations); PRA SS1/21 / FCA PS21/3 (scenario testing within impact tolerances)
- India: RBI IT Governance Direction (continuous monitoring, control maturity assessment)

### 5. Data Sovereignty and Cloud Placement
Jurisdiction-specific data residency requirements:
- USA: No federal data localization mandate; sector-specific requirements
- EU: DORA (data location tracking in registers); GDPR (adequacy decisions for transfers)
- India: RBI (core banking/payment data must reside in India); IFS Cloud (domestic sovereign cloud)

## Gaps and Unresolved Questions

1. **OCC Heightened Standards — cloud-specific guidance:** Appendix D does not explicitly reference cloud computing. Its application to cloud operations is through interpretation of general risk governance requirements. Whether the OCC issues cloud-specific supplementary guidance is an open question. 2. **DORA delegated/implementing acts completion:** Not all secondary legislation was finalized by the January 2025 effective date. Specific reporting templates, testing standards, and incident classification taxonomies may still be evolving. [needs monitoring of ESA publications]

3. **DORA TLPT scope and timing:** The first wave of TLPT notifications is expected in 2026, but which institutions will be required to conduct threat-led penetration testing — and the specific testing methodology — requires confirmation from national competent authorities. [unverified — needs manual confirmation]

4. **RBI IT outsourcing direction — specific cloud provisions:** The master direction covers IT outsourcing broadly. Whether the RBI has issued cloud-specific supplementary circulars beyond the general IT outsourcing direction and the IFS Cloud initiative requires verification against the complete set of RBI circulars. [needs manual review of RBI circular database]

5. **SEBI CSCRF — banking applicability:** SEBI regulates securities market participants, not banks directly. The extent to which CSCRF compliance flows through to banks via their market infrastructure dependencies (exchanges, depositories, clearing corporations) requires clarification. [needs cross-reference with RBI/SEBI coordination]

6. **ECB SREP — ICT risk capital add-on methodology:** The specific methodology for translating SREP ICT risk scores into Pillar 2 capital requirements is not publicly documented in detail. The quantum of capital impact for cloud operations deficiencies is not verifiable from public sources. [unverified — needs supervisory source]

7. **US federal cloud computing regulation:** No US regulation specifically mandates or prohibits cloud adoption by banks. The regulatory posture is "cloud is acceptable if risks are managed" (FFIEC 2020 statement). Whether a more prescriptive federal cloud framework emerges — similar to DORA — remains an open question.

8. **CERT-In 6-hour requirement — practical enforcement:** The 6-hour window is operationally demanding. How CERT-In evaluates compliance — particularly for incidents detected outside business hours or for complex multi-vector attacks — is not well documented. Enforcement data on penalties imposed for late reporting would strengthen this analysis. [unverified — needs enforcement data]

9. **Cross-jurisdictional incident reporting coordination:** A bank operating in the US, EU, and India faces overlapping incident reporting obligations with different timelines (6 hours, ~24 hours, 4 business days), different thresholds (all incidents vs. major vs. material), and different authorities. Whether any harmonization or mutual recognition mechanisms exist is unresolved. [needs further research]

10. **RBI IFS Cloud — timeline and adoption mandate:** Whether IFS Cloud adoption becomes mandatory for regulated entities or remains optional is unclear. The initiative's maturity, service catalog, and pricing relative to hyperscaler alternatives will determine adoption trajectory. [needs monitoring as IFS Cloud launches]

## Raw Notes

### US Research Notes
- The US has no single DORA-equivalent — instead, cloud operations compliance is the intersection of ~7 overlapping frameworks. This fragmentation is itself a compliance cost driver.
- OCC Appendix D's three-lines-of-defense model is structurally relevant for cloud: platform engineering teams (front line), cloud risk/security teams (independent risk management), and IT audit. Most banks have not fully operationalized this model for cloud-specific risks.
- The 2023 interagency third-party guidance explicitly covers fintech relationships — this is the regulatory hook for scrutinizing cloud-native banking-as-a-service providers and embedded finance platforms.
- SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules are unusual in that they apply a securities-law framework (material disclosure, liability) to what is fundamentally an operational/technology risk. This creates board-level urgency for cybersecurity investment that supervisory examination alone may not achieve.
- NIST CSF 2.0's Govern function fills a gap that CSF 1.0 had: it provides the governance framing that connects cybersecurity to business strategy. For cloud operations, this means cloud security is no longer a technical concern — it's a governance obligation.
- SR 11-7's application to AIOps is a sleeper issue. Banks deploying AI for capacity planning, automated scaling, incident prediction, and remediation are building model risk without model risk governance. The GARP February 2026 analysis is the first public acknowledgment of this gap.

### EU/UK Research Notes
- DORA's Register of Information requirement (April 2025 first submission) forced a sector-wide inventory of ICT dependencies. This data will be used by ESAs to identify concentration risk — meaning banks that are heavily dependent on a single cloud provider may face supervisory pressure to diversify.
- The 19 designated CTPPs include hyperscale cloud providers. These providers now face direct regulatory oversight by European supervisory authorities — a structural shift in the regulatory dynamic between banks and their cloud providers.
- UK PRA/FCA operational resilience regime (March 2025 full compliance) is the most mature framework globally. UK banks' experience with impact tolerance setting, dependency mapping, and scenario testing provides a template for other jurisdictions.
- ECB's integration of DORA into SREP creates a quantifiable financial incentive: poor cloud operations → lower SREP ICT score → higher Pillar 2 capital requirement → higher cost of capital. This is the strongest business case for cloud operations investment.
- The interaction between DORA and GDPR creates dual reporting obligations for incidents involving personal data in ICT systems. Banks must report to both the competent authority (DORA) and the data protection authority (GDPR) — potentially within different timelines.

### India Research Notes
- India's regulatory approach combines prescriptive governance (RBI IT Governance Direction) with aggressive enforcement mechanisms (CERT-In 6-hour reporting) and data sovereignty mandates (localization). This triad creates a uniquely demanding cloud operations environment.
- The IFS Cloud initiative represents a sovereign cloud model that has no direct parallel in the US or EU. If it succeeds, it could become a template for other emerging market regulators.
- RBI's insistence on audit access to cloud provider facilities is a practical barrier for banks using hyperscaler services. AWS, Azure, and GCP do not typically provide physical facility access to individual customer regulators. Banks must negotiate contractual provisions that satisfy this requirement — typically through third-party audit reports (SOC 2/3, ISO 27001) and regulatory access mechanisms.
- CERT-In's 180-day log retention mandate interacts with cloud cost management. Retaining 180 days of detailed logs across cloud environments can be expensive, particularly for high-throughput financial services workloads. This is a cloud FinOps consideration that banks may not have priced into their cloud TCO models.
- SEBI's CSCRF, while not directly applicable to banks, sets a cybersecurity standard for market infrastructure that banks depend on. Bank cloud operations teams must consider whether their upstream and downstream market infrastructure connections meet CSCRF standards.
