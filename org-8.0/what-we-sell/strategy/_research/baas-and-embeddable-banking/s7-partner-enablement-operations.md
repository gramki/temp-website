### Cross-Reference: Cloud and Platform Operations Research

The `_research/cloud-and-platform-operations/s6-banking-cloud-governance.md` research directly addresses multi-tenancy in banking SaaS and concludes:

- **Banking-specificity assessment**: "Regulated-industry-specific (not unique to banking)" — fundamental technical patterns (silo vs. pooled isolation) are the same across regulated industries, but banking has more prescriptive regulatory oversight of tenant isolation than most sectors
- **Key differentiator**: The combination of FFIEC/OCC examination authority, MAS's specific advisory on cloud multi-tenancy risks, and enforcement actions (TSB £48.65M fine for migration failure) creates a compliance burden heavier than general enterprise requirements
- **TSB Bank case**: £48.65M FCA/PRA fine for inadequate isolation and testing of new multi-tenant platform (Proteo4UK) — 7+ months of disruption for 5.2M customers
- **DBS Bank case**: MAS imposed six-month pause on non-essential IT activities after digital disruptions; shift to cloud-native microservices created complex failure modes

The synthesis notes (`synthesis-notes.md`) further confirm: "No horizontal vendor natively provides tenant isolation at the operational layer, data sovereignty for operational data, banking-grade SLA governance, or DORA-compliant incident records."

### Unit Economics Observations

- **Interchange compression at scale**: As fintech partners grow transaction volume, the issuing bank's share compresses from ~20 basis points to 2–3 basis points. This creates a structural tension: the bank bears increasing compliance burden while its per-transaction economics deteriorate. (Source: Sacra)
- **Durbin exemption dependency**: BaaS economics for sponsor banks depend heavily on Durbin Amendment exemption (banks <$10B in assets can charge higher interchange). Loss of this exemption would fundamentally alter the BaaS business model for community banks. (Source: Sacra)
- **Overhead scales non-linearly with partners**: "Your overhead cost is going to increase if you want to do it correctly" — Sunrise Banks' Chief Risk Officer, per American Banker. Each additional fintech partner adds compliance monitoring load, examination scope, and regulatory risk. (Source: [American Banker](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/what-is-the-cost-of-doing-banking-as-a-service-well))
- **13.5% of severe US enforcement actions in 2023 targeted BaaS banks** — disproportionate regulatory attention relative to the number of banks operating BaaS programs. (Source: [Castellum.AI](https://www.castellum.ai/insights/banking-as-a-service-compliance-best-practices))

## 7. Key Findings

### Structural Findings

- **BaaS is an operating model, not an API integration.** The 12 operational requirements identified in §1 (from due diligence through offboarding) represent a full-stack operating system that must run alongside API infrastructure. Eight enforcement actions in 2023–2024 confirm that regulators treat BaaS as a supervised activity requiring the same controls as direct banking operations.

- **The middleware layer is the single point of failure.** Synapse's collapse proved that an unregulated intermediary maintaining ledger records between fintechs and banks creates catastrophic risk. The $85M fund shortfall and 100,000+ affected customers demonstrate that ledger accuracy, reconciliation, and beneficial ownership tracking cannot be delegated to entities outside the regulatory perimeter. Column's vertically integrated model (bank + platform, no middleware) is a direct structural response to this risk.

- **Compliance overhead scales non-linearly with partners.** Blue Ridge Bank grew to ~70 fintech partnerships without proportional compliance infrastructure scaling, resulting in a consent order and troubled condition designation. The common pattern across enforcement actions: partner count growth outpaces compliance staffing and systems investment.

- **Partner offboarding is a critical gap.** No BaaS vendor offers productized wind-down tooling. Blue Ridge Bank and Metropolitan Commercial Bank each spent the better part of a year exiting their BaaS programs. The OCC Interagency Guidance lifecycle stage 5 ("Termination") remains the least operationalized requirement.

- **Regulatory guidance is delivered through enforcement, not rulemaking.** Bankers are "mining consent orders for clues" to managing BaaS (American Banker). The 2024 interagency joint statement on deposit arrangements is the closest regulators have come to BaaS-specific guidance, but operational requirements are largely inferred from enforcement action patterns.

### Vendor Landscape Findings

- **No single vendor covers the full operational lifecycle.** Synctera comes closest with its Ground Control operational support, Company Profile due diligence tool, and Case Manager — but even Synctera lacks productized offboarding and wind-down capabilities.

- **Sandbox maturity varies significantly.** Column's sandbox (full feature parity, KYC simulation, ACH return simulation) sets the benchmark. Treasury Prime recently upgraded to match production. Others lag in fidelity.

- **Multi-tenant administration is maturing.** Q2 Helix's Admin Console (one program across multiple banks or multiple programs with one bank) and Mambu's Configuration as Code represent the most advanced multi-tenant governance approaches.

- **Core banking platforms (Mambu, Thought Machine) serve banks building BaaS; BaaS platforms (Column, Unit, Synctera, Treasury Prime) serve fintechs consuming BaaS.** This is a distinct market segmentation with different buyer personas and operational requirements.

### Economic Findings

- **Interchange compression creates a structural cost-coverage gap.** Bank's share compresses from ~20bps to 2–3bps at scale while compliance obligations increase. This means the BaaS economic model depends on (a) transaction volume growth outpacing per-unit margin compression, (b) non-interchange revenue (account fees, lending revenue share), or (c) Durbin exemption preservation.

- **Enforcement is expensive even without fines.** Consent orders requiring independent third-party assessments, enhanced staffing, technology investment, and regulatory pre-approval for new partnerships impose costs that may exceed financial penalties.
