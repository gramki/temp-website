## Key Findings

### USA: Regulatory tightening is systemic, not episodic

- **The 2023 interagency guidance is the foundation.** OCC/FDIC/Fed replaced fragmented prior guidance with a unified third-party risk management framework. It applies to all banks with third-party relationships and covers the full life cycle — planning, due diligence, contract negotiation, ongoing monitoring, and termination. Every BaaS bank must now demonstrate risk management commensurate with the criticality of each fintech relationship.
- **The Synapse collapse is the inflection point.** The April 2024 bankruptcy of Synapse Financial Technologies — middleware connecting fintechs to banks — exposed structural fragility in BaaS arrangements. $265M in deposits were frozen, up to $96M remains missing, and 100K+ consumers lost access. The FDIC responded with (a) a proposed custodial deposit recordkeeping rule (Sept 2024), (b) a proposed brokered deposit rule overhaul (July 2024), and (c) a direct monitoring system for fintech companies partnering with banks.
- **Enforcement is industry-wide, not targeted.** Piermont Bank's CEO stated: "Every bank that touches BaaS is getting an enforcement action." In 2023–2024 alone, Cross River, Evolve, Blue Ridge, Lineage, Piermont, Sutton, Choice, and First Fed all received consent orders. Common deficiencies: BSA/AML compliance, third-party risk management, internal controls, end-user account monitoring.
- **FDIC brokered deposit rule overhaul would restructure BaaS economics.** The July 2024 proposed rule would eliminate the enabling transactions exception and exclusive deposit placement exception. Many BaaS deposits currently classified as non-brokered would be reclassified, increasing deposit insurance assessments and potentially making some BaaS models economically unviable.
- **CFPB BNPL regulation is in flux.** The May 2024 interpretive rule classifying BNPL lenders as card issuers under Reg Z was withdrawn in May 2025. However, the CFPB continues market monitoring (Dec 2025 market report). The regulatory direction is clear even if timing is uncertain.
- **State MTL licensing creates distribution friction.** 49 states require money transmitter licenses at $1–3M cost and 12–18 months to obtain full coverage. The MTMA is standardizing requirements (25+ states adopted 2023–2025), but BaaS platforms must still manage multi-state compliance.

### India: RBI has closed the regulatory arbitrage loop

- **Digital Lending Directions (2022, consolidated 2025) eliminated pass-through accounts.** Loan disbursement and repayment must flow directly between the bank and the borrower's account — no LSP intermediary accounts. This structurally requires direct integration between the lending bank's core and the borrower's bank account.
- **FLDG (Default Loss Guarantee) capped at 5%.** Fintech partners providing credit enhancement to their bank partners are limited to 5% of the outstanding loan portfolio. This constrains the risk-transfer model that many BNPL/embedded lending fintechs used.
- **Co-lending framework expanded beyond PSL (Jan 2026).** The new Co-Lending Arrangements Directions 2025 allow bank-NBFC co-lending across all loan categories, with minimum 10% retention per lender and borrower-level asset classification that synchronizes across all co-lending partners. This is a BaaS-enabling regulation — it creates a formal framework for distributed lending.
- **PPI framework now enables UPI interoperability (Dec 2024).** Full-KYC PPIs can now transact on UPI via third-party apps, making prepaid wallets interoperable with the broader payments ecosystem. This opens embedded payment use cases.
- **Payment Aggregator regulations unified (Sept 2025).** RBI's Master Direction consolidates online, physical, and cross-border PA regulations. Non-bank PAs require RBI authorization. Merchant KYC and escrow requirements tightened.
- **Account Aggregator framework is at scale.** 252.9M linked users and 2.61B enabled accounts create a consent-based data-sharing layer that enables embedded finance products (credit, insurance, wealth) without screen scraping or bilateral data agreements.

### UK/EU: Regulatory enablement alongside tightening

- **FCA Consumer Duty applies to BaaS-delivered products.** Any financial product delivered through a BaaS arrangement must meet the four Consumer Duty outcomes (products/services, fair value, consumer understanding, consumer support). The bank, not the fintech, bears regulatory responsibility — but must demonstrate outcomes monitoring across all distribution channels.
- **Critical Third Party regime (Jan 2025) creates new oversight layer.** FCA/PRA/BoE can designate systemically important third-party providers for direct oversight. While no CTPs have been designated yet, BaaS infrastructure providers (core banking vendors, middleware providers) are plausible candidates.
- **PSD3/PSR (expected H2 2027–2028) strengthens open banking as embedded finance infrastructure.** Mandatory FAPI/OAuth API standards, Verification of Payee, and the parallel Financial Data Access Regulation extending data sharing to investments/insurance/pensions create the regulatory plumbing for broader embedded finance.

### Enforcement patterns: What the consent orders demand

Across all enforcement actions, regulators consistently require:

1. **Board-level governance** — Third-party risk management programs must be board-adopted and board-monitored, not delegated to compliance teams
2. **End-user account-level compliance** — BSA/AML obligations extend through the BaaS chain to the end customer, not just the fintech partner
3. **Independent assessment** — Banks must engage independent firms to assess existing fintech partnerships (Lineage: within 90 days)
4. **Termination planning** — Contingency plans for orderly wind-down of fintech partnerships (Lineage: within 60 days)
5. **Growth restrictions** — Banks cannot grow >10% annually without regulatory approval while under consent orders
6. **Pre-approval for new partnerships** — Cross River must obtain regulatory non-objection for every new fintech partner
7. **Retroactive transaction review** — Piermont was ordered to review all transactions since September 2022

Under the current administration, the rule may be withdrawn, modified, or finalized. This is the single most consequential pending regulation for BaaS economics. `[needs manual confirmation]`
2. **FDIC custodial deposit recordkeeping NPR final status unknown.** Comment period closed Dec 2024. Final rule status unconfirmed as of March 2026. `[needs manual confirmation]`
3. **OCC fintech charter policy direction.** The OCC approved five national trust bank charters in 2025 (digital currency-focused). It is unclear whether a broader fintech charter path (beyond crypto) will re-emerge under the current administration.
4. **CFPB enforcement posture.** The CFPB has withdrawn the BNPL interpretive rule and reduced enforcement activity under the current administration. The degree to which this extends to embedded lending oversight broadly is uncertain.
5. **RBI's stance on BaaS-like intermediary models.** India does not have a formal "BaaS" regulatory category. The RBI regulates via activity-specific directions (digital lending, PPIs, payment aggregators, co-lending). Whether the RBI will issue consolidated BaaS-specific guidance — similar to the US interagency guidance — is an open question.
6. **FCA CTP designations.** No Critical Third Parties have been designated as of March 2026. If core banking platform vendors or BaaS middleware providers are designated, the compliance burden on banks using them would increase substantially.
7. **UK-specific BaaS regulatory framework.** The UK does not have BaaS-specific regulation equivalent to the US interagency guidance. The FCA relies on existing outsourcing rules, Consumer Duty, and the CTP regime. Whether a more targeted framework emerges is unclear.
8. **EU DORA impact on BaaS providers.** DORA (effective Jan 2025) imposes ICT risk management, incident reporting, and resilience testing requirements. Its specific impact on BaaS middleware providers (who sit between banks and fintechs) needs deeper analysis. `See: account-products-and-banking/s2-regulatory-landscape.md, row 18`
9. **India DPDP Act implementation timeline.** The Digital Personal Data Protection Act's phased rollout (Nov 2025 foundation, Nov 2026 consent managers, May 2027 full compliance) will layer additional consent management requirements on all BaaS-style data sharing. `See: account-products-and-banking/s2-regulatory-landscape.md, row 13`

## Raw Notes and Excerpts

### Synapse collapse — structural anatomy

The Synapse collapse exposed a specific architectural vulnerability: BaaS middleware providers maintaining deposit records on behalf of banks without adequate reconciliation. When Synapse filed for bankruptcy, banks could not determine which end customers owned which deposits because the records were held by the middleware, not the bank. FDIC insurance applies to the bank's deposit — but if the bank cannot identify the beneficial owners, the insurance mechanism breaks down. The FDIC's proposed custodial recordkeeping rule is a direct response: banks must maintain their own records of beneficial owners and balances, with daily reconciliation, regardless of whether a third-party handles the customer-facing layer.

> "More than 100,000 customers of fintech apps like Yotta, Juno, and Copper were locked out of their accounts starting in May, with $265 million in deposits frozen." — CNBC, July 2024

> "Up to $96 million in customer funds remains missing." — CNBC/NBC, July 2024

### Piermont Bank CEO on enforcement climate

> "Every bank that touches BaaS is getting an enforcement action." — Piermont Bank CEO, quoted in Banking Dive, 2024

### FDIC on brokered deposit reclassification rationale

The FDIC cited the Synapse bankruptcy and 2023 large bank failures as motivating the proposed brokered deposit rule overhaul. The proposal aims to bring fintech-intermediated deposits within the brokered deposit framework, increasing capital and liquidity requirements for banks holding them.

### RBI Digital Lending — structural mandate

The 2022/2025 digital lending directions structurally require that loan disbursement and repayment flow directly between the bank and the borrower — no intermediary pass-through accounts. This eliminates the "middleware holds the money" pattern that caused the Synapse crisis in the US. India's regulatory architecture is preemptively more restrictive on this specific failure mode.

### Co-lending as BaaS enablement

The RBI's 2025 Co-Lending Arrangements Directions create a formal, regulated framework for distributed lending — banks and NBFCs co-originating loans with defined risk sharing (80:20), minimum retention (10%), and synchronized asset classification. This is functionally a regulated BaaS lending model. The expansion beyond priority sector lending to all loan categories (effective Jan 2026) makes it broadly applicable.

### FCA Consumer Duty — chain-of-responsibility question

Under FCA Consumer Duty, the regulated firm (the bank) bears responsibility for consumer outcomes across all distribution channels — including BaaS-delivered products. If a fintech partner delivers a deposit product with poor outcomes (unfair pricing, inadequate support, non-transparent terms), the bank faces FCA enforcement. This creates a regulatory incentive for banks to maintain deep visibility into partner-delivered product experience — or exit BaaS arrangements where they cannot ensure outcomes.

### MTMA adoption trajectory

The Money Transmission Modernization Act is being adopted state-by-state to create uniform licensing standards:
- 2023: Arkansas, California, Georgia, Hawaii, Indiana, Iowa, Maryland, Minnesota, Nevada, North Dakota
- 2024: Connecticut, Illinois, Kansas, Missouri, New Hampshire, South Carolina, Wisconsin
- 2025: Colorado, Massachusetts, Mississippi, Nebraska, Virginia, Alaska, Hawaii

This reduces but does not eliminate the multi-state licensing burden for BaaS-distributed payment products.

### GENIUS Act — stablecoin as BaaS-adjacent

The GENIUS Act (signed July 2025) creates the first federal licensing framework for payment stablecoins. While not directly BaaS regulation, it intersects: banks offering stablecoin-backed products through BaaS arrangements will need to comply with both stablecoin regulation and BaaS oversight requirements. The Act preempts state regulation, potentially simplifying the licensing landscape for stablecoin-based embedded finance products.
