## EU AI Act — Banking Implications

### High-Risk Classifications

Under Annex III, Category 5(b) — "Access to and enjoyment of essential private services and essential public services and benefits" — the following banking AI use cases are **explicitly classified high-risk**:

1. **Credit scoring / creditworthiness assessment** — AI systems intended to evaluate the creditworthiness of natural persons or establish their credit scores (with a narrow exception for systems solely used to detect financial fraud)
2. **Insurance risk assessment and pricing** — AI systems used for risk assessment and pricing for natural persons in life and health insurance

Additional banking AI use cases that fall under **adjacent high-risk categories** or require careful classification analysis:

- **Employment-related AI** (Annex III, Category 4) — hiring, performance evaluation, and promotion AI used within banks
- **Biometric identification** (Annex III, Category 1) — remote biometric identification used in KYC or customer onboarding
- **AI affecting access to essential services** — any AI system that determines access to financial services broadly

**What is not explicitly classified**: Fraud detection AI, AML/CFT monitoring AI, and operational risk AI are not explicitly listed in Annex III. However, the European Commission is mandated to issue guidelines on high-risk classification by **2 February 2026**, which may expand or clarify banking-specific classifications.

Source: [EU AI Act Annex III](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/annex/3)

### Accountability Requirements

The EU AI Act establishes a **tiered accountability chain** across the AI value chain:

**Provider obligations (Article 16):**
- Establish and maintain quality management systems
- Produce and maintain technical documentation
- Ensure automatic logging capabilities
- Conduct conformity assessments before market placement
- Cooperate with competent authorities
- Bear primary responsibility for the system's design and safety

**Deployer obligations (Article 26):**
- Use AI systems in accordance with provider instructions
- **Assign competent, trained natural persons** with authority and support to perform human oversight
- Monitor system operation for risks and malfunctions
- Maintain event logs for a minimum of **6 months**
- Conduct **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessments** (FRIA) where required
- Report serious incidents to the provider and relevant authorities
- Register use of high-risk AI systems (public bodies)

Critically: **deployer obligations cannot be contractually transferred to the provider**. Banks that deploy third-party AI systems remain accountable for oversight, monitoring, and incident reporting regardless of what their vendor contracts say.

**Value chain accountability (Article 25):** Distributors, importers, and authorized representatives each bear defined obligations, creating an end-to-end accountability chain from system development through deployment.

Source: [Article 26](https://www.aiact-info.eu/article-26-obligations-of-deployers-of-high-risk-ai-systems/), [Article 16](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/16/)

### Identity Infrastructure Implications

The EU AI Act does not use the term "identity" in the IAM sense, but its requirements create **implicit identity infrastructure demands**:

**Traceability (Article 12 — Record-Keeping):**
- High-risk AI systems must have automatic event logging throughout their lifetime
- Logs must record usage periods (start/end timestamps), input data matched, reference databases used, and — critically — **identification of the natural persons involved in result verification**
- This implies a binding between human identities and AI system actions: every decision must be traceable to a responsible person

**Human oversight (Article 14):**
- Systems must include human-machine interface tools enabling effective oversight
- Oversight personnel must be enabled to understand system capabilities and limitations, detect anomalies, and remain aware of automation bias
- Oversight measures must be **proportionate to the system's autonomy level** — more autonomous systems require more robust oversight infrastructure

**Transparency (Article 13):**
- Deployers must receive sufficient information to interpret system outputs
- System instructions must document performance levels, known limitations, and intended purpose

**Risk management (Article 9):**
- Continuous, iterative risk management throughout the system lifecycle
- Testing under real-world conditions with predefined metrics and probabilistic thresholds

**Practical identity infrastructure requirements this implies for banks:**

| EU AI Act Requirement | Identity Infrastructure Needed |
|---|---|
| Article 12 — identify personnel verifying results | Human identity binding to AI decisions |
| Article 14 — assign competent oversight persons | Role-based access control for AI oversight |
| Article 26 — retain logs for 6+ months | Immutable audit trails linking AI actions to human sponsors |
| Article 9 — continuous risk management | Lifecycle identity management for AI systems themselves |
| Article 13 — transparency to deployers | AI system identity metadata (capabilities, limitations, version) |

### Compliance Timeline

| Date | Milestone | Banking Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **2 Feb 2025** | Prohibited practices (Art. 5) and AI literacy (Art. 4) | Banks must train staff; social scoring and subliminal manipulation banned |
| **2 Aug 2025** | GPAI obligations begin | Banks using foundation models (LLMs) must ensure provider compliance with transparency and copyright rules |
| **2 Feb 2026** | EC guidelines on high-risk classification due | May clarify or expand which banking AI use cases are high-risk |
| **2 Aug 2026** | **High-risk AI obligations fully applicable** | Credit-scoring and insurance-pricing AI must comply; human oversight, logging, FRIA required |
| **2 Aug 2027** | Full applicability for legacy systems | High-risk AI in regulated products and pre-existing GPAI models must comply |

**Penalty structure:**
- Prohibited practices: up to **€35M or 7% of global annual turnover** (whichever is higher)
- Other infringements: up to **€15M or 3% of global annual turnover**

Source: [EU AI Act Implementation Timeline](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/eu-ai-act-implementation-timeline)

## Regulatory Guidance (Beyond EU AI Act)

### USA (OCC/FDIC/Fed, NIST)

**Federal banking regulators:**

- The OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve have each developed compliance plans for **OMB Memorandum M-25-21** ("Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust"), published September 2025
- The Federal Reserve established a **Chief Artificial Intelligence Officer (CAIO)** to advance AI objectives and governance
- The FDIC appointed a CAIO and established an **AI Coordination Committee (AICC)** across divisions to oversee AI use, develop governance procedures, and maintain an AI use case inventory
- Both agencies are developing enterprise-level AI workforce development plans and updating acceptable use policies for generative AI tools
- **No specific joint statement** addressing AI agent identity or non-human identity governance in banking has been issued as of March 2026. Existing guidance focuses on model risk management (SR 11-7 / OCC 2011-12) rather than AI agent identity

Source: [Federal Reserve AI Compliance Plan](https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/files/compliance-plan-for-omb-memorandum-m-25-21-202509.pdf), [FDIC AI Compliance Plan](https://www.fdic.gov/artificial-intelligence-ai-compliance-plan.pdf)

**NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF):**

Released January 2023, the NIST AI RMF is organized around **four core functions**:

1. **Govern** — cross-cutting function establishing risk management culture, organizational processes, and alignment with values across the full AI lifecycle
2. **Map** — context and risk identification across system lifecycle
3. **Measure** — performance and risk monitoring
4. **Manage** — mitigation and response

The Govern function is most relevant to agent identity: it requires organizations to define who is accountable for AI system decisions, establish governance structures, and ensure appropriate human oversight. However, the AI RMF does not specifically address non-human identity infrastructure or AI agent identity delegation.

**NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile):** Released July 2024, provides tailored risk management actions for generative AI. Relevant to agentic AI governance but does not address agent identity specifically.

**NIST SP 800-63-4 (Digital Identity Guidelines, Revision 4):**

- **Finalized July 2025** after ~6,000 public comments across two drafts
- Scope expanded to include subscriber-controlled wallets, syncable authenticators (passkeys), and controls for injection attacks and forged media
- The changelog notes that future revisions will "support expanding the scope to include device identity or machine-to-machine authentication" — but **Revision 4 does not itself establish non-human identity standards**
- The absence is notable: the most authoritative US digital identity standard does not yet cover machine or AI agent identity

Source: [NIST SP 800-63-4](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/63/4/2pd), [NIST AI RMF](https://nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework)

### India (RBI)

**RBI FREE-AI (Framework for Responsible and Ethical Enablement of AI):**

- The FREE-AI Committee report was released in **August 2025**, establishing India's first regulatory AI governance framework for financial services
- **Seven core principles ("Sutras"):** Trust as Foundation, People First, Innovation over Restraint, Fairness and Equity, Accountability, Understandable by Design, Safety/Resilience/Sustainability
- **Strategic pillars:** Innovation enablement (Infrastructure, Policy, Capacity) and Risk mitigation (Governance, Protection, Assurance)
- The RBI's Department of Technology includes an **AI Governance Policy framework** in its 2025–26 technology agenda for responsible AI/ML use by employees, vendors, and third parties

**RBI Managing Risks in Outsourcing Directions (November 2025):**

- Enhanced safeguards for cloud computing and SOC outsourcing, including governance, access controls, data lifecycle management, auditability, and exit planning
- Board-approved outsourcing policies with clear accountability
- **Compliance deadline:** April 2026 for existing arrangements
- Relevant to AI agent identity because banks outsourcing AI capabilities must maintain governance and audit rights

**AI agent identity specifically:** No direct RBI guidance on non-human identity or AI agent identity governance exists as of March 2026. The FREE-AI principles establish accountability norms but do not prescribe identity infrastructure.

Source: [KPMG/RBI FREE-AI Report](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/in/pdf/2025/08/rbi-free-ai-committee-report.pdf), [RBI Outsourcing Directions 2025](https://taxguru.in/rbi/rbi-all-india-financial-institutions-managing-risks-outsourcing-directions-2025.html)

### EU (EBA/ECB)

**EBA Assessment (2025):**

- The EBA mapped EU AI Act requirements against existing EU banking and payment legislation, finding **no significant contradictions** — the AI Act is complementary to existing sectoral rules
- The EBA concluded there is **no immediate need for new EBA-specific AI guidelines**, instead focusing on supervisory cooperation
- The European Commission is mandated to issue guidelines on high-risk AI classification by **2 February 2026**
- **92% of EU banks are actively deploying AI** (2025 EBA survey); 55% already using GPAI or agentic AI in consumer-facing applications
- Common applications: client/transaction profiling, customer support, fraud/AML detection, credit scoring, regulatory credit risk modelling

**ECB Supervisory Guide to Internal Models (July 2025):**

- Includes specific principles for machine learning techniques in internal models
- Sets expectations for model validation, backtesting, and governance when ML is used in risk models

**AI agent identity specifically:** Neither the EBA nor ECB has issued guidance specifically on AI agent identity, delegation, or non-human identity management in banking. The regulatory focus remains on model risk management and AI Act compliance rather than identity infrastructure.

Source: [EBA AI Act Implications](https://www.eba.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-11/AI%20Act%20implications%20for%20the%20EU%20banking%20sector.pdf), [EBA Rising Application of AI](https://www.eba.europa.eu/sites/default/files/2025-09/Rising%20application%20of%20AI%20in%20EU%20banking%20and%20payments%20sector.pdf)

### Canada (OSFI)

**Guideline E-23 — Model Risk Management:**

- Published **September 2025**, effective **1 May 2027** (18-month transition)
- Explicitly includes AI and machine learning within scope
- Principles-based approach requiring:
  - Enterprise-wide model risk management frameworks
  - Multi-disciplinary teams for model risk assessment
  - Clear governance at board, senior management, and operational levels
  - **Functional separation** between model developers, owners, and independent reviewers
  - Enterprise model inventories tracking all models with non-negligible risk
- Risk-based and proportionate approach tailored to institution size and complexity

**AI adoption in Canadian FIs:**
- ~70% of federally regulated FIs expected to use AI by 2026 (up from 50% in 2023)
- Key identified risks: business risk, legal risk, model risk, and data privacy
- OSFI's 2025–26 Annual Risk Outlook identifies AI as an emerging risk that may amplify fraud and financial crime

**AI agent identity specifically:** Guideline E-23 addresses model governance and accountability (developer vs. owner vs. reviewer separation) but does not address AI agent identity, delegation chains, or non-human identity infrastructure.

Source: [OSFI Guideline E-23](https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/guidance/guidance-library/draft-guideline-e-23-model-risk-management), [OSFI AI Risk Report](https://www.osfi-bsif.gc.ca/en/about-osfi/reports-publications/osfi-fcac-risk-report-ai-uses-risks-federally-regulated-financial-institutions)

## Vendor Landscape for Non-Human / Agent Identity

### Microsoft Entra Workload ID + Agent ID

**Product:** Microsoft Entra Workload ID (GA) + Microsoft Entra Agent ID (preview)

**What it covers:**
- Workload identities for apps, services, scripts, and containers
- Conditional Access policies for workload identities based on location and risk level
- Threat detection for compromised workload identities
- **Agent ID (preview, May 2025):** First major platform vendor to treat AI agents as first-class identities with their own identity constructs

**Agent ID specifics (Conditional Access for Agent Identities — preview, January 2026):**
- Agent identities: software entities performing agentic tasks and token acquisitions
- Agent users: non-human user identities for agent experiences requiring user accounts
- Conditional Access evaluates agent access requests similarly to human users and workload identities, but with agent-specific logic
- **Current limitations (intentionally minimal in preview):** Supports identity targeting and block access controls; does not yet support MFA, device compliance, location conditions, or session controls (because agents authenticate via machine-driven credential flows)
- Architecture: agent blueprints → agent identity blueprint principals → instantiated agent identities → agent users

**Roadmap:** Microsoft plans additional access management, security, and identity governance capabilities for Agent ID over the following six months from May 2025, including support for agents from Security Copilot, Microsoft 365 Copilot, and third-party solutions.

**Banking adoption:** Not specifically documented. Microsoft Entra is widely deployed in banking (Azure is a major banking cloud), making Agent ID a natural extension for Azure-native bank deployments. [unverified — specific banking adoption data needs manual confirmation]

**Assessment:** Microsoft is the **first hyperscaler to offer a dedicated agent identity construct**. The product is in preview with intentionally limited controls — it solves the "agents need their own identity" problem but does not yet solve delegation chains, human accountability binding, or cross-platform agent identity.

Source: [Microsoft Entra Agent ID](https://techcommunity.microsoft.com/blog/microsoft-entra-blog/announcing-microsoft-entra-agent-id-secure-and-manage-your-ai-agents/3827392), [Conditional Access for Agent Identities](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/entra/identity/conditional-access/agent-id)

### CyberArk + Venafi

**Deal:** CyberArk acquired Venafi for **$1.54B** ($1B cash + ~$540M in shares), completed **October 2024**. Venafi was previously owned by Thoma Bravo.

**Combined capabilities:**
- CyberArk: secrets management, privileged access management, identity security platform
- Venafi: certificate lifecycle management, PKI, IoT identity management, cryptographic code signing
- Combined: end-to-end machine identity security (certificates + secrets + privileged access) deployable as SaaS or hybrid

**Market data:**
- Machine identities outnumber human identities 40:1 to 45:1 in typical enterprises
- Acquisition expands CyberArk's TAM by ~$10B to ~$60B
- CyberArk's 2025 Machine Identity Security Report (1,200 security leaders surveyed):
  - **50%** experienced security incidents linked to compromised machine identities in the past year
  - **77%** acknowledge every undiscovered machine identity is a potential compromise point
  - **81%** view securing machine identities as vital for the future of AI
  - **72%** experienced at least one certificate-related outage in the past year
  - **67%** experience outages monthly; **45%** weekly

**What it does NOT cover:** CyberArk + Venafi addresses traditional machine identity (certificates, keys, secrets, service accounts). It does **not** address AI agent-specific identity challenges: delegation chains, dynamic authority, human accountability binding, or context-specific permissions. The platform manages *what machines are* (authentication), not *what agents are authorized to decide* (authorization + accountability).

Source: [CyberArk/Venafi Acquisition](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20240930990953/en/), [CyberArk 2025 Report](https://www.cyberark.com/press/new-cyberark-research-rapid-growth-of-machine-identities/)

### HashiCorp Vault (IBM)

**Deal:** IBM acquired HashiCorp for **$6.4B** ($35/share, 42.6% premium), announced April 2024, completed **February 2025**.

**Key capabilities:**
- **HashiCorp Vault:** Secrets management and identity-based security for zero-trust architecture across hybrid cloud
- **Terraform:** Infrastructure-as-code provisioning across clouds
- Integration with IBM Red Hat OpenShift (secrets management) and Red Hat Ansible (infrastructure automation)

**Strategic context:**
- ~75% of enterprises now use hybrid cloud
- GenAI expected to drive creation of 1 billion new cloud-native apps by 2028
- IBM positions the acquisition as creating "the industry's most comprehensive hybrid cloud platform"

**What it covers for agent identity:** Vault manages secrets (API keys, tokens, passwords, certificates) with dynamic secrets, lease-based revocation, and audit logging. Relevant to agent identity as the **secrets backend** — agents need credentials, and Vault can manage their lifecycle. However, Vault manages *credentials*, not *identity* in the broader governance sense (delegation, authority, accountability).

Source: [IBM/HashiCorp Acquisition](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/ibm-completes-acquisition-of-hashicorp-302387460.html)

### SPIFFE/SPIRE (CNCF)

**What it is:** SPIFFE (Secure Production Identity Framework For Everyone) is an open specification providing cryptographic identities to workloads in distributed systems. SPIRE (SPIFFE Runtime Environment) is the reference implementation.

**Status:** **CNCF Graduated project** (August 2022). Community: ~5,000 contributors, ~1,900 contributing organizations, health score of 74 (Healthy), $19.4M estimated software value.

**Adopters:** Bloomberg, ByteDance, Netflix, Niantic, Pinterest, Uber, GitHub, Anthem. Integrated with Envoy, gRPC, Istio, Kubernetes, Sigstore, Tekton.

**What it solves:** Cryptographic workload identity in cloud-native environments — every workload gets a verifiable identity (SVID — SPIFFE Verifiable Identity Document) without requiring secrets distribution. Enables zero-trust networking between services.

**Relevance to AI agent identity:** SPIFFE provides the **identity substrate** — a standardized way to give workloads (including AI agent runtime processes) verifiable identities. But it addresses workload authentication, not the agent-specific layer of delegation, authority boundaries, or human accountability chains.

Source: [SPIFFE](https://spiffe.io/), [CNCF Graduation](https://www.cncf.io/announcements/2022/09/20/spiffe-and-spire-projects-graduate-from-cloud-native-computing-foundation-incubator/)

## Standards Body Activity

### W3C Verifiable Credentials

**Status:** W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 achieved **W3C Recommendation** (highest standardization level) on **15 May 2025**.

**Published specifications (seven W3C Recommendations):**
- Verifiable Credentials Data Model v2.0
- Data Integrity specifications (ECDSA and EdDSA cryptosuites)
- Securing Verifiable Credentials using JOSE and COSE
- Controlled Identifiers v1.0
- Bitstring Status List v1.0

**Relevance to agent identity:**
- The VC standard supports a flexible three-party model (issuer, holder, verifier) that can apply to AI agents: an organization (issuer) could issue a verifiable credential to an AI agent (holder) attesting its authority, which a receiving system (verifier) can cryptographically validate
- W3C explicitly lists "Smart Things identities" and "Organization identities" among supported use cases — both relevant to agent identity
- The Controlled Identifiers specification enables managing cryptographic material and service endpoints for non-human entities
- **Gap:** The VC standard provides the credential format but does not define agent-specific semantics — there is no standard vocabulary for "this agent has been delegated authority X by human Y with constraint Z"

Source: [W3C VC 2.0 Recommendation](https://www.w3.org/news/2025/the-verifiable-credentials-2-0-family-of-specifications-is-now-a-w3c-recommendation/)

### OpenID Foundation

**OpenID for Verifiable Credentials (OID4VC):**

- **OpenID4VCI 1.0** (Verifiable Credential Issuance) — Final status, September 2025
- **OpenID4VP 1.0** (Verifiable Presentations) — Finalized
- **HAIP 1.0** (High Assurance Interoperability Profile) — Final membership vote December 2025
- **Self-certification launch: 26 February 2026** — enables wallet, issuer, and verifier developers across 38 jurisdictions to self-certify implementations
- Interop testing results: 98% passing rate (OpenID4VP + HAIP), 82% passing rate (OpenID4VCI)
- Referenced in EU Digital Identity (EUDI) Wallet project and draft ISO standards

**Agentic AI Identity Management (critical document):**

The OpenID Foundation released a whitepaper in **October 2025**: *"Identity Management for Agentic AI: The new frontier of authorization, authentication, and security for an AI agent world."* Key findings:

- Existing frameworks (OAuth 2.1, MCP) work for simple agent scenarios within single trust domains
- **Critical unsolved problems:**
  - Agent identity delegation — agents act indistinguishably from users, creating accountability gaps
  - Agent identity fragmentation — proprietary systems risk market fragmentation
  - Scalability of consent — users will face thousands of authorization requests from agents
  - Recursive delegation — agents spawning sub-agents with cascading authority
- The Foundation established an **Artificial Intelligence Identity Management Community Group** to work on standards
- The Foundation has **responded to NIST** on AI agent security, signaling standards-body coordination

**Assessment:** The OpenID Foundation whitepaper is the **most authoritative single document** on the AI agent identity gap as of March 2026. It explicitly names the problems that existing machine identity vendors do not solve.

Source: [OpenID Agentic AI Whitepaper](https://openid.net/new-whitepaper-tackles-ai-agent-identity-challenges/), [OID4VCI 1.0](https://openid.net/specs/openid-4-verifiable-credential-issuance-1_0-final.html), [Self-Certification](https://openid.net/openid-for-verifiable-credential-self-certification-to-launch-feb-2026/)

### IETF OAuth Working Group

**Transaction Tokens (Txn-Tokens):**
- Core specification: **draft-ietf-oauth-transaction-tokens-06** (July 2025) — short-lived, signed JWTs that preserve identity and authorization context across distributed service call chains
- **Agent extension: draft-oauth-transaction-tokens-for-agents-04** (February 2026) — extends Txn-Tokens with two new context fields:
  - `actor` — identifies the AI agent performing the action
  - `principal` — identifies the human or system entity that initiated the agent's action (optional for fully autonomous agents)
- Status: **Individual draft** (not yet adopted as working group item or published as RFC)
- The extension explicitly addresses zero-trust authorization challenges for AI agent workloads

**Assessment:** The IETF Transaction Tokens for Agents draft is the **closest thing to a formal protocol specification** for AI agent identity delegation as of March 2026. It directly addresses the actor/principal separation that banking compliance requires. However, it is early-stage (individual draft, not RFC).

Source: [IETF Txn-Tokens for Agents](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-oauth-transaction-tokens-for-agents/), [IETF Txn-Tokens Core](https://www.ietf.org/archive/id/draft-ietf-oauth-transaction-tokens-05.html)

### Agent Protocols (A2A, MCP)

**Google Agent-to-Agent Protocol (A2A):**
- Version 0.1.0, supported by 50+ technology partners (including Anthropic, Cohere, Langchain, Salesforce, ServiceNow)
- Enables peer-to-peer agent collaboration with dynamic, stateful, multi-turn interactions
- Built on HTTP, JSON-RPC, and Server-Sent Events
- **Secure by default** — enterprise-grade authentication and authorization is a design principle
- **Opaque execution** — agents collaborate without sharing internal memory or proprietary logic
- Defines: Agent Cards (metadata/capabilities), Tasks (units of work with unique IDs), Messages, Parts

**Anthropic Model Context Protocol (MCP):**
- Standardizes how agents connect to tools, APIs, and external resources
- Structured inputs/outputs for tool invocation
- Complementary to A2A: MCP for tools, A2A for agent-to-agent communication

**Identity implications:**
- A2A's Agent Card concept provides a structured way to describe agent capabilities and identity metadata — a building block for agent identity
- Neither A2A nor MCP currently defines delegation authority, accountability chains, or human oversight requirements
- Both protocols assume an identity layer exists but do not specify it — this is the gap that IETF Txn-Tokens for Agents and OpenID's agentic AI work aim to fill

Source: [Google A2A Protocol](https://google.github.io/A2A/), [A2A and MCP Comparison](https://google.github.io/A2A/topics/a2a-and-mcp/)

## The Critical Gap: Machine Identity ≠ Agent Identity

### What Existing Solutions Cover

| Capability | Machine Identity (CyberArk, Vault, SPIFFE) | AI Agent Identity (What Banks Need) |
|---|---|---|
| **Authentication** | Certificates, keys, secrets, tokens | All of this + agent-specific credentials |
| **Lifecycle** | Certificate rotation, secret expiry | Dynamic creation/destruction of ephemeral agents |
| **Behavior model** | Static, deterministic, repeatable | Non-deterministic, autonomous, adaptive |
| **Delegation** | Fixed service-to-service trust | Human → agent → sub-agent delegation chains |
| **Authority scope** | All-or-nothing (has access / doesn't) | Context-specific, time-bounded, task-scoped |
| **Accountability** | Action attributable to a workload | Action attributable to a *human decision-maker* |
| **Session model** | Long-lived, persistent | Ephemeral, seconds to hours |
| **Cross-domain** | Within organizational boundary | Across organizational boundaries (multi-party) |
| **Audit trail** | What happened | What happened + *why* + *who authorized it* |
| **Revocation** | Certificate/key revocation | Real-time authority withdrawal across all instances |

### What Machine Identity Gets Right

Machine identity vendors (CyberArk + Venafi, HashiCorp Vault, SPIFFE) solve a **real and necessary** problem: the cryptographic authentication of workloads, certificate lifecycle management, and secrets management. This is the **infrastructure substrate** on which agent identity must be built. No agent identity solution can exist without a functioning machine identity layer.

### What Is Specifically Missing for AI Agents in Banking

**1. Delegation chains with accountability:**
Banking regulators (EU AI Act Article 12, Article 26) require that AI decisions be traceable to a responsible natural person. Current machine identity systems authenticate *what* is making an API call, but not *who authorized that agent to make decisions* or *what boundaries constrain its authority*. A credit-scoring AI agent must prove not only its workload identity but also which human analyst delegated scoring authority, under what policy, with what constraints.

**2. Dynamic, context-specific authority:**
Machine identities have static permissions (RBAC or certificate-scoped). AI agents need permissions that vary by context: an agent might have authority to approve a $10K loan in one context but not a $100K loan; authority during business hours but not overnight; authority over retail clients but not corporate clients. No current vendor provides this granularity for non-human identities.

**3. Human oversight binding:**
EU AI Act Article 14 requires human oversight proportionate to system autonomy. This means the identity system must enforce that a human is "in the loop" before certain agent actions — not just log that actions occurred. The oversight person's identity must be bound to the agent's authority in real-time, not just in post-hoc audit.

**4. Agent-to-agent authority propagation:**
When Agent A delegates a sub-task to Agent B, how does Agent B inherit (and constrain) Agent A's authority? How does the receiving system verify the chain? Machine identity systems have no concept of transitive delegation. The IETF Transaction Tokens for Agents draft (actor + principal fields) is the first formal attempt to address this.

**5. Intent and reasoning attribution:**
Banking compliance requires not just *what* decision was made and *who* is responsible, but *why* the decision was made. AI agent identity must include the ability to associate reasoning traces with decisions — something entirely absent from the machine identity layer.

**6. Cross-organizational agent identity:**
When a bank's AI agent interacts with a counterparty's AI agent (e.g., in syndicated lending, trade settlement, or regulatory reporting), how do both parties verify the other's agent identity, authority, and constraints? No standard exists. A2A protocol provides the communication framework, but the identity and trust framework is undefined.

### The ISACA Authorization Crisis Framing

ISACA has explicitly labeled this the **"looming authorization crisis"**: traditional IAM (OAuth 2.0, OpenID Connect, SAML) was designed for human users and static service accounts with predictable behavior and single authenticated principals. Agentic AI violates every one of these assumptions.

Seven critical identity management gaps for agentic AI (ISACA):
1. Global logout/revocation complexity — each system maintains its own session state
2. Impersonation risks when agents use user credentials
3. Inability to distinguish AI decisions from human actions in audit trails
4. Lack of "why" traceability (not just "who" and "what")
5. EU AI Act Article 14 non-compliance for human oversight
6. Absence of policy-based governance at scale
7. Cross-organizational federation challenges

Source: [ISACA — The Looming Authorization Crisis](https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/industry-news/2025/the-looming-authorization-crisis-why-traditional-iam-fails-agentic-ai)

## Key Findings

1. **The EU AI Act creates implicit identity infrastructure requirements for banking AI** — Articles 12, 13, 14, and 26 require traceability to natural persons, human oversight proportionate to autonomy, and immutable audit trails. No banking AI system can be compliant without solving the agent identity problem.

2. **No regulator has explicitly addressed AI agent identity** — The EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF, EBA, ECB, OSFI, and RBI all establish accountability and governance requirements, but none prescribe identity infrastructure for AI agents specifically. This is a gap that banks must fill themselves.

3. **Microsoft Entra Agent ID is the only shipping product that treats AI agents as first-class identities** — but it is in preview with limited controls and addresses single-tenant scenarios. No vendor offers a complete agent identity solution.

4. **Machine identity vendors (CyberArk, HashiCorp, SPIFFE) solve a different problem** — they provide the necessary cryptographic substrate but do not address delegation, authority boundaries, human accountability binding, or context-specific permissions.

5. **Standards are emerging but not ready** — The IETF Transaction Tokens for Agents draft (actor/principal separation), the OpenID Foundation's Agentic AI whitepaper, and W3C Verifiable Credentials 2.0 provide building blocks, but no complete standard for agent identity exists.

6. **Enterprise readiness is dangerously low** — Only 18% of security leaders are confident their IAM can manage agent identities; only 21% have real-time agent inventories; 84% doubt they could pass a compliance audit on agent behavior.

7. **Banking AI adoption is running ahead of identity infrastructure** — 92% of EU banks are deploying AI, 55% using agentic AI in consumer applications, yet no bank has a complete agent identity governance framework. The gap between deployment velocity and identity readiness is the primary compliance risk for Aug 2026 EU AI Act high-risk obligations.

8. **The 2026–2027 window is critical** — EU AI Act high-risk obligations take effect Aug 2026, OSFI Guideline E-23 takes effect May 2027, and RBI outsourcing directions take effect April 2026. Banks deploying agentic AI must solve identity governance within this window or face regulatory action.

9. **Gartner predicts >40% of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by end 2027** — inadequate risk controls is cited as a primary reason. Identity governance is a risk control. Banks that solve agent identity first gain competitive advantage in sustaining AI deployments that others will have to cancel.

10. **The OpenID Foundation whitepaper (Oct 2025) is the most authoritative framing of the problem** — it names the specific gaps (delegation, fragmentation, consent scalability, recursive delegation) and calls for standards-body acceleration.

## Gaps and Unresolved Questions

1. **No standard for AI agent identity delegation chains** — The IETF draft is early-stage (individual draft, not RFC). OpenID Foundation has formed a community group but has not published a specification. W3C VC provides the credential format but not agent-specific semantics.

2. **No regulatory guidance on what "AI agent identity" means** — Regulators require accountability and traceability, but none define how AI agents should be identified, credentialed, or governed. Banks are building bespoke solutions without regulatory safe harbors.

3. **EBA high-risk classification guidance (due Feb 2026) may expand banking scope** — If the Commission guidelines classify additional banking AI use cases as high-risk (e.g., AML monitoring, fraud detection), the identity infrastructure burden increases significantly.

4. **Cross-border agent identity interoperability is unaddressed** — A bank operating under EU AI Act, US OCC guidance, RBI FREE-AI, and OSFI E-23 simultaneously has no way to maintain consistent agent identity governance across jurisdictions.

5. **NIST SP 800-63-4 deferred machine identity to future revisions** — The most authoritative US digital identity standard explicitly punted non-human identity. Until NIST addresses this, there is no US federal standard for machine or agent identity.

6. **DIF agent identity work is absent or invisible** — The Decentralized Identity Foundation, which would be a natural home for open agent identity standards, has no visible work stream on this topic.

7. **The relationship between AI agent identity and AI model identity is undefined** — Is the agent's identity tied to the model it runs (GPT-4, Claude), the deployment instance, the orchestration framework, or the business process it serves? This question has no consensus answer.

8. **Sub-agent and multi-agent orchestration identity is entirely unsolved** — When an agent spawns sub-agents (common in agentic AI frameworks), how do delegation chains, authority constraints, and accountability propagate? No protocol, product, or standard addresses this beyond the IETF draft's actor/principal fields.

9. **Human oversight scalability** — EU AI Act requires human oversight proportionate to autonomy, but banks deploying hundreds of agents cannot have a human "in the loop" for every decision. How should "proportionate" oversight be operationalized in identity infrastructure?

10. **Audit trail completeness** — Article 12 requires logging that identifies personnel involved in result verification, but agentic AI may produce thousands of decisions per hour. What level of granularity satisfies the logging requirement without creating an unmanageable data volume?
