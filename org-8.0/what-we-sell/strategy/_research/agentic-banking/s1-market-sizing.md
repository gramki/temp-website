## 6. TAM Construction: Agentic Banking (Vendor-Addressable)

### 6A. Methodology

The TAM is constructed bottom-up from three converging approaches, then cross-validated against top-down envelope constraints.

**Approach A — Direct category extrapolation:** Use the emerging "AI agents in financial services" category and isolate the banking/customer-facing portion.

**Approach B — Adjacent category overlay:** Sum the customer-facing agentic share across conversational AI, banking automation, and servicing technology replacement pools.

**Approach C — Top-down share of total banking AI:** Apply customer-facing agentic penetration rates to total banking AI spending.

### 6B. Approach A — Direct Category (AI Agents in Financial Services)

```
Grand View Research: AI agents in financial services 2030 = USD 4,485.5M (~USD 4.5B)
Banking share (excl. insurance, asset mgmt) ≈ 55–60%
  → Banking AI agents 2030 = USD 2.5–2.7B

Mordor Intelligence: Agentic AI in financial services 2031 = USD 43.52B
Commercial banks share = 45.6%
  → Banking agentic AI 2031 = USD 19.8B
  → Back-calculated to 2030 at 41% CAGR ≈ USD 14.1B

NOTE: Mordor's scope is broader (includes all agentic AI applications
      in banking, not just customer-facing). GVR's narrower definition
      of "AI agents" is closer to vendor-addressable platform spend.

Customer-facing share of banking agentic AI ≈ 40–50%
  GVR: USD 2.5–2.7B × customer-facing = already narrow ≈ USD 2.5–2.7B
  Mordor: USD 14.1B × 40–50% = USD 5.6–7.0B

RANGE A: USD 2.5–7.0B by 2030
(GVR conservative, Mordor aggressive)
```

### 6C. Approach B — Adjacent Category Overlay

```
1. Conversational AI/Contact center AI for banking (2030)
   Banking contact center AI ≈ USD 1.1–1.4B
   [from Section 1A derived estimate]

2. Banking automation — customer-facing share (2030–2031)
   Customer-facing automation ≈ USD 2.1–2.8B
   [from Section 1B derived estimate]

3. Banking servicing tech displacement (2030)
   CRM/servicing stack displacement ≈ USD 4.9–8.2B
   [from Section 1D derived estimate]
   Agentic share of this displacement ≈ 30–40%
   → USD 1.5–3.3B

4. Enterprise agent platform — banking share (2030)
   Banking share of platform market ≈ USD 2.0–2.5B
   [from Section 1C derived estimate]

Sum (avoiding double-counting by taking MAX of overlapping categories):
  Contact center AI + Automation + Platform ≈ USD 2.0–2.8B
  (These overlap significantly — an agent platform subsumes
   both the contact center AI and automation tool layers)

  + Servicing tech displacement (additive) ≈ USD 1.5–3.3B

RANGE B: USD 3.5–6.1B by 2030
```

### 6D. Approach C — Top-Down (Share of Total Banking AI)

```
Total AI in banking 2030 = USD 143.56B (Grand View Research)

Customer-facing share ≈ 30% (front-office 43% × ~70% customer-facing)
  → USD 43B

Agentic share of customer-facing AI ≈ 15–25% by 2030
  (BCG: agentic = 17% of AI value in 2025, rising to 29% by 2028)
  → USD 6.5–10.8B

RANGE C: USD 6.5–10.8B by 2030
```

### 6E. TAM Synthesis

| Approach | Conservative | Aggressive | Midpoint |
|----------|-------------|------------|----------|
| A: Direct category | USD 2.5B | USD 7.0B | USD 4.8B |
| B: Adjacent overlay | USD 3.5B | USD 6.1B | USD 4.8B |
| C: Top-down share | USD 6.5B | USD 10.8B | USD 8.6B |

**Convergence:** Approaches A and B converge at ~USD 4.5–5.0B midpoint. Approach C is higher because it includes bank-built/in-house AI that is not vendor-addressable.

#### Vendor-Addressable TAM (VTAM)

```
  Conservative VTAM (2030): USD 3.5B
  Base case VTAM (2030):    USD 5.0–6.0B
  Aggressive VTAM (2030):   USD 8.0–10.0B
```

The base case aligns with the convergence of direct category and overlay approaches. The aggressive case assumes faster-than-expected agentic adoption and includes partial servicing tech displacement.

## 7. Key Findings

### Market size
- **The vendor-addressable TAM for agentic banking is approximately USD 5–6B by 2030** (base case), growing from ~USD 200M–1.4B in 2025 — a 25–45% CAGR depending on scope definition.
- The total economic value including in-house builds and cost displacement could be USD 10–25B, but the vendor-addressable portion is substantially smaller.
- **AI agents in financial services** is emerging as a distinct analyst category (Grand View Research, Mordor Intelligence both publish dedicated reports as of 2024–2025).

### Adoption
- **70% of banking institutions are deploying or actively exploring agentic AI** (MIT Tech Review/EY, 2025), split: 16% deployed, 52% piloting.
- **44% of finance teams are using agentic AI in 2026**, up 600%+ from 2025 (Azilen/industry composite).
- Autonomous agents achieve **75–90% resolution rates** vs. 20–35% for chatbots — a 3–4x improvement that justifies the premium over conversational AI.

### Economics
- Early adopters report **USD 3M cost savings per use case**, **40–60% reduction in average handle time**, and **30x faster time-to-serve**.
- McKinsey projects **15–20% net cost base reduction** for banks that fully adopt AI, with certain categories seeing up to 70% cuts.
- However, McKinsey also warns of **9% average profit pool shrinkage** as benefits are competed away — the adoption imperative is defensive as much as offensive.

### Geography
- **USA/North America** leads with 35–40% market share and the most advanced deployments (JPMorgan, BofA, Capital One).
- **India** is the fastest-growing market (35%+ CAGR) with USD 80B GenAI value-add potential to financial services by 2030.
- **UK/EU** is investing heavily (USD 20.9B European banking AI by 2028) but is constrained by regulatory fragmentation.

### Build vs. Buy
- **Tier 1 builds, Tier 3 buys.** The vendor opportunity is concentrated in Tier 2 and Tier 3, which represent ~60–70% of institutions but only ~30–35% of spend.
- 90% of sellers plan to use AI agents in CRM by 2027 (Salesforce data) — platform vendors are embedding agents aggressively.

## 8. Gaps and Unresolved Questions

### Data gaps
1. **Sub-segment breakdown (consumer vs. business vs. wealth):** No analyst reports this split for agentic AI specifically. Our estimates are constructed from banking revenue pool distributions and are directional only.
2. **Tier 2/Tier 3 technology spending:** Celent's Dimensions reports contain this data but are paywalled. We cite the headline growth rates but not the tier-specific breakdowns. `[unverified — needs manual confirmation via Celent subscription]`
3. **Dispute resolution automation market:** No analyst tracks this as a standalone category. It is embedded within banking operations automation and customer servicing technology.
4. **Capital One AI spending:** Specific figures not found in public sources. Capital One is known as a tech-forward bank but does not disclose AI-specific budget breakdowns.
5. **India agentic AI specifically:** Market data exists for India AI in BFSI broadly but not for the agentic sub-segment. The USD 0.3–0.4B estimate is constructed.

### Methodological caveats
1. **Double-counting risk:** The five adjacent categories overlap substantially. A single agentic banking platform could be counted in conversational AI, contact center AI, banking automation, CRM/servicing tech, and enterprise AI agents simultaneously. The TAM construction uses MAX-of-overlapping rather than sum to mitigate this.
2. **Definition variance:** "Agentic" means different things across reports. Mordor Intelligence includes any AI with autonomous decision-making; Grand View Research's "AI agents" category is narrower. This explains the 3–4x range between conservative and aggressive estimates.
3. **CAGR divergence:** Forecast CAGRs range from 23% (contact center AI) to 46% (enterprise agentic AI). The variance reflects different base years, scope definitions, and analyst methodologies.
4. **Vendor-addressable vs. total economic value:** The TAM construction focuses on vendor-addressable spend (software licenses, platform fees, SaaS subscriptions, professional services). In-house builds by Tier 1 banks and cost displacement value are excluded.

### Questions requiring further investigation
1. What is the implied price point per agent/per interaction that underpins the platform TAM? Is it per-seat, per-resolution, per-API-call?
2. How much of the Tier 1 build market eventually converts to buy (platform consolidation cycle)?
3. What is the regulatory ceiling for autonomous agent action in different jurisdictions? (Impacts scope of "agentic" in the TAM)
4. How does the wealth/family banking segment differ in willingness-to-pay vs. volume? (Low volume, high value per interaction — may justify higher agent pricing)
