## Key Findings

### AI in Customer Engagement

- **Next-best-action is proven but unevenly adopted.** Pega CDH at NatWest drives 60% of all sales from AI-prompted NBAs across 3.6B interactions/year. BCG data shows only 5% of firms generate value at scale from AI, while 60% see no results — indicating a massive execution gap between leaders and the field.

- **Personetics is the dominant banking-specific engagement AI vendor.** With 150M+ monthly users across 130+ FIs in 35 markets, Personetics has the deepest moat in banking-specific personalization. Key customers include U.S. Bank, Huntington, Ally, RBC, BMO, and Santander. The company's $178M in total funding (backed by Thoma Bravo, Warburg Pincus, Sequoia) signals institutional confidence. Revenue/ARR are not publicly disclosed.

- **Personalization is the top declared investment priority for banks.** KPMG's 2025 survey found 91% of banks cite data-driven insights and personalization as a top focus. Yet Cornerstone Advisors shows two-thirds of community FIs score below 50 on data execution quality for marketing — the gap between aspiration and operational reality is wide.

- **Consumer expectations are accelerating faster than bank capabilities.** Salesforce reports 65% of consumers expect AI to speed financial transactions (up from 46% in 2023). J.D. Power finds banking chatbots are "far less advanced than state-of-the-art models with which customers are increasingly accustomed," with virtual assistant satisfaction actually declining in 2025.

### AI in Servicing

- **BofA Erica is the gold standard for banking conversational AI.** 3.2B+ cumulative interactions, 20.6M users, 98%+ satisfaction rate, and 30B total digital interactions in 2025. Erica uses deterministic AI (not gen AI) for 100% accuracy on financial information. The expansion to employee-facing (90% adoption, 50% IT call reduction) and corporate (CashPro Chat) makes it the most comprehensive deployment in the industry.

- **Wells Fargo Fargo demonstrates privacy-first generative AI architecture.** 245M interactions in 2024 alone with zero PII exposure to LLMs — proving that consumer-facing gen AI can operate within regulatory constraints.

- **Agent assist delivers measurable productivity gains.** 44% of banking teams report 30%+ AHT reduction with AI voice bots. Microsoft Copilot delivers 12% AHT reduction. Beyond Bank achieved 13% AHT reduction with Genesys plus CSAT improvement from 89% to 92%. These are operational benefits that fund further AI investment.

- **Chatbot adoption is growing but trust remains limited.** Cornerstone reports 35% of community FIs have chatbots. Deloitte finds 37% of US bank customers have never interacted with a banking chatbot. Only 27% trust AI for financial advice. For disputes specifically, only 21% of cardholders have used chatbots, with 40% preferring live agents.

- **AI case routing is an emerging category with strong early results.** Spring Labs achieves 97% complaint identification accuracy (vs. 65–70% manual). Glia automates 34% of interactions without human help. This category is consolidating around compliance-aware classification engines.

### Regulatory Landscape

- **CFPB enforces a "no technology exception" to consumer protection.** The CFPB has explicitly stated there is no advanced-technology exception to fair lending and UDAAP obligations. Banks deploying AI chatbots must avoid "doom loops," recognize consumer rights, and provide accurate responses. For AI-driven credit decisions, lenders must provide specific adverse action reasons reflecting the actual model factors — template reasons are insufficient.

- **FCA treats Consumer Duty as the primary AI governance lens.** The FCA will not introduce AI-specific regulation; instead, existing Consumer Duty requirements apply. Banks must evidence that AI does not produce systematically poorer outcomes for vulnerable or protected-characteristic groups. The FCA AI Live Testing program (NatWest, Monzo, Santander among participants) enables supervised experimentation.

- **SR 11-7 applies to engagement AI that touches credit or pricing.** Where NBA or personalization models affect credit access, pricing, or product eligibility, SR 11-7 model risk management obligations apply — including explainability as part of development validation. GARP notes SR 11-7's foundational assumptions (static, bounded, reconstructible decisions) are strained by agentic AI's autonomous, probabilistic nature.

| Vendor | Governance Capabilities | Evidence |

### Vendors with Moderate / Emerging Governance

| Vendor | Governance Status | Gaps |
|---|---|---|
| **Salesforce Einstein** | Process Compliance Navigator; AI Trust Layer with data masking and toxicity detection; bias mitigation frameworks documented | Banking-specific explainability for credit-adjacent decisions is less mature than Pega/FICO; relies on customer implementation for audit completeness |
| **Adobe Journey Optimizer** | Structured content management with version history and sign-off workflows; compliance-by-design for marketing content | Not purpose-built for regulated financial decisioning; weaker on model-level explainability; governance is content/campaign-oriented, not decision-oriented |
| **Genesys** | Embedded guardrails in agentic virtual agent; policy compliance enforcement | Governance capabilities are newer (LAM-powered agents launched Feb 2026); limited track record in banking-specific audit requirements |

### Vendors with Notable Governance Gaps

| Vendor | Gap Description |
|---|---|
| **Kasisto** | No publicly documented explainability framework or audit trail capabilities for banking decisions. $90M total funding suggests resource constraints relative to governance investment required. Product focus is conversational quality, not decision governance. |
| **Generic LLM providers** (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google via direct integration) | No built-in financial services governance; banks must build explainability, audit, and bias mitigation layers themselves. Wells Fargo's Fargo demonstrates the architectural effort required (zero-PII-to-LLM pipeline). |
| **Martech platforms** (HubSpot, Braze, Iterable entering banking) | Not designed for regulated financial services; no SR 11-7 or UDAAP-aware governance; no adverse action or fair lending compliance tooling. |

### Competitive Implications

1. **Pega holds the strongest governance position** among engagement AI vendors, with the Wells Fargo explainability deployment as a proof point that satisfies both banking regulators and internal model risk teams.
2. **Personetics' governance is adequate for engagement insights** but will face pressure as its platform moves into agentic AI and credit-adjacent decisions via MCP Server integrations.
3. **The governance gap is a barrier to entry** for martech-origin vendors trying to serve regulated banking use cases — and an opportunity for banking-native vendors to defend premium pricing.
4. **FINOS AI Governance Framework** provides an emerging industry standard (tiered from Tier 0 to Tier 3) that could become the de facto benchmark for AI governance in financial services.

## Gaps and Unresolved Questions

1. **Personetics revenue/ARR unknown.** The company is private (Series D, $178M raised) and does not disclose revenue. Estimates place it in the $50M–$150M ARR range based on customer count and typical per-bank SaaS pricing, but this is speculative. `[unverified — needs manual confirmation]`

2. **NBA adoption rates by bank tier are not precisely quantified.** BCG's "5% at scale" is cross-industry. No banking-specific survey was found that breaks down real-time NBA vs. batch campaign adoption by Tier 1 / Tier 2 / community bank segments. Cornerstone's data on community FI chatbot adoption (35%) is the closest proxy.

3. **J.D. Power virtual assistant satisfaction decline lacks granularity.** The 2025 report notes declining satisfaction and utilization for banking virtual assistants but does not publish the specific scores or identify which banks are performing well vs. poorly. The full report is paywalled.

4. **CFPB enforcement status under current administration.** CFPB guidance on chatbots (2023) and AI credit decisions (2022, 2024, 2025) remains formally in effect. However, the political environment around the CFPB creates uncertainty about enforcement intensity. The "no technology exception" principle has not been rescinded, but active enforcement actions specifically targeting AI chatbots have not been publicly reported as of March 2026.

5. **SR 11-7 modernization timeline.** GARP and multiple industry commentators note SR 11-7's 2011 framework strains under agentic AI. No formal update or supplemental guidance from the Fed or OCC has been issued as of March 2026. Banks are self-interpreting how to apply SR 11-7 to AI engagement models. `[unverified — needs manual confirmation of any forthcoming guidance]`

6. **Adobe and Salesforce banking penetration for AI-driven engagement** (as distinct from CRM or marketing automation) is not well-documented by independent surveys. Both vendors claim strong financial services presence, but independent adoption data for AI-specific use cases (not just platform licenses) is lacking.

7. **Contact center AI ROI at scale for banking specifically.** Most productivity metrics (AHT reduction, containment rates) come from vendor case studies or cross-industry surveys. Banking-specific, independently verified productivity data across a representative sample of institutions remains sparse.

8. **Generational divide in chatbot trust.** Deloitte confirms Millennials/Gen Z are more positive; Boomers/Gen X less so. But no data quantifies what this means for bank-level NPS or attrition when chatbot-first strategies are deployed across the full customer base.

### Personetics Deep Dive
- Founded 2011, Israel-based, now global
- Product suite: Personetics Engage (flagship insights), Personetics ACT (automated financial wellness), Personetics Enrich (transaction enrichment), Engagement Builder (low-code), PrimacyEdge (wallet share growth)
- New (2025): Personetics Labs (GenAI tools including Insight Copilot Builder), MCP Server (agentic AI enablement)
- Key differentiator: banking-domain-specific AI trained on financial transaction data, not general-purpose LLMs
- Vulnerability: private company with limited R&D budget vs. Pega/Salesforce platform plays; MCP Server strategy depends on banks building agentic apps (unproven demand)
- Competitive moat: deep integration with core banking systems at 130+ FIs; 10+ years of financial transaction pattern data; high switching costs once embedded in digital banking UX
