## United States

### FFIEC Authentication Guidance (2021)

In August 2021, the FFIEC issued updated guidance on [Authentication and Access to Financial Institution Services and Systems](https://occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2021/bulletin-2021-36.html), replacing guidance from 2005 and 2011. The update was jointly issued by the OCC ([Bulletin 2021-36](https://occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2021/bulletin-2021-36.html)), FDIC ([FIL-21-055](https://fdic.gov/news/financial-institution-letters/2021/fil21055.html)), and Federal Reserve ([SR 21-14](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr2114.pdf)).

**Capability demanded:** Multi-factor authentication (MFA) or controls of equivalent strength for all users accessing financial institution services and systems — including customers, employees, board members, and third parties. Institutions must conduct periodic risk assessments and implement layered security based on the assessed risk of each access channel.

**Status:** Effective since August 2021; enforced through supervisory examinations. Not prescriptive on specific technologies — institutions have flexibility based on operational complexity and risk tolerance.

**Penalty:** No statutory penalty; enforcement via supervisory actions including Matters Requiring Attention (MRAs), formal enforcement actions, and consent orders.

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must deploy enterprise-wide MFA infrastructure covering customer-facing digital banking, employee access, and third-party/vendor access. This drives investment in authentication platforms, adaptive risk engines, and identity governance systems.

> Sources: [OCC Bulletin 2021-36](https://occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2021/bulletin-2021-36.html), [FDIC FIL-21-055](https://fdic.gov/news/financial-institution-letters/2021/fil21055.html), [FFIEC Press Release](https://www.ffiec.gov/news/press-releases/2021/pr-08-11)

### CCPA / CPRA

The [California Consumer Privacy Act](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?division=3.&part=4.&lawCode=CIV&title=1.81.5) (2018), as amended by the [California Privacy Rights Act](https://vig.cdn.sos.ca.gov/2020/general/pdf/topl-prop24.pdf) (2020, effective January 2023), establishes comprehensive consumer data privacy rights.

**Capabilities demanded:**
- Consent management with right to opt out of sale/sharing of personal information
- Right to deletion with orchestration across all downstream processors
- Right to know (data access and portability)
- Right to correct inaccurate personal information
- Opt-out preference signal processing (Global Privacy Control)
- Data minimization and purpose limitation (CPRA additions)

**Status:** In force. The California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) has active enforcement authority since July 2023, in addition to the Attorney General.

**Penalty data (2025 adjusted rates):**
- $2,663 per violation (general)
- $7,988 per intentional violation or violations involving consumers under 16
- $107–$799 per consumer per incident (statutory damages via private right of action)
- 30-day cure period eliminated under CPRA

**Recent enforcement actions:**
- **Disney — $2.75M** (Feb 2026): Largest CCPA settlement. Opt-out mechanisms only applied per-device, requiring consumers to opt out up to 10 times. Source: [CA AG Press Release](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/california-wont-let-it-go-attorney-general-bonta-announces-275-million)
- **PlayOn Sports — $1.1M** (Mar 2026): Forced users to agree to tracking without adequate opt-out. Source: [CPPA Announcement](https://privacy.ca.gov/2026/03/youth-sports-media-company-to-pay-1-1-million-fine-change-practices-over-privacy-violations/)
- **Data broker enforcement** (Jan 2026): Datamasters ($45K), S&P Global ($62.6K) for registration failures. Source: [CPPA Enforcement](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2026/20260108.html)

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must build consent management platforms that process GPC signals, orchestrate deletion across all systems and processors, maintain data inventories, and support data subject access requests at scale.

> Sources: [CPPA Penalty Announcement](https://privacy.ca.gov/2024/12/california-privacy-protection-agency-announces-2025-increases-for-ccpa-fines-and-penalties/), [CA AG Disney Settlement](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/california-wont-let-it-go-attorney-general-bonta-announces-275-million)

### State Privacy Law Patchwork

**Twenty US states** now have comprehensive privacy laws in effect as of January 2026, covering over 184 million residents (>50% of the US population).

**States with laws in force (by effective date):**

| Year | States |
|------|--------|
| 2020–2023 | California, Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, Utah |
| 2024 | Oregon, Texas, Montana, Delaware, Iowa, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Nebraska, Tennessee |
| 2025 | Maryland, Minnesota |
| Jan 2026 | Indiana, Kentucky, Rhode Island |

**Key variations that prevent unified compliance:**
- **Applicability thresholds:** Range from 35,000 consumers (Rhode Island) to 100,000+ consumers (Virginia model states)
- **Cure periods:** Some states (IN, KY) allow 30-day cure; others (RI) do not
- **Children's data:** CT and OR prohibit selling data of minors under 16 and restrict geolocation data sales
- **Enforcement:** All enforce via state AG; no state has a private right of action equivalent to California's
- **No new laws passed in 2025** — the regulatory posture has shifted from legislation to enforcement

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks operating nationally must build a unified privacy compliance engine that reconciles 20+ different consent, opt-out, data subject rights, and enforcement regimes. This is functionally a federal compliance burden without a federal standard.

> Sources: [MultiState — 2026 State Privacy Laws](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/2/4/all-of-the-comprehensive-privacy-laws-that-take-effect-in-2026), [JDSupra — 2026 Privacy](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/data-privacy-in-2026-state-enforcement-7930613/), [Foley & Lardner Comparison](https://www.foley.com/insights/publications/2026/01/us-state-consumer-data-privacy-laws/)

### FinCEN CDD / KYC Rules

The [FinCEN Customer Due Diligence Final Rule](https://www.fincen.gov/resources/statutes-and-regulations/cdd-final-rule) (31 CFR 1010, 1020, 1023, 1024, 1026), effective May 2018, established four core CDD requirements for covered financial institutions.

**Capabilities demanded:**
1. **Customer identity verification** — existing Bank Secrecy Act requirement made explicit
2. **Beneficial ownership identification and verification** — identify any individual owning ≥25% of a legal entity and any individual controlling the entity
3. **Customer risk profiling** — understand nature and purpose of customer relationships
4. **Ongoing monitoring** — detect and report suspicious transactions; maintain and update customer information on a risk basis

**Status:** In force since May 2018. The Corporate Transparency Act (2021) created a complementary FinCEN Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) registry; BOI reporting requirements have faced legal challenges (multiple court injunctions in 2024–2025) and remain in flux as of March 2026. [unverified — needs manual confirmation on current BOI reporting status]

**Penalty:** Civil money penalties for BSA violations (up to $100K–$1M per violation depending on severity); criminal penalties for willful violations (fines and imprisonment).

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must maintain robust KYC/AML platforms with beneficial ownership registries, automated screening, ongoing transaction monitoring, and risk-based customer information refresh capabilities.

> Sources: [FinCEN CDD Final Rule](https://www.fincen.gov/resources/statutes-and-regulations/cdd-final-rule), [Federal Register — CDD Rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2016/05/11/2016-10567/customer-due-diligence-requirements-for-financial-institutions)

### NIST SP 800-63-4 (Digital Identity Guidelines)

[NIST Special Publication 800-63 Revision 4](https://pages.nist.gov/800-63-4/) was published in final form in **July 2025** after nearly four years of development and ~6,000 public comments.

**Capabilities demanded (across four volumes):**
- **SP 800-63A-4 (Identity Proofing):** Restructured controls for identity proofing roles and types; expanded fraud requirements; controls for injection attacks and forged media (deepfakes); continuous evaluation metrics
- **SP 800-63B-4 (Authentication):** Integration of syncable authenticators (synced passkeys); phishing-resistant authentication; updated assurance levels
- **SP 800-63C-4 (Federation):** Subscriber-controlled wallets added to federation model; updated federation assurance levels
- **Cross-cutting:** AI/ML considerations for digital identity services; redress processes

**Status:** Final (July 2025). Implementation FAQs and conformance criteria forthcoming. While not regulatory, NIST 800-63 is referenced by federal agencies and increasingly by financial regulators as the identity assurance benchmark.

**Penalty:** N/A — guidance document, not regulation. However, federal agencies and contractors are bound to comply, and financial regulators reference it in examination guidance.

**Infrastructure impact:** Sets the technical standard for identity proofing (including remote proofing with deepfake resistance), modern authentication (passkeys, FIDO2), and federated identity. Banks adopting these standards need investment in identity verification platforms, biometric liveness detection, wallet-based credential management, and phishing-resistant authenticator infrastructure.

> Sources: [NIST — SP 800-63-4 Release](https://csrc.nist.gov/News/2025/nist-revises-digitial-identity-guidelines-sp-800-6), [NIST 800-63-4 Full Text](https://pages.nist.gov/800-63-4/)

### FTC Enforcement Actions on Identity and Data Breaches

The FTC's enforcement actions on data breaches and identity protection provide compelling evidence of escalating regulatory pressure on financial services firms.

**Recent actions demonstrating the enforcement trajectory:**

| Entity | Date | Issue | Outcome |
|--------|------|-------|---------|
| Marriott/Starwood | Oct 2024 | Three breaches affecting 344M+ customers (2014–2020) | Comprehensive security program mandate; $52M state penalty | 
| Illusory Systems (Nomad) | Dec 2025 | $186M data breach; inadequate security despite "security-first" marketing | Comprehensive infosec program; biennial third-party assessments |
| GM/OnStar | Jan 2026 | Collecting/selling geolocation data without consent | 5-year data sharing ban; 20-year consent requirement |
| Consumer refunds | Dec 2025 | Unauthorized billing schemes | $27.6M distributed to 1.2M consumers |

**Infrastructure impact:** The FTC's pattern demonstrates that "reasonable security" is not static — it requires continuous investment in security programs, breach detection and response, consent architectures, and third-party assessment readiness. The GM/OnStar action specifically establishes that affirmative consent is required before data collection and sharing — a consent infrastructure mandate.

> Sources: [FTC — Marriott Action](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/10/ftc-takes-action-against-marriott-starwood-over-multiple-data-breaches), [FTC — GM/OnStar Order](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/01/ftc-finalizes-order-settling-allegations-gm-onstar-collected-sold-geolocation-data-without-consumers)

### OCC / FDIC / Fed Joint Guidance — AI in Banking

Federal banking regulators oversee AI in financial institutions primarily through existing supervisory frameworks, most notably [SR 11-7 (Model Risk Management)](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm), issued jointly by the Federal Reserve and OCC in 2011.

**Capabilities demanded for AI/ML systems:**
- **Model development:** Explainability, data quality assessment, algorithm justification, hyperparameter documentation
- **Validation:** Conceptual soundness for ML approaches, adversarial robustness, reproducibility
- **Fair lending / bias testing:** Prohibited basis testing, proxy discrimination analysis, disparate impact assessment, accurate adverse action reasons (per CFPB guidance)
- **Ongoing monitoring:** Performance monitoring, data drift detection, concept drift monitoring, retraining triggers
- **Governance:** Board-level reporting for AI models; third-party AI model assessment

**Status:** SR 11-7 in force since 2011; applied to AI/ML through examination practice. The OCC issued [Bulletin 2025-26](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html) clarifying flexibility for community banks. As of February 2026, regulators are actively reassessing SR 11-7's applicability to **agentic AI systems** that are dynamic, probabilistic, and autonomous — the framework's assumptions of periodic review and stable model form are being tested.

**Implications for AI agent identity and accountability:** Agentic AI — systems that act autonomously on behalf of institutions or customers — creates novel identity and accountability questions that existing frameworks were not designed to address. The identity of an AI agent (who is acting, on whose authority, with what scope) becomes a trust infrastructure problem.

**Penalty:** Supervisory actions (MRAs, enforcement actions, consent orders).

> Sources: [SR 11-7](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm), [GARP — SR 11-7 and Agentic AI](https://www.garp.org/risk-intelligence/operational/sr-11-7-age-agentic-ai-260227), [OCC Bulletin 2025-26](https://www.occ.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2025/bulletin-2025-26.html)

### Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act — Safeguards Rule (Revised)

The [FTC Safeguards Rule](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-safeguards-rule-what-your-business-needs-know) (16 CFR Part 314), amended in October 2021 with key provisions effective June 2023, requires non-banking financial institutions to maintain comprehensive information security programs.

**Capabilities demanded (2021 amendments added specificity):**
- Designated qualified individual overseeing the security program
- Written risk assessment
- Access controls limiting who can access consumer data
- Data encryption (in transit and at rest)
- Multi-factor authentication
- Secure development practices
- Incident response plan
- Periodic penetration testing and vulnerability assessments
- **Breach notification to the FTC** (added by 2023 amendment; effective May 2024)

**Status:** In force. The FTC provided [additional guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/06/ftc-provides-guidance-updated-safeguards-rule) on the updated rule in June 2025.

**Scope:** Applies to "financial institutions" under GLBA — broadly defined to include mortgage lenders/brokers, payday lenders, finance companies, motor vehicle dealers, account servicers, check cashers, wire transferors, and similar entities. Banks are covered by OCC/FDIC/Fed equivalents (Interagency Guidelines).

**Penalty:** FTC enforcement actions; civil penalties; consent orders.

**Infrastructure impact:** Requires MFA deployment, encryption infrastructure, access governance, formal penetration testing programs, incident response capabilities, and FTC breach reporting infrastructure.

> Sources: [FTC Safeguards Rule](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/ftc-safeguards-rule-what-your-business-needs-know), [16 CFR Part 314](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/federal-register-notices/16-cfr-part-314-standards-safeguarding-customer-information)

## India

### Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act 2023

The [DPDP Act 2023](https://www.meity.gov.in/writereaddata/files/Digital%20Personal%20Data%20Protection%20Act%202023.pdf) is India's first comprehensive data protection law. The [DPDP Rules 2025](https://www.cadp.in/resources/guides/dpdp-rules-2025/) were notified on November 13, 2025, operationalizing the Act's provisions.

**Capabilities demanded:**
- Verifiable consent from data principals before processing personal data
- Consent Manager infrastructure — registered entities providing interoperable consent management platforms (minimum ₹2 crore net worth requirement)
- Data principal rights: access, correction, erasure, grievance redressal
- Verifiable parental consent for processing children's data
- Breach notification to the Data Protection Board and affected data principals
- Security safeguards proportionate to the risk
- Cross-border data transfer restrictions (pending notification of restricted jurisdictions)

**Implementation timeline (phased):**

| Phase | Date | Obligations |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Phase 1 | November 13, 2025 | Foundation — Data Protection Board setup, key definitions |
| Phase 2 | November 13, 2026 | Consent Manager registration and infrastructure development |
| Phase 3 | May 13, 2027 | Full compliance — breach notification, security safeguards, cross-border transfers, data principal rights |

**Current status:** Of 40 total obligations in the Rules, 26 have been notified; 14 remain pending — including cross-border transfer restrictions and Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF) designations.

**Penalties:**

| Violation | Maximum Penalty |
|-----------|----------------|
| Failure to maintain reasonable security safeguards | ₹250 crore (~$30M) |
| Failure to notify breach / children's data breach | ₹200 crore (~$24M) |
| Breach of Significant Data Fiduciary obligations | ₹150 crore (~$18M) |
| Breach of consent or other provisions | ₹50 crore (~$6M) |

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must build consent management infrastructure that is interoperable with registered Consent Managers, implement data principal rights portals, develop breach detection and notification systems, and ensure security safeguards across all personal data processing. The phased timeline allows preparation but the obligations are substantial.

> Sources: [CADP — DPDP Implementation Tracker](https://cadp.in/resources/guides/dpdp-implementation-tracker/), [CADP — DPDP Rules 2025](https://www.cadp.in/resources/guides/dpdp-rules-2025/), [IndiaLaw — Timelines & Penalties](https://www.indialaw.in/blog/data-privacy/digital-personal-data-protection-act-2025-timelines-penalties/)

### RBI KYC Master Direction (KYC Directions, 2025)

The RBI has consolidated its KYC framework under the [KYC Directions, 2025](https://www.rbi.org.in/ScriptS/BS_ViewMasDirections.aspx?id=10292), superseding earlier KYC Master Directions. Amended directives issued June 12, 2025, address periodic KYC updation.

**Capabilities demanded:**
- **Video KYC (V-CIP):** Live video interaction for customer identification; session recording; real-time document verification; recognized as a primary onboarding method and for periodic KYC updation
- **Aadhaar eKYC:** Biometric-based electronic KYC authentication for face-to-face onboarding; self-declaration for address discrepancies with UIDAI records
- **Periodic KYC updation:** Banks must conduct regular KYC refresh on a risk basis; Banking Correspondents can now facilitate KYC updation; intensive camps mandated for rural/semi-urban areas
- **CKYCR integration:** KYC records must be updated incrementally to the Central KYC Records Registry; additional customer information furnished within seven days

**Status:** In force; June 2025 amendments specifically addressed large pendency in periodic KYC updation for DBT/EBT and PMJDY accounts.

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must invest in V-CIP platforms with live video, document verification, and session recording; Aadhaar biometric integration (fingerprint, iris, face); CKYCR connectivity for centralized KYC records; and mobile/field-based KYC updation tools for Banking Correspondents.

> Sources: [RBI KYC Directions](https://www.rbi.org.in/ScriptS/BS_ViewMasDirections.aspx?id=10292), [HyperVerge — RBI Video KYC Guidelines 2026](https://hyperverge.co/blog/rbi-video-kyc-guidelines/), [RBI Notification](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12746&Mode=0)

### RBI Master Direction on Digital Payment Security Controls (2021)

The RBI issued the [Master Direction on Digital Payment Security Controls](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12032&Mode=0) in February 2021, applicable to scheduled commercial banks (excluding RRBs), small finance banks, and payment banks.

**Capabilities demanded:**
- Board-approved digital payment security policy
- Multi-tier application security architecture
- End-to-end encryption for sensitive data
- Device binding and secure element management
- Transaction risk scoring and anomaly detection
- Customer notification for all digital payment transactions
- Incident response and forensic readiness

**Status:** In force since February 2021. Further updated by the [Authentication Mechanisms for Digital Payment Transactions Directions, 2025](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12746&Mode=0), with full compliance required by **April 1, 2026**.

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must maintain multi-layered authentication infrastructure, real-time transaction risk scoring engines, device management systems, and incident response capabilities.

**Cross-reference note:** The existing payments infrastructure research (`market-study/regulatory-landscape-payments-infrastructure.md`) covers the April 2026 Authentication Mechanisms deadline in Section 2.4 under "Authentication Mechanisms Directions."

> Sources: [RBI — Digital Payment Security Controls](https://www.rbi.org.in/Scripts/NotificationUser.aspx?Id=12032&Mode=0)

### Account Aggregator Framework

India's Account Aggregator (AA) framework enables consent-based financial data sharing between regulated entities through licensed Account Aggregators.

**Scale (FY25 data):**
- **2.84 lakh daily consents** (78.6% YoY growth from FY24)
- **11.58 crore consent-based data transactions** in FY25
- **4.77 crore users** accessed financial products through AA
- **₹887 billion** in loan disbursements via AA (out of ₹1.32 trillion total since September 2021 inception)

### GDPR

The [General Data Protection Regulation](https://gdpr-info.eu/) (Regulation (EU) 2016/679), effective since May 25, 2018, remains the global benchmark for data protection regulation.

**Capabilities demanded (identity and trust relevant):**
- **Consent:** Freely given, specific, informed, unambiguous; positive opt-in required; pre-checked boxes prohibited; granular choices per processing purpose; separate from T&C; withdrawable at any time
- **Data minimization:** Collect only what is necessary for specific purposes
- **Right to erasure:** Data subjects can request deletion; organizations must erase data when no longer necessary, consent withdrawn, or processing unlawful
- **DPO requirement:** Mandatory for public authorities and organizations whose core activities involve large-scale systematic monitoring or processing of special categories of data
- **Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA):** Required for high-risk processing
- **Breach notification:** Within 72 hours to supervisory authority; without undue delay to data subjects if high risk
- **Data portability:** Right to receive personal data in structured, machine-readable format

**Enforcement data (DLA Piper GDPR Fines Survey, January 2026):**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Cumulative fines (May 2018 – Jan 2026) | **€7.1 billion** |
| 2025 fines issued | **~€1.2 billion** |
| Total documented fines | **2,560+** |
| Highest single fine | €1.2 billion (Meta, May 2023) |
| Largest 2025 fine | €530 million (TikTok, data transfers) |
| Ireland aggregate | €4.04 billion (4x second-place Luxembourg) |
| Spain enforcement count | 932+ actions (highest volume) |
| Breach notifications (Jan 2026) | 443/day (22% increase YoY; first time >400/day since 2018) |

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must build and maintain consent management platforms, data subject rights processing infrastructure (access, deletion, portability, correction), DPO function with supporting technology, DPIA processes, 72-hour breach notification capability, and data minimization controls across all processing activities.

### PSD2 / PSD3 (Proposed)

**PSD2** ([Directive (EU) 2015/2366](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A32015L2366)) established Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) requirements for electronic payments across Europe.

**PSD2 SCA requirements:**
- **Two-factor authentication** using at least two of: knowledge (password/PIN), possession (phone/token), inherence (biometric)
- **Dynamic linking:** Authentication codes must be bound to specific transaction amount and payee; invalidated if either changes
- **Exemptions:** Low-value transactions, recurring payments, trusted beneficiaries, merchant-initiated transactions, transaction risk analysis (TRA) exemptions

**PSD3/PSR** — the trilogue agreement was reached **November 27, 2025**. PSD2 is being replaced by two instruments:
- **PSR (Regulation):** Directly applicable across EU — SCA, open banking, user rights, transparency
- **PSD3 (Directive):** National transposition — licensing, prudential requirements

**PSD3 key changes (identity and trust relevant):**
- **APP fraud liability:** Expanded liability for authorized push payment impersonation fraud
- **Confirmation of Payee:** Mandatory 24 months after entry into force
- **Consent dashboards:** Stricter requirements for open banking consent management
- **SCA updates:** Modernized authentication requirements

**Timeline:** Implementation expected **H2 2027 at earliest, possibly early 2028**; 2–2.5 year transition period for existing payment/e-money institutions.

**Cross-reference note:** The existing payments infrastructure research covers PSD3/PSR extensively in Section 3.1, including structural changes, APP fraud liability, open banking, and Confirmation of Payee. This section focuses on the identity and authentication implications.

> Sources: [Norton Rose Fulbright — PSD3/PSR](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/inside-fintech/blog/2026/03/eu-payment-services-reform-preparing-for-psd3-and-psr), [EBA — PSD2 SCA Dynamic Linking](https://www.eba.europa.eu/single-rule-book-qa/qna/view/publicId/2020_5366)

### eIDAS 2.0 (European Digital Identity Regulation)

[Regulation (EU) 2024/1183](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1183) (eIDAS 2.0) entered into force **May 20, 2024**, mandating EU-wide Digital Identity Wallets.

**Timeline:**

| Date | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| May 20, 2024 | Regulation enters into force |
| H1 2026 (by Dec 31, 2026) | All 27 member states must make EUDI Wallets available to citizens/residents |
| Dec 31, 2026 | Public/semi-public organizations must accept EUDI Wallet |
| **Dec 31, 2027** | **Private sector (including banks, telecoms, healthcare) must accept EUDI Wallet** |

**Capabilities demanded of banks:**
- Accept EUDI Wallet for identity verification during customer onboarding
- Integrate wallet-based Strong Customer Authentication
- Accept government-verified credentials (replacing document-based KYC)
- Support credential verification and attribute sharing from wallets

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks must integrate EUDI Wallet authentication into their onboarding, KYC/AML, and SCA processes by December 2027. This represents a fundamental shift from document-based to credential-based identity verification. Early adopters gain streamlined KYC and reduced fraud through government-verified credentials; laggards face a hard compliance deadline.

**Strategic significance:** eIDAS 2.0 creates a pan-European digital identity layer that banks must connect to — effectively mandating a unified trust infrastructure across 27 member states.

> Sources: [eIDAS 2.0 Timeline](https://eidasreadiness.com/eidas-2-timeline), [Yousign — eIDAS 2.0 Compliance](https://yousign.com/blog/eidas-2-0-digital-identity-wallet-compliance-requirements), [Sphereon — eIDAS 2.0 Timeline](https://sphereon.com/news-and-insights/the-new-eu-eidas2-regulation-timeline/)

### EU AI Act

[Regulation (EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689) (the EU AI Act) establishes a risk-based framework for AI systems, with direct implications for financial services.

**Compliance timeline:**

| Date | Obligation |
|------|-----------|
| Feb 2, 2025 | General provisions (definitions, AI literacy) and prohibitions on unacceptable AI practices |
| Aug 2, 2025 | GPAI model rules; member states designate competent authorities; penalties framework |
| **Aug 2, 2026** | **High-risk AI systems (Annex III) rules apply — includes creditworthiness, credit scoring, insurance pricing** |
| Aug 2, 2027 | High-risk AI embedded in regulated products |

**Financial services classification as high-risk (Annex III):**
- AI systems used to evaluate creditworthiness or establish credit scores
- AI systems used for risk assessment and pricing in life and health insurance
- AI systems used for recruitment, employee evaluation, and task allocation (applies to all sectors)

**Requirements for high-risk AI in banking:**
- Risk management system throughout the AI system's lifecycle
- Data governance and data quality requirements
- Technical documentation and record-keeping
- Transparency and information to deployers
- Human oversight measures
- Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity requirements
- Conformity assessment before deployment
- Post-market monitoring

**Penalties:**
- **€35 million or 7% of global turnover** for prohibited AI practices
- **€15 million or 3% of global turnover** for high-risk AI violations
- **€7.5 million or 1.5% of global turnover** for supplying incorrect information

**Implementation challenges:** The European Commission has identified delays in member states designating competent authorities and conformity assessment bodies, and a lack of harmonized standards. A Digital Omnibus proposal (November 2025) addresses these through targeted simplification.

**Infrastructure impact:** Banks using AI for credit decisioning, risk assessment, fraud detection, and customer service must build AI governance frameworks, conformity assessment processes, human oversight mechanisms, bias monitoring, and transparency documentation — all before August 2026 for Annex III systems.

> Sources: [EU AI Act Service Desk — Timeline](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/eu-ai-act-implementation-timeline), [EUR-Lex — AI Act Text](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689)

### DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act)

[Regulation (EU) 2022/2554](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2554) — the Digital Operational Resilience Act — became applicable on **January 17, 2025**.

**Scope:** 20+ categories of financial entities including credit institutions, investment firms, payment institutions, insurance companies, and — critically — their ICT third-party service providers.

**Five pillars of compliance:**

1. **ICT Risk Management:** Documented framework reviewed annually; governance, risk assessment, protective measures, detection, incident response, recovery
2. **ICT Incident Reporting:** Standardized classification; mandatory reporting of significant incidents to authorities within specified timeframes
3. **Digital Operational Resilience Testing:** Periodic testing of critical ICT systems; advanced threat-led penetration testing (TLPT)
4. **ICT Third-Party Risk Management:** Information registers of all ICT providers; due diligence; contractual resilience provisions; concentration risk monitoring
5. **Information Sharing:** Collaborative networks for threat intelligence exchange

**Status:** Effective January 17, 2025. Not all delegated and implementing regulations are finalized, complicating full compliance.

**Penalty:** National supervisory authorities impose proportionate sanctions; specific penalty levels determined by member state transposition.

**Infrastructure impact (identity and trust relevant):** DORA's third-party oversight requirements directly impact identity and authentication infrastructure — banks must maintain registers of all ICT providers (including identity verification, authentication, and KYC service providers), demonstrate operational resilience of identity systems, and conduct penetration testing of authentication infrastructure.

**Cross-reference note:** The existing payments infrastructure research does not cover DORA directly. This is net-new research for the identity and trust engagement area.

> Sources: [DORA Compliance Guide](https://www.compliancehub.wiki/digital-operational-resilience-act-dora-a-comprehensive-guide-to-compliance/), [DLA Piper — DORA Application](https://www.dlapiper.com/en-us/insights/publications/2025/02/application-of-the-digital-operational-resilience-act---dora), [EBA — DORA](https://www.eba.europa.eu/regulation-and-policy/single-rulebook/interactive-single-rulebook/17716)

## Regulatory Convergence Analysis

These regulations, despite originating from different jurisdictions and addressing different immediate concerns, converge on a small set of infrastructure capabilities that compound into a case for unified trust infrastructure.

### Convergence Theme 1: Consent as Infrastructure

Every jurisdiction now mandates explicit, granular, revocable consent before processing personal data or sharing financial information:

| Jurisdiction | Consent Mandate | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| USA | CCPA/CPRA opt-out + 20-state patchwork | GPC signal processing, per-purpose consent |
| India | DPDP Act + Account Aggregator | Consent Managers (registered entities), consent-based data flow |
| EU | GDPR + PSD3 consent dashboards | Explicit opt-in, granular purpose specification, withdrawal rights |

**Compounding effect:** A bank operating across these jurisdictions needs a single consent architecture that can express consent semantics compatible with all three regimes. Building three separate consent systems is not sustainable.

### Convergence Theme 2: Authentication Escalation

Every jurisdiction is raising the authentication bar, with MFA as the minimum and biometric/credential-based authentication as the direction:

| Jurisdiction | Authentication Mandate | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| USA | FFIEC MFA guidance; NIST 800-63-4 (passkeys, phishing-resistant auth); GLBA Safeguards MFA | Passkeys, FIDO2, wallet-based credentials |
| India | RBI authentication directions; Aadhaar biometric/face auth; V-CIP | Biometric + face authentication at scale (27B+ txns/yr) |
| EU | PSD2 SCA (two-factor + dynamic linking); eIDAS 2.0 wallet; PSD3 updates | Government-issued digital identity wallets |

**Compounding effect:** Banks need a unified authentication platform that can support MFA, biometric modalities, passkeys, dynamic linking, and wallet-based credential verification — not siloed authentication systems per channel or jurisdiction.

### Convergence Theme 3: Identity Proofing at Onboarding and Beyond

KYC/identity proofing is being regulated as a continuous process, not a one-time onboarding check:

| Jurisdiction | Identity Proofing Mandate |
|---|---|
| USA | FinCEN CDD (ongoing monitoring, risk-based refresh); NIST 800-63-4 (deepfake-resistant proofing) |
| India | RBI KYC Directions 2025 (periodic updation; V-CIP; CKYCR integration) |
| EU | eIDAS 2.0 (wallet-based credential verification); FCA Consumer Duty (inclusive verification) |

### Convergence Theme 4: AI Governance as Identity Problem

AI systems making decisions about people (credit, insurance, fraud) are being regulated as identity and trust problems:

| Jurisdiction | AI Identity/Trust Mandate |
|---|---|
| USA | SR 11-7 (model governance); agentic AI accountability gap being assessed |
| EU | EU AI Act (high-risk classification for credit/insurance AI; transparency; human oversight) |

**Compounding effect:** When an AI agent acts on behalf of a bank, the identity of that agent — its authorization scope, its decision audit trail, its accountability chain — becomes a trust infrastructure problem. The regulations are converging on requiring this, but no standard exists yet.

### Convergence Theme 5: Breach Notification and Operational Resilience

Every jurisdiction now mandates breach notification with tight timelines, and the EU adds operational resilience testing for identity infrastructure:

| Jurisdiction | Breach/Resilience Mandate |
|---|---|
| USA | GLBA Safeguards (FTC breach notification); FTC enforcement actions |
| India | DPDP Act (breach notification to Board and affected principals) |
| EU | GDPR (72-hour notification); DORA (ICT incident reporting + resilience testing) |

### The Case for Unified Trust Infrastructure

The regulatory convergence creates a compound investment mandate:

1. **Consent management** that works across GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, DPDP, and 20+ state laws
2. **Authentication** that supports MFA, biometrics, passkeys, dynamic linking, and wallet-based credentials
3. **Identity proofing** that handles continuous KYC, video verification, biometric authentication, and deepfake resistance
4. **AI governance** that provides model transparency, bias monitoring, and — increasingly — AI agent identity
5. **Operational resilience** for identity and trust systems, including breach notification and penetration testing

Building these as five separate point solutions per jurisdiction is exponentially more expensive than building a unified trust platform that can be configured per regulatory regime.

## Key Findings

- **20 US states** now have comprehensive privacy laws, covering 184M+ residents. No federal privacy law exists, making the patchwork structurally permanent for planning purposes.

- **GDPR enforcement has reached €7.1 billion cumulative** with 443 breach notifications per day (22% YoY increase), demonstrating that enforcement is accelerating, not stabilizing.

- **eIDAS 2.0 mandates** that banks accept EU Digital Identity Wallets by December 2027 — the most structurally significant identity regulation globally, as it creates a mandatory pan-European digital identity layer.

- **India's Aadhaar authentication processes 27+ billion transactions annually** (FY24-25), making it the world's largest digital identity verification system by volume. The Account Aggregator framework adds consent-based data sharing at scale (580 FIUs, 173 FIPs).

- **The EU AI Act classifies credit scoring and creditworthiness AI as high-risk** with compliance required by August 2026 — banks have ~5 months remaining. Penalties reach 7% of global turnover.

- **NIST 800-63-4** (finalized July 2025) sets the new technical benchmark for identity proofing with deepfake-resistant controls, syncable authenticators (passkeys), and wallet-based federation — this will cascade through examination expectations.

- **DORA** (effective January 2025) adds operational resilience requirements for identity infrastructure, including mandatory registers of all ICT third-party providers and threat-led penetration testing.

- **India's DPDP Act** enters its critical compliance phase: Consent Manager registration by November 2026, full compliance (including breach notification and security safeguards) by May 2027. Penalties up to ₹250 crore.

- **Regulatory convergence across all jurisdictions** creates compound investment pressure on five capabilities: consent management, authentication, identity proofing, AI governance, and operational resilience. These are not independent procurement decisions — they are facets of a unified trust infrastructure.

- **The agentic AI accountability gap** is emerging as the next regulatory frontier: SR 11-7's assumptions are being tested against autonomous AI systems (GARP, February 2026), and the EU AI Act's high-risk framework was not designed for dynamically autonomous agents.
