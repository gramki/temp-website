# S2: Enforcement Actions & Compliance Failures (2022–2026)

> **Stream purpose:** Enforcement actions are the primary evidence source for the Compliance Operations engagement area. They define the pain, the buying urgency, and the specific operational failures a compliance-operations platform must address. Banks under consent orders are the most likely near-term buyers.

## 2 — Key Findings

### 2.1 Enforcement Volume Is at Historic Highs

- **2024 was a record year:** $10.4B in global AML fines, driven by TD Bank ($3.09B) and cascading actions across jurisdictions.
- **H1 2025 showed 417% YoY increase** in banking sector penalties ($1.23B in first half alone).
- **Regulatory penalties surged 31% in H1 2024** compared to H1 2023.
- **North America accounted for 95% of global penalties in 2024** — the US regulatory apparatus remains by far the most punitive.
- **FCA (UK) imposed £456M in fines across three firms in Q3 2025**, including a £150M penalty on a high-street bank for inadequate due diligence on high-risk customers.
- **ECB enforcement proceedings increased 19% in 2024**, with 182 administrative penalties totaling €27.9M.

### 2.2 Operational Failures Follow Repeatable Patterns

Across the 21 enforcement actions documented above, regulators consistently cite the same categories of operational failure:

| Operational Failure Pattern | Frequency | Representative Cases |
|---|---|---|
| **Transaction monitoring gaps** — systems not covering all transaction types, channels, or customer segments | Very high | TD Bank (92% unmonitored), NatWest (cash misclassified as cheques), Starling (partial sanctions screening) |
| **Growth outpacing compliance investment** — "flat cost paradigm," headcount not scaling with volume | Very high | TD Bank, USAA, Starling, Robinhood |
| **SAR/CTR filing failures** — backlogs, unfiled reports, inaccurate filings | Very high | TD Bank, Capital One, USAA, BitMEX |
| **Inadequate customer due diligence / KYC** — no identity verification, incomplete risk profiling | High | Binance, BitMEX, Standard Chartered, RBL Bank |
| **Remediation non-completion** — banks fail to meet consent order timelines, resulting in escalated penalties | High | Citibank (4 years late), Deutsche Bank (8+ years of unresolved orders), Wells Fargo (resolved one order, got another), USAA (missed multiple deadlines) |
| **Data quality and risk data aggregation failures** — inability to aggregate risk data enterprise-wide | High | Citibank (primary violation), Wells Fargo, Deutsche Bank |
| **Technology system misconfiguration** — monitoring tools improperly calibrated or covering wrong data | Moderate | NatWest (cash → cheque misclassification), Starling (partial sanctions list), Robinhood (failed manual-to-automated transition) |
| **Inadequate board/senior management oversight** — governance not engaging on compliance risk | Moderate | Citibank, TD Bank, Capital One |
| **Correspondent banking failures** — failure to apply controls to relationships with other banks | Moderate | Danske Bank, Deutsche Bank, Standard Chartered |

### 2.3 The "Second Penalty" Pattern

A critical finding for buyer urgency: **multiple banks received second or escalated penalties for failing to remediate initial consent orders:**

- **Citibank:** $400M (2020) → $136M additional (2024) for insufficient progress. Original order still in force.
- **Deutsche Bank:** 2015/2017 consent orders → $186M Fed fine (2023) → €23M BaFin fine (2025) for still not resolving.
- **Wells Fargo:** Resolved one AML consent order (Jan 2021) → new OCC enforcement action (Sep 2024) for deficiencies in the same domain. 8 active consent orders remain.
- **USAA:** Made 7 commitments → missed Mar 2020 deadline → extended to Jun 2021 → still non-compliant → $140M penalty (Mar 2022).
- **OCC Capital One:** 2015 order → $100M (2018) for failure to achieve timely compliance → $390M FinCEN (2021).

This pattern demonstrates that consent orders create multi-year remediation programs that banks struggle to execute. The operational challenge is not *knowing* what to fix — it's *operationalizing* the fix at scale.

### 2.4 Enforcement Is Expanding Beyond Traditional Banks

- **Crypto/fintech enforcement is accelerating:** Binance ($4.3B), BitMEX ($300M total), Robinhood ($30M), Paxful ($3.5M), Starling ($37M), Monzo (£21M reported in 2025).
- **Non-bank financial institutions:** Brink's (cash logistics), casinos, MSBs all receiving FinCEN actions.
- **Implication:** The compliance operations addressable market extends beyond traditional banking to any entity with BSA/AML obligations.

### 2.5 Asset Caps and Growth Restrictions as Enforcement Tools

Regulators are increasingly using growth restrictions as a non-monetary compliance lever:

- **TD Bank:** OCC imposed asset cap alongside $3.09B fine.
- **Wells Fargo:** $1.95T Fed asset cap in place since 2018 — now expected to be prolonged by 2024 OCC enforcement action.
- **Starling:** Prohibited from onboarding high-risk customers under voluntary requirement (then violated it).

Growth caps are uniquely punitive for banks because they constrain revenue while remediation costs increase.

## 3 — Enforcement Trend Analysis

### 3.1 Is Enforcement Getting Worse?

**Yes, unambiguously.**

| Year | Notable Penalty Volume | Key Actions |
|---|---|---|
| 2022 | ~$2.2B+ | Danske Bank $2B plea, USAA $140M, Robinhood $30M |
| 2023 | ~$4.8B+ | Binance $4.3B, Deutsche Bank $186M (Fed), Swedbank ~$90M |
| 2024 | **$10.4B (record)** | TD Bank $3.09B, Citibank $136M (escalation), Wells Fargo formal agreement, Starling £29M, BitMEX $100M (criminal) |
| 2025 (H1) | $1.23B (417% YoY increase for banking sector) | Deutsche Bank €23M (BaFin), Paxful $3.5M, Brink's CMP, community bank consent orders, FCA £456M across firms |
| 2026 (Q1) | Ongoing | ECB penalties (J.P. Morgan €12.18M, Nordea €2.26M), new OCC enforcement actions, Swedbank new investigation launched |

**Compounding factors driving further escalation:**

1. **FinCEN AML Whistleblower Program** — Operational since late 2025 / early 2026 under the AML Whistleblower Improvement Act of 2022. Awards range from 10–30% of penalties collected (minimum $1M threshold). Funded by a $300M Financial Integrity Fund. Anonymous tips permitted. International eligibility. TD Bank's $3.09B settlement alone could generate $310M–$930M in whistleblower awards. This creates a massive financial incentive for insiders to report compliance failures, dramatically increasing detection probability.

2. **Regulatory capacity expansion** — ECB enforcement proceedings up 19% YoY; BaFin powers expanded post-Wirecard; FCA pursuing criminal (not just civil) prosecution of banks.

3. **Cross-border coordination** — Danske Bank, Deutsche Bank, and Swedbank cases all involved multiple regulators (DOJ, SEC, FCA, BaFin, Finansinspektionen, OFAC) coordinating enforcement. Regulatory arbitrage is decreasing.

4. **AI and data analytics** — Regulators are investing in technology to detect compliance gaps, narrowing the asymmetry between supervisors and supervised entities.

### 3.2 What Does "Getting Worse" Mean for Buyers?

For banks evaluating compliance operations platforms, the trend creates three buying drivers:

1. **Penalty magnitude is increasing non-linearly.** The jump from hundreds-of-millions to billions-level penalties (TD Bank, Binance) means compliance failure is now an existential financial risk, not a cost-of-doing-business line item.

2. **Remediation failure triggers escalation.** The Citibank, Deutsche Bank, and Wells Fargo patterns prove that failing to operationalize remediation leads to compounding penalties. Banks need systems that can *prove* compliance progress to regulators.

3. **The whistleblower program creates internal threat surface.** Any compliance gap that an insider observes is now a potential multi-hundred-million-dollar whistleblower claim. This shifts compliance from a periodic audit exercise to a continuous, defensible posture.

## 4 — FinCEN AML Whistleblower Program

### Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Legal basis** | Anti-Money Laundering Act of 2020; AML Whistleblower Improvement Act of 2022 |
| **Status** | Operational — accepting tips as of early 2026; final implementing regulations still pending |
| **Award range** | 10–30% of monetary sanctions exceeding $1M |
| **Funding** | Financial Integrity Fund ($300M revolving fund financed by collected penalties) |
| **Eligibility** | No US citizenship required; anonymous reporting via attorney permitted |
| **Retaliation protections** | Reinstatement, double back pay, attorney fees |
| **Covered violations** | BSA, IEEPA, TWEA, Foreign Narcotics Kingpin Designation Act |

### Impact on Compliance Urgency

- **Magnitude of incentive:** TD Bank's $3.09B settlement could yield $310M–$930M in whistleblower awards — creating enormous financial motivation for compliance staff, auditors, or any bank employee aware of deficiencies.
- **Behavioral shift:** Banks must now assume that *any internal compliance gap will eventually be reported*. This eliminates the option of tolerating known deficiencies while scheduling future remediation.
- **Operational implication:** Compliance operations must produce auditable, contemporaneous evidence of controls functioning — not just policy documents. Whistleblower claims will be adjudicated against operational reality, not stated intent.

## 5 — Patterns in Regulator-Cited Operational Failures

Beyond the high-level categories in §2.2, regulators cite specific operational deficiencies that map directly to platform capabilities:

### 5.1 Transaction Monitoring Failures

| What Regulators Cite | Operational Root Cause | Platform Capability Required |
|---|---|---|
| "92% of total transaction volume went unmonitored" (TD Bank) | Monitoring system configured to exclude transaction types (ACH, checks) | Universal transaction ingestion across all channels and payment rails |
| "Automated system misclassified cash deposits as cheque deposits" (NatWest) | Data mapping errors in monitoring system | Data quality validation layer between core banking and monitoring |
| "Only screening against a fraction of the complete sanctions list" (Starling) | Incomplete reference data integration | Automated, continuously updated sanctions list management |
| "Failed transition from manual to automated monitoring" (Robinhood) | System migration without validation | Parallel-run validation capability; monitoring coverage dashboards |

### 5.2 Resourcing and Governance Failures

| What Regulators Cite | Operational Root Cause | Platform Capability Required |
|---|---|---|
| "Flat cost paradigm" — budgets held flat despite growth (TD Bank) | No linkage between business volume and compliance resource allocation | Volume-based capacity modeling; automated alert triage to reduce headcount dependency |
| "Growth outpaced compliance capabilities" (USAA, Starling) | Manual scaling of compliance processes | Automated workflows; ML-assisted alert adjudication |
| "Failure to establish effective front-line units" (Citibank) | First-line-of-defense model not operationalized | Role-based compliance task assignment integrated with business processes |
| "Board and senior management oversight inadequate" (Citibank) | No compliance MIS at board level | Executive dashboards with real-time compliance posture |

### 5.3 Remediation Management Failures

| What Regulators Cite | Operational Root Cause | Platform Capability Required |
|---|---|---|
| "Has not made sufficient progress" (Citibank, 4 years later) | No systematic remediation tracking | Consent order management: milestones, evidence collection, regulator reporting |
| "Insufficient progress addressing … shortcomings previously flagged" (Deutsche Bank, 8+ years) | Remediation treated as project, not operating model | Continuous compliance monitoring that proves sustained adherence |
| "Made 7 commitments … missed deadline … extended … still non-compliant" (USAA) | Commitment tracking disconnected from operational delivery | Integrated remediation project management with compliance evidence |
| "Resolved prior AML order, then received new enforcement action" (Wells Fargo) | Point-in-time remediation without sustained controls | Always-on compliance controls with drift detection |

### Analytical Questions to Resolve

1. **What is the average time from consent order to resolution?** Citibank (4+ years and counting), Deutsche Bank (8+ years and counting), Wells Fargo (6+ years on some orders) suggest multi-year remediation cycles are the norm, not the exception. Quantifying this would strengthen the compliance ops platform value proposition.

2. **What is the consent-order-to-second-penalty rate?** How often do banks under consent orders receive escalated enforcement? If the rate is high, it strongly supports the thesis that banks need better remediation management tooling.

3. **How does enforcement intensity vary by bank tier?** Community banks (First National Bank of Pasco, Patriot Bank) receive consent orders without CMPs, while G-SIBs receive billion-dollar penalties. This has implications for product-market fit and pricing.

4. **What is the spending increase at banks under consent orders?** Anecdotally, banks under consent orders increase compliance spending by 2–5x. Quantifying this would size the addressable spend per enforcement event.
