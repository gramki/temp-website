**United Kingdom**
- Total fraud losses exceeded £1.1 billion in 2024 (unauthorised transactions: £722 million, up 2% from 2023).

- £682 million in card fraud prevented in H1 2025 — highest ever, up 21% YoY.

*Sources: UK Finance Annual Fraud Report, 2025; UK Finance Half Year Fraud Report, 2025*

**India**
- Card/internet fraud cases halved in FY25: 13,516 cases (₹520 crore) vs. 29,082 cases (₹1,457 crore) in FY24.
- Card/internet fraud accounts for 66.8% of total fraud cases but only 7.2% of total fraud amount.
- H1 FY26 (April–September 2025): only 128 card/internet fraud cases involving ₹4 crore — dramatic decline attributed to tokenization, MFA, and AI/ML fraud detection.

*Sources: RBI data via Medianama, 2025; Times of India, 2025*

**Europe**
- 119 million compromised cards found on the dark web with an estimated $9.4 billion in preventable losses.
- Card-not-present fraud remains the leading fraud category across all European markets.

*Source: FICO European Fraud Map, 2025*

**Brazil**
- Card fraud data is less publicly reported. However, credit card volume continues to grow (9.7% increase in H1 2025 with 243 million active cards), and 21.9% of credit card transactions are online — increasing CNP exposure.

*Source: Brasil 247 / Central Bank of Brazil, 2025*

### 3.4 Impact on Card Issuance Strategies

Digital wallets are fundamentally reshaping card issuance:

- **74% of consumers** prioritise instant card issuance (Gen Z: 85%, Millennials: 86%).
- **91% of consumers** still consider physical cards "critical" or "important" — only 2% want fully digital-only.
- **36% of Gen Z** prefer hybrid models: immediate digital wallet provisioning + physical card mailed later.
- **Cards projected to decline** from 69% to 47% of all transactions by 2029 as digital wallets and alternative payments grow.

Issuers must support instant digital provisioning to wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay) on day zero, with physical card delivery as a secondary fulfilment. The wallet push-provisioning capability is now table stakes.

*Sources: Vericast, 2026; Tietoevry, 2026; The Payments Association, 2025*

### 4.4 How Virtual Cards Change Issuance Platform Requirements

Virtual cards demand fundamentally different platform capabilities:

1. **Real-time issuance via API**: cards must be created programmatically in milliseconds — batch processes are obsolete.
2. **Granular controls**: per-card spend limits, MCC restrictions, velocity controls, geo-blocking, time-based validity.
3. **Multi-form-factor support**: a single platform must issue physical, virtual, and tokenized cards with unified management.
4. **BIN management at scale**: high-volume virtual card programs may burn through BIN ranges rapidly.
5. **Webhook-driven eventing**: real-time transaction notifications and authorization decisioning via API callbacks.
6. **PCI DSS Level 1 compliance**: with tokenization and 3D Secure built in.

Legacy batch-oriented card management systems (VisionPLUS, TS2) were not designed for this paradigm.

- **SmartVista** (legacy versions) — being upgraded to cloud-native architecture
- **In-house/bespoke card systems** — often 20–30 years old

### 5.3 Key Drivers for Card Platform Modernisation

| Driver | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Regulatory pressure** | PSD2 (EU), RBI directives (India), network mandates (tokenization, SCA) |
| **Speed to market** | Legacy systems take months to add features; modern platforms enable near-instant updates |
| **Digital-first customer expectations** | Instant issuance, real-time controls, wallet provisioning, virtual cards |
| **Cost reduction** | Cloud-based SaaS models vs. expensive mainframe maintenance |
| **Fraud & security** | Advanced tokenization, biometric authentication, AI/ML-driven fraud detection |
| **Multi-rail support** | Platforms must handle cards, A2A, BNPL, wallets, instant payments — not just card rails |
| **Profitability pressure** | Card late fees under regulatory scrutiny (15% of US credit issuer earnings); interchange challenged by pay-by-bank alternatives |
| **Competitive threat** | Cards projected to decline from 69% to 47% of all transactions by 2029 |

### 6.2 How BNPL Changes Issuance Infrastructure

BNPL creates new requirements for card issuance platforms:

1. **Hybrid credential support**: Mastercard's One credential already allows credit, debit, prepaid, and BNPL preferences behind a single token. Platforms must support multi-product routing from a single instrument.
2. **Real-time credit decisioning**: BNPL requires instant underwriting at point of sale — batch credit bureau pulls are too slow.
3. **Flexible repayment orchestration**: configurable instalment schedules (pay-in-3, pay-in-4, monthly, deferred), auto-scheduling, and collections integration.
4. **Virtual card issuance**: many BNPL providers (Klarna, Afterpay, Zip) issue virtual cards at checkout that ride existing card rails — requiring instant virtual card provisioning infrastructure.
5. **Compliance complexity**: BNPL regulation is tightening globally (CFPB in the US, FCA in the UK, EU Consumer Credit Directive). Platforms must be configurable for varying regulatory regimes.

### 6.4 How Digital-Native Instruments Compete with Card Issuance

| Competitive Vector | Impact on Cards |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| **UPI / Pix / A2A** | Bypass card rails entirely for retail payments; zero/near-zero merchant cost vs. interchange |
| **BNPL** | Competes for credit spending at point of sale; often uses virtual cards on card rails but captures customer relationship |
| **Digital wallets** | Abstract the card away from the consumer; the wallet becomes the relationship, not the card |
| **Open Banking / PSD2** | Enables direct bank-account payments, reducing dependence on card networks |
| **Embedded finance** | Non-bank brands issue cards/wallets via BaaS platforms, fragmenting traditional issuer relationships |

**Net effect for card issuance platform vendors**: The platform must evolve from "card management" to "instrument management" — supporting cards, virtual cards, tokenized credentials, A2A, BNPL, and wallet provisioning from a unified stack. Issuers that remain card-only risk being disintermediated at both the consumer and merchant ends.
