# Agentic Systems Are Advancing, Our Narratives Are Not
> On hype, conflation, and the need for honest disclosure


This note was triggered by a cluster of recent artifacts: the viral spread of [Moltbook](https://www.moltbook.com/) and [the surrounding discourse about emergent agency](http://www.kdnuggets.com/the-absolute-insanity-of-moltbook), [Cursor's multi-agent coding work](https://cursor.com/blog/scaling-agents), and the way these experiments were [subsequently circulated and interpreted within the AI community](https://x.com/derrickcchoi/status/2019807376136609804).

What struck me was not the work itself (much of which reflects serious, thoughtful engineering) but how quickly application engineering was conflated with AI advancement. Much of what is being showcased today sits firmly in the application layer of agentic systems. Many of these possibilities were anticipated by experts years ago. Yet the hype, amplified by unprecedented distribution power and an ecosystem eager to read inevitability into every new demo, inflates the significance of what is being demonstrated and obscures where the actual progress lies.

The most consequential example of this conflation is how long-running sessions are discussed. In most cases, these are long agent sessions, not extended reasoning or model sessions. The distinction is rarely made explicit. As a result, endurance of orchestration is implicitly presented as evidence of advances in underlying intelligence.

Models are undoubtedly improving. But the public narrative has shifted decisively from models to "agents," often treating the term as shorthand for intelligence itself. In practice, an agent typically represents a model embedded within a substantial amount of application engineering: planning logic, tool orchestration, memory scaffolding, retries, and guardrails. A long-running agent session, by itself, does not demonstrate a qualitative leap in intelligence. Agent engineering and model engineering remain fundamentally different disciplines.

The systems demonstrated recently, across products such as Claude Cowork, Claude Code, OpenAI Operator, Frontier, and Codex, remain formative. They amplify the throughput of human-directed work, but do not yet replace the judgment that directs it. They reflect useful but early work in agentic systems, not a viable basis for the displacement narratives they have inspired.

Some of this is driven by genuine excitement, and that impulse is understandable. But excitement does not exempt disclosure from responsibility. The problem is not that these systems are immature. It is that the narrative surrounding them is irresponsible. Outputs are presented without stated hypotheses, demonstrations without acknowledged limitations, and results without the conditions under which they were produced. This has real consequences. It shapes expectations that current systems are not structurally designed to meet.

When a team reports that agents "built a browser from scratch," for example, the accurate framing would be that humans designed the architecture, selected the models, defined the coordination topology, and set the restart criteria, while agents generated code within that human-defined space. The former framing is not a lie, but it is a narrative choice that makes misinterpretation predictable and correction difficult. 

The responsibility does not rest solely with those who amplify. When producers of serious work disclose in ways that leave the interpretive burden to an ecosystem structurally incentivized to overinterpret, they are complicit in the distortion they later disclaim. This is not accidental in outcome, even if not always intentional in design. The incentive structures of the current AI ecosystem, from fundraising pressure and competitive positioning to talent acquisition and market narrative control, reward spectacle over substance.

This pattern of irresponsible framing has a second-order effect beyond mismanaged expectations. It implicitly positions a small set of foundation model companies as the primary, or exclusive, sources of agentic progress, encouraging the broader ecosystem to over-index on their announcements as directional signals for the future of work.

That assumption is unwarranted. Agents are a powerful mechanism for diffusion and adoption of AI, but they are fundamentally an application layer, one among several ways AI manifests in products. As such, they do not constitute a natural or enduring moat for foundation model companies by default.

Foundation model companies do hold an initial advantage through privileged access to model internals and the ability to co-optimize training objectives with agent scaffolding. But this advantage is structurally bounded. Agent effectiveness is ultimately determined by domain fit, not model capability, and domain fit is not something foundation model companies can monopolize. A model optimized for agentic coding already underperforms general-purpose models at planning within coding itself. If a model company cannot optimize for all facets of a single domain, the premise that it will optimize for all facets of all domains is implausible.

The capabilities required to design effective agentic systems (product judgment, domain modeling, UX design, orchestration, evaluation, and operational analytics) are broadly accessible. As fine-tuned and distilled models become viable for domain-specific work, the advantage shifts further toward organizations that own the workflows, data, and evaluation criteria that define what "effective" means in their context. Cursor's multi-agent experiment illustrates this directly: one of the most ambitious agentic coding results in the ecosystem was produced by an application-layer company using off-the-shelf models, not by a foundation model company. Many of the most valuable agentic breakthroughs are therefore likely to emerge not from proximity to the models, but from proximity to the work itself.

None of this negates the real engineering work underway in agentic systems, nor does it discount the likelihood that some of today's agent products will succeed in specific domains. But the gap between narrative and reality is already producing tangible harm: misaligned buyer expectations, rushed adoption decisions, and an ecosystem where honest, disciplined progress is penalized for appearing slow.

## What Would Honest Disclosure Look Like?

Honest disclosure would frame these systems as experiments, not endpoints. It would state hypotheses, acknowledge gaps, and distinguish what is demonstrated from what is not. Progress would appear no less impressive, but it would be legible. The choice is not between optimism and caution. It is between disclosure that earns trust and spectacle that eventually exhausts it.
